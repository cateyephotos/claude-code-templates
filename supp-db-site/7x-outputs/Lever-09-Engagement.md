# Lever 9: Your Engagement — Engagement Architecture Blueprint

---

## STEP 1: Engagement Profile Summary

**Primary Touchpoints:** Weekly email (realistic constraint), website (passive), social (aspirational)
**Disengage Pattern:** Users browse the free database, get surface-level answers ("Is magnesium good? Yes."), and leave before discovering the AI Stack Analyzer, guides, or evidence tiers. They got "enough" to feel informed but not enough to change behavior.
**Core Tension:** The free database is *too* satisfying at surface level. Users don't know what they're missing because they don't know what questions to ask.
**Secret Goal:** "Stop guessing and start supplementing with confidence."

---

## STEP 2: The 7 Engagement Pillars

### Pillar 1: The Entry Hook — Pattern Interrupt That Earns 8 More Seconds

**How it should be used:** Open every email, social post, or landing page interaction with a question or statement that creates an information gap the reader can't close without engaging further. Not clickbait — a legitimate knowledge gap they didn't know they had.

**Where it fits best:** Email subject lines and opening lines. This is your single most important engagement lever given the 1x/week email constraint.

**Tailored Example:**

> Subject: 3 of the top 10 selling supplements have almost no clinical evidence
>
> You probably own at least one of them.
>
> I ran the numbers across 471 papers and 93 supplements. Three of the most popular supplements in the US sit at Tier 3 or Tier 4 in our evidence classification — meaning the research is either preliminary or functionally nonexistent.
>
> The uncomfortable part: they're not obscure. They're in your cabinet right now.
>
> [See the evidence tiers for your supplements →]

**Why this works for SupplementDB:** You're not making a health claim. You're stating a research finding and letting the gap between "popular" and "evidence-backed" do the emotional work. Dry. Factual. Mildly alarming.

---

### Pillar 2: The Participation Trigger — Low-Friction Action That Creates Investment

**How it should be used:** Give readers something to DO within the first 60 seconds of engagement. The action should be easy, feel productive, and generate a personal result they want to keep.

**Where it fits best:** Email body CTAs and on-site interactions. The AI Stack Analyzer is your built-in participation trigger — the problem is people don't reach it.

**Tailored Example:**

> **The 90-Second Stack Check**
>
> Open your supplement shelf (or the Notes app where you keep your stack).
> Count how many you take daily.
> Now ask yourself: for how many of those could you cite a single clinical trial?
>
> If the answer is "maybe one?" — that's normal. That's also fixable in about 5 minutes.
>
> [Run your free AI Stack Analysis → takes under 5 minutes, tells you exactly where you stand]

**Why this works:** You're converting passive reading into physical action (look at your shelf). Once they've counted, they've invested. The gap between "I take 6 supplements" and "I can cite evidence for 0 of them" is the trigger.

---

### Pillar 3: The Attention Loop — Incomplete Information That Pulls Forward

**How it should be used:** Present a partial reveal that can only be completed by taking the next step. Not withholding — sequencing. Give them a genuine insight, then make the full picture require one more click or action.

**Where it fits best:** Mid-email, mid-article, and anywhere you need to bridge from free content to deeper engagement.

**Tailored Example:**

> **"Spot the Tier 4"**
>
> Here are 5 supplements commonly recommended for sleep:
> - Magnesium glycinate
> - Melatonin
> - Valerian root
> - L-theanine
> - 5-HTP
>
> Four of these have at least Tier 2 evidence (multiple RCTs or meta-analyses). One is sitting at Tier 4 — "Preliminary/Limited" — meaning the clinical support is thin enough that you'd be better off saving your money.
>
> Can you guess which one?
>
> [Check the evidence tiers →]

**Why this works:** It's a quiz without being a quiz. The reader will guess (creating mental investment), and they'll click to verify because being wrong feels worse than not knowing.

### Pillar 4: The Soft Continuation Cue — Frictionless Bridge to the Next Interaction

**How it should be used:** At the end of every engagement, plant a reason to come back. Not a hard sell — a breadcrumb. "Next week, we're covering..." or "The next thing to check is..."

**Where it fits best:** Email closers and post-analysis follow-up.

**Tailored Example:**

> **Email closer:**
>
> Next week: the supplement that Huberman recommends but most people take at the wrong dose — and why the dose matters more than you think.
>
> (Hint: it's not the one you're expecting.)

**Why this works:** Teases specific value, invokes a trusted authority reference, and creates a knowledge gap that won't resolve until the next email. The parenthetical adds the dry tone.

**How it should be used:** Create a tangible, finishable action that produces a result the reader wants to save, share, or reference. Completion = investment. Investment = return visits.

### Pillar 6: The Relatability Anchor — "You Get Me"

**How it should be used:** Name the exact frustration your audience feels but hasn't articulated. Not empathy theater — precision. You've been in the same position. Say so.

**Where it fits best:** Email intros, about page, guide introductions.

**Tailored Example:**

> I spent about 6 months taking a supplement stack I found on Reddit. Seemed reasonable. Had upvotes. Several comments said "changed my life."
>
> When I actually looked up the evidence, two of the five were Tier 4 — meaning the clinical support was a couple of small studies with questionable methodology. One had zero RCTs.
>
> I wasn't angry. I was just tired. Tired of the gap between what gets recommended online and what the research actually says.
>
> That's why SupplementDB exists. Not to tell you what to take. To show you what the evidence says so you can decide for yourself.

**Why this works:** You're not performing frustration — you're sharing a specific experience that mirrors exactly what your audience has done (Reddit stacks, influencer recommendations). The "I wasn't angry, I was tired" line is the brand voice in two sentences.

---

### Pillar 7: The Yes-Ladder — Micro-Agreements That Build Momentum

**How it should be used:** Sequence 2-3 questions the reader will internally answer "yes" to before presenting your CTA. Each yes lowers resistance to the next action.

**Where it fits best:** Pre-CTA sections in emails, landing page copy above the fold.

**Tailored Example:**

> Would you feel more confident about your supplements if every one was backed by clinical trials?
>
> Does it make sense to check the evidence before spending $40/month on something?
>
> Would it help to know — in 5 minutes — which supplements in your current stack have strong evidence and which ones don't?
>
> [Run your free Stack Analysis →]

**Why this works:** Each question is unanswerable with "no" by anyone in your target audience. By the third yes, clicking the CTA feels like the logical next step rather than a sales pitch.

## STEP 5: Engagement Upgrade Plan

### 3 Changes to Implement Immediately

**1. Add "Spot the Tier 4" to Every Email**
Every weekly email should include one "can you guess?" element — a mini-quiz that creates an information gap resolvable only by clicking through to the database. This is your lowest-effort, highest-return engagement tactic. Rotate through health goals: sleep, focus, energy, joint health, longevity.

**2. Install the Stack Score Screenshot CTA**
After every AI Stack Analysis, prompt the user to screenshot their score. Add copy: "This is your baseline. Come back after making one swap." This creates a return trigger without requiring a reminder email. The screenshot sits in their camera roll as a dormant engagement device.

**3. Tease Next Week's Email in Every Closer**
Never end an email without planting a reason to open the next one. Format: "Next week: [specific supplement or claim] + [why it matters] + (parenthetical dry aside)." This converts your weekly email from "another newsletter" to "a series I'm following."

### 1 Habit to Install Permanently

**The "One Question" Rule:** Every piece of content you publish — email, social post, guide intro, homepage copy — must open with or contain one genuine question the reader will want to answer. Not rhetorical. Not leading. A real question that creates a gap between what they think they know and what the evidence says. This is your engagement engine. It never gets old because every supplement has a gap between perception and evidence.

### 1 Common Engagement Mistake to Avoid

**Don't teach in emails. Tease in emails.** The instinct is to pack the weekly email with value — evidence summaries, dosage charts, mechanism explanations. Resist. Your database is the value. The email's job is to create a reason to visit the database. If someone reads your email and feels "that was informative" but doesn't click, you've failed. If they read it and feel "I need to check my stack," you've won. The email is the hook. The site is the meal.

## FINAL CHECK

- **Engagement feels natural, not gimmicky:** Every hook is grounded in real evidence gaps — not manufactured curiosity. The questions are genuine. The reveals are factual. The dry tone prevents anything from feeling like a BuzzFeed quiz.
- **Audience effort is rewarded:** Every click leads to a real answer (evidence tier, interaction warning, dosage comparison). No bait-and-switch. No "sign up to find out."
- **Attention deepens across touchpoints:** Email → Database search → Stack Analysis → Score screenshot → Return visit → Guide purchase. Each step provides more value and more investment.
