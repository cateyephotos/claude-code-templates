## THE 5 CORE EMAIL TYPES

### 1. Brand Likability

**Purpose:** Make the reader feel like they know you. Build familiarity and warmth without sacrificing credibility.

**Emotional Goal:** "This person is real. I like getting these emails."

**What It Contains:**
- A personal detail, quirk, or behind-the-scenes moment
- A light connection to evidence or supplements (never forced)
- A conversational, first-person tone
- Short — under 300 words

**What to Avoid:**
- Performing vulnerability for engagement
- Making the personal detail the whole email with no value
- Excessive self-deprecation that undermines authority
- Forced humor

**5 Example Angles:**
1. "I drink everything out of one cup" — how simplifying one thing led to simplifying supplement research
2. The sleep/immunity crash story — building a health database while ignoring your own health
3. "I spent 6 hours reading papers on Tuesday. I also forgot to eat." — the obsession is real
4. Photography obsession as a lens (literally) for how you see evidence — most people look at the label, you look at the methodology
5. "I have difficulty doing things that don't interest me. Building this database was interesting. Marketing it is the part where I stare at the wall."

---

### 2. Engagement

**Purpose:** Get the reader to reply, click, or interact. Build two-way relationship.

**Emotional Goal:** "I actually want to answer this."

**What It Contains:**
- A direct question drawn from the audience's silent thoughts
- A short insight or surprising fact that earns the question
- A reply prompt that feels low-effort and genuinely interesting
- One clear CTA (reply, click, or forward)

**What to Avoid:**
- Polls or gimmicks
- Questions that feel like homework
- "What do you think?" without first giving them something to think about
- Generic engagement bait

**5 Example Angles:**
1. "What's the one supplement you take but secretly suspect does nothing?" (reply prompt)
2. "How many supplements in your current stack have you actually checked the evidence for? Hit reply with a number."
3. "If you could get a straight answer on one supplement question, what would it be?" (use replies to guide future content)
4. "I'll tell you the evidence tier of any supplement you're taking. Reply with one name."
5. "Your doctor probably doesn't know this about magnesium types — here's what the trials say. Which form are you taking?"

---

### 3. Dopamine Dealer

**Purpose:** Deliver a satisfying, shareable insight. Something the reader feels smarter for knowing.

**Emotional Goal:** "I didn't know that. I need to tell someone."

**What It Contains:**
- One surprising evidence fact or counterintuitive finding
- A clean setup > reveal > punchline structure
- A "save-worthy" or "forward-worthy" density of insight
- Optional dry humor line at the end

**What to Avoid:**
- Clickbait reveals that don't deliver
- "You won't believe" energy
- Oversimplifying the science to the point of inaccuracy
- Stacking too many facts (one insight, well-delivered)

**5 Example Angles:**
1. The #1 selling sleep supplement on Amazon is Tier 3. The one with the strongest evidence is on page 4.
2. "Clinically proven" can mean a 3-week study on 12 college students. Here's what the phrase actually requires (nothing).
3. There are 7 types of magnesium. Only 2 have Tier 1 evidence for any health outcome. Here's which ones.
4. The supplement with the most clinical trials behind it costs less than $0.10/day. The one with the best marketing costs $2.50/day.
5. One popular nootropic has 0 human RCTs. Its evidence tier: Preliminary. Its Amazon rating: 4.7 stars.

---

### 4. Unignorable Polarity

**Purpose:** Take a clear, defensible position that separates you from the supplement industry consensus. Attract the right readers; repel the wrong ones.

**Emotional Goal:** "Finally someone said it." OR "This is uncomfortable but I can't look away."

**What It Contains:**
- A clear stance backed by evidence (not opinion for opinion's sake)
- A "belief statement" that challenges the status quo
- Enough evidence to make the position defensible
- No hedging — state it, support it, move on

**What to Avoid:**
- Controversy for engagement (no clickbait outrage)
- Attacking individuals or brands by name
- Positions you can't support with citations
- Softening the take so much it says nothing

**5 Example Angles:**
1. "If a supplement company won't link to the actual study, they're hiding something. Every citation in this database has a DOI. Every single one."
2. "Evidence tiers should be legally required on supplement labels. Here's what would happen to 70% of the supplements on your shelf."
3. "Most supplement advice is about as evidence-based as my dentist's recommendation to visit every 6 months."
4. "The supplement industry doesn't have a quality problem. It has a transparency problem. The studies exist. They just don't show you the ones that didn't work."
5. "You shouldn't need a PhD to know if your supplement works. The fact that you currently do is a design failure, not a knowledge failure."

---

### 5. Authority Positioning

**Purpose:** Quietly demonstrate competence through work product, methodology, and outcomes. Never claim authority — show it.

**Emotional Goal:** "This person clearly knows what they're doing. I trust this."

**What It Contains:**
- A behind-the-scenes look at methodology, process, or findings
- Specific numbers (93 supplements, 471 papers, 4 tiers)
- Framework explanation or evidence walkthrough
- The work itself as proof — no "I'm an expert" language

**What to Avoid:**
- Self-congratulation
- "As an expert in..."
- Listing credentials or titles
- Making the email about you instead of the work

**5 Example Angles:**
1. "How we classify evidence: the 4-tier system explained in 200 words" — show the methodology transparently
2. "We reviewed 471 papers. Here are the 3 findings that surprised us most."
3. "What happens when you run a supplement through the AI Stack Analyzer" — walk through a real analysis
4. "Every citation in the database links to a DOI. Here's why that matters and what it costs us."
5. "93 supplements. Same methodology. Same evidence criteria. No exceptions. Here's what the data looks like when you remove the marketing."

## EMAIL TEMPLATES BY PILLAR

### Likability Email Template

**Subject Line Rules:**
- First-person, conversational
- Reference a personal detail or quirk
- No exclamation marks
- Under 50 characters when possible

**Opening Pattern:** Start with a personal moment — 1-2 sentences maximum. Then connect it to evidence or supplements within the next sentence. The personal detail earns attention; the connection earns the read.

**Engagement CTA:** "Hit reply and tell me [something low-effort and personal]."

**3 Email Topic Examples:**
1. "I have one cup" — the childhood habit, the simplification metaphor, how one framework works for 93 supplements
2. "My workspace is a mess and I know where everything is" — controlled chaos, visual thinker, the citation system beneath the surface
3. "I studied color grading for fun" — how training your eye for one thing trains it for everything, including supplement evidence

---

### Engagement Email Template

**Subject Line Rules:**
- Ask a question OR make a promise you fulfill with reader interaction
- Imply "your input matters"
- Avoid "quick question" energy

**Opening Pattern:** State a surprising fact or insight (1-2 sentences). Then ask a question that the fact makes them want to answer. The insight creates the itch; the question is the scratch.

**Engagement CTA:** "Reply with [specific, easy-to-answer prompt]."

**3 Email Topic Examples:**
1. "What supplement question keeps you up at night?" — use replies to source future content
2. "How many supplements are you taking right now? Just the number." — reveals buying behavior, opens dialogue
3. "Name one supplement you'd stop taking if the evidence said to." — tests commitment vs. evidence willingness

---

### Dopamine Dealer Email Template

**Subject Line Rules:**
- Lead with the surprising finding, not the topic
- Create a curiosity gap without clickbait
- Specificity over vagueness ("7 types of magnesium" not "the truth about magnesium")

**Opening Pattern:** State the surprising fact immediately — no warm-up. First sentence should make them stop scrolling. Then contextualize it in 2-3 sentences. Close with the implication.

**Engagement CTA:** "See the full evidence breakdown" or "Forward this to someone who takes [supplement]."

**3 Email Topic Examples:**
1. "The supplement with 0 human RCTs and a 4.7 Amazon rating"
2. "Two forms of magnesium have Tier 1 evidence. Five don't."
3. "The most-researched supplement in the database costs less than your morning coffee"

---

### Polarity Email Template

**Subject Line Rules:**
- State the belief clearly — no hedging
- Use "should" or "isn't" language (signals a stance)
- Uncomfortable but defensible

**Opening Pattern:** Lead with the belief in one sentence. Follow with the evidence that supports it. Close with what this means for the reader. No apology. No "I know this is controversial." Just state it.

**Engagement CTA:** "Agree or disagree — reply and tell me." OR "See the evidence for yourself" (link).

**3 Email Topic Examples:**
1. "Evidence tiers should be required on supplement labels"
2. "The supplement industry has a transparency problem, not a quality problem"
3. "If your supplement company won't link their own study, ask yourself why"

---

### Authority Positioning Email Template

**Subject Line Rules:**
- Lead with a number or a process
- Sound like a research update, not a sales pitch
- Understated confidence

**Opening Pattern:** Start with what was done — the work, the process, the methodology. Let the scope speak. "We reviewed X. Here's what we found." Never "I'm proud to announce." The work announces itself.

**Engagement CTA:** "Explore the database" or "Try the analyzer" (link). No "buy now."

**3 Email Topic Examples:**
1. "How we verify every citation in the database (and why it matters)"
2. "93 supplements rated by the same criteria. Here's the methodology."
3. "The AI Stack Analyzer now covers 13 health goals. Here's what changed."

## WEEKLY RHYTHM RECOMMENDATION

**Cadence:** 1 email per week (matching 1-hour/week constraint)

**Rotation Order:**
1. Dopamine Dealer
2. Engagement
3. Polarity
4. Likability
5. Authority

Then repeat. This order is intentional:

- **Dopamine first** — hook new subscribers with an insight they'll remember. Earns the open on the next one.
- **Engagement second** — now that they're paying attention, get them to reply. Builds relationship.
- **Polarity third** — take a stance. Separates you from every other supplement newsletter. People who stay are your people.
- **Likability fourth** — after a polarity email, soften. Show the human. Let them like you again.
- **Authority fifth** — close the cycle by showing the work. Earns trust for the next Dopamine email.

**Time to write:** 15-20 minutes per email using the templates above. Pull from the Input Library for hooks and angles. Do not overthink. Send.

## OPTIMIZATION LOOP

### Weekly Review Checklist (5 minutes, Sunday)

- [ ] Did I send the email? (Seriously. Consistency > perfection.)
- [ ] Which pillar type was it?
- [ ] Open rate vs. last 4 weeks (trending up, flat, or down?)
- [ ] Any replies? What did they say?
- [ ] Any clicks? To what?
- [ ] One thing to try differently next week:

### What to Tweak If...

**Low engagement (low opens, no replies):**
- Subject lines are too generic. Add specificity: numbers, supplement names, or a surprising claim.
- Try leading with a silent thought ("You're probably wasting money on at least one supplement") instead of a topic ("This week: magnesium").
- Move the CTA earlier in the email. People drop off after paragraph 3.

**High opens, low clicks:**
- The email is satisfying enough that they don't need to click. Good for engagement, bad for traffic.
- Add an information gap: give 80% of the insight in the email, make them click for the remaining 20%.
- Make the link text specific: "See magnesium's evidence tier" not "Read more."

**Good engagement, no sales:**
- You're building trust but not connecting it to the offer. This is fine for weeks 1-8. After that:
- Add a soft mention of the Pro offer or a guide in the P.S. line. One sentence. No pitch.
- Use the P.S. as a "by the way" — "P.S. The full magnesium breakdown with dosage protocols and interaction warnings is in the database. Free users see the tier. Pro users see everything."
- Track which pillar type generates the most clicks to the offer. Double down on that one.

**Unsubscribes after polarity emails:**
- This is working correctly. Polarity is designed to filter. If the wrong people unsubscribe, the list gets better.
- Only worry if your most-engaged readers (repliers, clickers) are leaving. If they are, the polarity is too aggressive or off-brand.
- Check: was the stance evidence-backed? If yes, keep going. If it was pure opinion without data, recalibrate.
