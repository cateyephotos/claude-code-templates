## OUTPUT 1: AUTHORITY POSITIONING STATEMENT

**This authority belongs in situations where** someone is about to spend money on a supplement and needs to know whether the evidence justifies the purchase — before they buy, not after.

**It is trusted when** the question is "does this supplement actually work for my specific goal?" and the expected answer is a classification backed by clinical trials, not a recommendation backed by someone's affiliate relationship.

**It deliberately excludes:**
- Medical diagnosis or treatment recommendations
- People looking for someone to confirm what they already want to believe
- Supplement brand endorsements or product-specific purchasing advice
- Anyone who wants an opinion without seeing the evidence behind it

## OUTPUT 4: PAGE-LEVEL AUTHORITY INSTALLATION

### Above the Fold

**What appears:**
- "93 supplements. 471 papers. 4 evidence tiers." (data bar or stat strip)
- Evidence tier badge example (visual — Tier 1 through Tier 4 color-coded)
- "Sources: PubMed | Cochrane | ClinicalTrials.gov" (source attribution line)

**Where:** Homepage hero section, sales page header, landing pages.

**Why the brain responds:** Specificity signals rigor. Named sources signal institutional adjacency. The brain processes "93 supplements, 471 papers" as scope before it processes the marketing copy below it. First impression: research project, not supplement blog.

### Mid-Page Proof Placement

**What appears:**
- Methodology explainer: "How the 4-Tier Evidence Classification System works" (2-3 paragraphs with tier definitions)
- Sample evidence page screenshot showing a real supplement with its tier rating, citation count, and DOI links
- "Every citation is DOI-verified. Click any one to read the original paper."

**Where:** Sales page middle section, About/Methodology page, evidence guide introductions.

**Why the brain responds:** Mid-page is where doubt peaks. The reader has absorbed the promise — now they need proof that the system is real. A visible methodology with verifiable citations satisfies the "show me" instinct. Offering DOI links converts skepticism into trust ("they're daring me to check").

### Objection-Adjacent Proof

**What appears:**
- "Zero supplements sold. Zero affiliate links. Zero industry funding." (conflict of interest disclosure)
- "Don't trust us — verify. Every citation links to the source." (self-verification prompt)
- "The 4-Tier system rates claims, not products. A supplement can be Tier 1 for sleep and Tier 4 for cognition." (nuance signal)

**Where:** Near the pricing section, near the CTA, near any "upgrade to Pro" prompt.

**Why the brain responds:** At the decision point, the brain is looking for reasons NOT to buy. These signals preempt the three biggest objections: "Are they selling supplements?" (no), "Can I trust this?" (verify yourself), "Is this oversimplified?" (nuance shown). Removing objections at the decision point is more effective than adding more benefits.

### Trust Markers Near Decisions

**What appears:**
- "7-day refund. Cancel within 7 days for a full refund." (risk reversal)
- "3 free AI analyses/month. Try it before you decide." (proof of workability)
- Citation count badge: "This page references X peer-reviewed papers" (per-page authority)

**Where:** Directly adjacent to the "Upgrade to Pro" button, pricing cards, checkout page.

**Why the brain responds:** Proximity of trust signals to decision points reduces friction at the exact moment it matters most. The free trial and refund policy eliminate "what if I waste money" fear. Per-page citation counts reinforce that evidence density is real, not a homepage claim.

## OUTPUT 5: ACCESS & FRICTION RECOMMENDATIONS

**Recommended Friction Level:** Low-to-Medium

**Rationale:** Mass Appeal positioning requires low barrier to entry. But some friction is needed to signal that Pro access has real value. The free tier must feel generous enough to build trust; the paid tier must feel earned enough to justify the price.

### Where Access Should Be Earned

1. **Free AI Stack Analysis (3/month):** Low friction. Anyone can try it. This is the believability engine — the "proof it works" moment.

2. **Full Evidence Depth (Pro):** Medium friction. Free users see the evidence tier and a summary. Pro users see the full mechanism analysis, dosage protocols, interaction warnings, and all citations. The gate is on depth, not access.

3. **Future: Annual Plan or B2B API:** Higher friction. Commitment (annual) or qualification (professional use) signals value escalation.

### Language Examples

**Eligibility Statement (for Pro):**
> "Pro is for people who want the full evidence picture — not just the tier, but the dosage, the mechanism, the interactions, and every citation. If you're making supplement decisions based on evidence, this is the depth that makes that possible."

**Boundary Framing (what Pro is not):**
> "SupplementDB Pro is not medical advice. It's evidence access. We rate what the research shows. Your decisions are yours."

**Earned Progression (post-free-analysis prompt):**
> "You've seen the evidence tier. Pro shows you why — the trials, the dosages, the mechanisms, and the interaction warnings behind the rating."

**Waitlist/Application:** Not recommended at current stage. Mass Appeal positioning + pre-revenue status means removing barriers, not adding them. Revisit when demand exceeds capacity.

## OUTPUT 6: BELIEVABILITY ENGINE

### "This will work for you if..."

> **SupplementDB Pro is built for you if:**
> - You take supplements but aren't sure which ones have real evidence behind them
> - You've tried Googling supplement research and felt overwhelmed within 10 minutes
> - You want to know the right dosage, not the manufacturer's recommended dosage
> - You're worried about interactions but don't know where to check
> - You don't have a science background and don't think you should need one
>
> **It's not for you if:**
> - You want someone to tell you supplements are all great (some aren't)
> - You're looking for a supplement store (we don't sell products)
> - You want medical advice (we provide evidence access, not diagnoses)

### Objection-Specific Reassurance

**"I can find this information for free."**
> You can. It takes about 5 hours per supplement if you know where to look. PubMed alone has 8 million abstracts. The database organizes 471 papers into evidence tiers so you don't have to read them. You're not paying for the information — you're paying for the classification.

**"I'm not scientific enough to use this."**
> The evidence tier system has 4 levels: Strong, Moderate, Emerging, Preliminary. If you can read a food nutrition label, you can read an evidence tier. The complexity is in the research. The output is designed to be clear.

**"I've been burned by supplement sites before."**
> We don't sell supplements. Zero affiliate links. Zero industry funding. Every citation has a DOI — click it and read the original paper yourself. The business model is the evidence, not the products. If a supplement has weak evidence, we say so. That's the point.

**"What if I pay and don't use it?"**
> 7-day refund. No questions. And: the free AI Stack Analysis is available before you pay anything. Try it. If the output isn't useful, don't upgrade.

### The Early Win Moment

**What they see:** The AI Stack Analyzer input screen. Two fields: health goals and current supplements.

**What they do:** Enter their top health goal (e.g., "sleep") and 1-3 supplements they currently take. Click "Analyze."

**What they feel after:** The analysis returns in under 60 seconds. It shows evidence tiers for their current supplements, flags any interactions, suggests alternatives with stronger evidence, and cites specific papers. The feeling: "I just learned more about my supplements in 47 seconds than I did in the last year of Googling."

**Why this works:** The early win is immediate, personalized, and verifiable. It's about their supplements, their goals, their stack. Personalization eliminates the "is this relevant to me?" doubt. Speed eliminates the "will this take too long?" objection. DOI links eliminate the "can I trust this?" concern.
