## STEP 1: OUTCOME VALUE MAP (Component 1)

### A. Outcome Statement

> "Stop guessing which supplements work and start knowing — backed by 471 clinical papers, rated by evidence tier, personalized to your health goals."

### B. 5 Value Drivers the Buyer Is Paying For

1. **Certainty** — know whether a supplement has strong, moderate, emerging, or preliminary evidence before buying it
2. **Speed** — replace 5+ hours/month of research with a 47-second AI analysis
3. **Safety** — interaction warnings and dosage protocols derived from clinical trials, not manufacturer labels
4. **Avoided mistakes** — stop spending $50-200/month on supplements with Tier 3-4 evidence
5. **Confidence** — make supplement decisions without needing a science degree or trusting marketing

### C. 3 Measurable Proxies for Value

1. **Time saved:** 5+ hours/month of DIY research eliminated. At minimum wage ($15/hr), that's $75/month in time value. At professional rates ($50+/hr), it's $250+/month.
2. **Money saved:** The average supplement user spends $50-200/month. If even one purchase per month is redirected from a Tier 4 to a Tier 1 supplement, the subscription pays for itself.
3. **Risk reduced:** Interaction warnings prevent potentially dangerous combinations. One prevented interaction is worth more than any subscription fee.

## STEP 3: POSITIONING DECISION (Component 3)

**Chosen Positioning:** Mass Appeal

### A. What the Buyer Must Feel (3 Emotional States)

1. **"This is obviously worth it."** The price should feel like a non-decision relative to what they already spend on supplements.
2. **"I'm getting access to something real."** The evidence depth, citation count, and AI analysis should make the price feel like they found a back door to professional-grade data.
3. **"I'm smarter than 95% of supplement users now."** The purchase should feel like an intelligence upgrade, not a subscription burden.

### B. What the Offer Must Signal (3 Signals)

1. **Accessibility** — anyone can afford this, no matter their supplement budget
2. **Substance** — this is research infrastructure, not a wellness app
3. **Fairness** — the price reflects the value without exploiting the complexity of the problem

### C. What Pricing Must Avoid (3 Red Flags)

1. **Too cheap** — below $5/month signals "hobby project" or "not real data." Undermines the authority of 471 papers.
2. **Complex tiers** — more than 3 options creates decision fatigue for a mass audience. Keep it clean.
3. **Discount-first culture** — never lead with discounts, coupons, or "limited time" urgency. Calm confidence. The evidence is the urgency.

## STEP 6: FINAL RECOMMENDATION + DECISION RULES

### Recommended Model: MODEL A — MASS APPEAL

**Why it wins, by component:**

1. **Outcome-Based Pricing:** $9.99/month is trivially justified by one prevented bad purchase ($15-60/month saved) or 5 hours of research replaced. The outcome value exceeds the price by 5-20x. The buyer doesn't need to calculate — it's obvious.

2. **Market Conditioning:** At $9.99/month, SupplementDB is 66% cheaper than Examine ($29/month), 80% cheaper than Natural Medicines ($300+/year equivalent), and costs less than a single nutritionist question. Every comparison makes the price feel generous. The buyer's reference points all point to "this should cost more than it does."

3. **Positioning Choice:** Mass Appeal requires the price to feel like a non-decision. $9.99/month is less than most individual supplement bottles. The price removes itself as an obstacle, which means the evidence quality and AI functionality become the deciding factors — where SupplementDB is strongest.

4. **Anchoring & Priming:** The three-anchor strategy (professional DB at $300+, nutritionist at $100-300/visit, DIY at 5hrs/month) frames $9.99 as an absurd bargain without ever using the word "bargain." The anchors appear before the price, training the brain's reference point upward.

5. **Price as Signal:** $9.99/month signals "accessible, serious, fair." It says: "We could charge more, but we'd rather more people have access to evidence." This aligns with the zero-affiliate, zero-industry-funding brand positioning. The price IS the authority signal — it says "we're not here to maximize revenue, we're here to fix a transparency problem."

6. **Justification Story:** "You're paying for the classification, not the science" is a clean narrative. The research already exists in PubMed. The value is in organizing, classifying, and personalizing it. $9.99/month for a classification layer on top of 471 papers is self-evidently reasonable.

7. **Presentation Psychology:** Three tiers (Free / PDF / Pro) give the buyer a clear progression without decision fatigue. Free builds trust. PDFs capture impulse buyers at $9. Pro captures committed users at $9.99/month. The tiering matches the buyer's journey: browse > learn > commit.

### Revenue Priority: PDF Guides at $9

Given the stated revenue priority (PDF guide sales), Model A optimizes for this:

- **Free tier** drives traffic and builds trust
- **PDF guides at $9** are the lowest-friction paid conversion — one-time, no commitment, specific to a health goal
- **Pro at $9.99/month** is the upsell for users who buy 2+ guides and realize the subscription includes all of them

**The PDF-first funnel:**
1. Free user browses the database
2. They discover a health domain they care about (sleep, cognition, stress)
3. The evidence guide for that domain is $9 — less than the supplement they're evaluating
4. After purchase, the thank-you page shows: "This guide is included free with Pro ($9.99/month) — along with 7 more guides and 25 AI analyses"
5. Users who buy 2+ guides convert to Pro because the math is obvious

### What to Adjust

- **Tier names:** Keep them simple. "Free," "Guides" (or just the guide name), "Pro." No clever branding needed.
- **Guide pricing:** $9 each is the right price point — less than $10 removes the "let me think about it" threshold. If a guide has exceptional depth, $12 is acceptable. Never above $15 for mass appeal.
- **Pro annual pricing:** $79/year (33% savings vs. monthly) incentivizes commitment without feeling like a sale.

### 3 Pricing Non-Negotiables

1. **Never discount Pro.** No coupons, no "limited time" pricing, no Black Friday deals. The price is $9.99/month because that's what it's worth. Discounting signals "it was overpriced before." Calm confidence means the price doesn't need to be justified by urgency.

2. **Always show anchors before the price.** The professional database price ($300+), the nutritionist cost ($100-300), and the time cost (5 hrs/month) must appear on the sales page BEFORE the $9.99 reveal. Anchor order is non-negotiable — the brain needs the reference points first.

3. **Free tier must remain generous.** The free tier is not a crippled version of Pro. It's a real tool that builds real trust. 3 AI analyses/month and full database browsing (with tier ratings visible) must stay free. The free tier IS the sales funnel. Reducing it to drive conversions will destroy the authority positioning that makes the price work.
