About SupplementDB

An open-access, evidence-based supplement research database

1. Our Mission

SupplementDB exists to bridge the gap between published scientific research and accessible supplement information. In a market saturated with unsubstantiated health claims and marketing-driven content, we provide a curated, peer-reviewed research database that empowers informed decision-making.

Our database currently covers 113 supplements across 20 categories, backed by 1,300+ peer-reviewed research papers from leading journals. Every data point is traceable to its original published source via verified DOI and PMID identifiers.

In plain language: We compile and organize the actual scientific research on supplements so you can see what the evidence really says, rather than relying on marketing claims.

2. Research Methodology

Our evidence curation process follows a systematic approach designed to ensure accuracy, traceability, and scientific rigor:

Literature Search

We conduct systematic searches across major biomedical databases, with primary sources including PubMed, PubMed Central (PMC), and preprint servers such as bioRxiv and medRxiv. Search strategies prioritize randomized controlled trials (RCTs), systematic reviews, meta-analyses, and network meta-analyses.

Citation Verification

Every citation in the database undergoes a multi-step verification process:

  • DOI validation — Each Digital Object Identifier is verified to resolve to the correct published paper
  • PMID confirmation — PubMed IDs are cross-referenced against the National Library of Medicine records
  • Author and journal verification — Publication details are confirmed against the original source
  • Study type classification — Each study is categorized (RCT, meta-analysis, cohort study, etc.) based on its design
  • Sample size recording — Participant counts are extracted and documented for transparency

Data Extraction

For each supplement, we extract and organize the following from the published literature:

  • Primary and secondary health benefits with supporting evidence
  • Dosage ranges with standardization requirements, as used in clinical studies
  • Optimal study durations observed in trials
  • Mechanisms of action documented in the research
  • Safety profiles, including side effects, contraindications, and drug interactions
  • Effect sizes where reported (Cohen's d, standardized mean difference, odds ratio)

In plain language: We search the major medical research databases, verify every citation is real and correct, and extract the key findings in a standardized format so you can compare supplements consistently.

3. Evidence Rating System

Each supplement in the database receives an evidence tier rating based on the overall quality, quantity, and consistency of the available research. Our four-tier system provides a quick assessment of how well-supported a supplement's claimed benefits are.

Tier Label Criteria Typical Study Base
1 Strong Evidence Consistent findings across multiple large RCTs, supported by systematic reviews or meta-analyses 5+ RCTs, 1+ meta-analyses, large sample sizes (n > 200 per study)
2 Moderate Evidence Multiple RCTs with moderate sample sizes showing generally consistent results 3+ RCTs, moderate sample sizes (n = 50–200), some meta-analytic support
3 Preliminary Evidence Limited RCTs available; evidence drawn primarily from smaller trials, observational studies, or mixed results 1–2 RCTs, mostly small samples (n < 50), observational data
4 Emerging Evidence Primarily preclinical research (cell/animal studies) or very early-phase human investigations In vitro/animal studies, pilot trials, mechanistic investigations

Important note: Evidence tiers reflect the state of published research, not the effectiveness of a supplement. A Tier 4 rating does not mean a supplement is ineffective — it means the current body of human clinical research is limited. Conversely, a Tier 1 rating does not guarantee a supplement will work for every individual.

In plain language: Our tier system tells you how much high-quality research exists for each supplement. More research = higher tier. It measures the strength of the evidence, not a recommendation.

4. Data Sources & Citation Standards

The SupplementDB citation library draws from the following primary sources:

  • PubMed / MEDLINE — The U.S. National Library of Medicine's database of biomedical literature, containing 36+ million citations
  • PubMed Central (PMC) — Full-text archive of biomedical and life sciences journal literature
  • Cochrane Library — Systematic reviews recognized as the highest standard of evidence-based healthcare
  • Clinical trial registries — ClinicalTrials.gov and international equivalents for ongoing and completed trials
  • Peer-reviewed journals — Including The Lancet, BMJ, JAMA, Nature, Phytomedicine, Nutrients, Journal of Clinical Psychopharmacology, and field-specific publications

We prioritize the following study types, in order of evidence strength:

  1. Systematic reviews and meta-analyses
  2. Network meta-analyses
  3. Randomized controlled trials (RCTs)
  4. Cohort and observational studies
  5. Mechanistic and preclinical studies (for Tier 4 supplements)

Every citation in the database includes: title, author(s), year, journal, DOI, PMID (where available), study type, sample size, and key findings.

5. Editorial Standards

All content on SupplementDB adheres to the following editorial principles:

  • Evidence-first approach — No supplement is included or rated based on popularity, commercial interest, or anecdotal reports. Inclusion requires published research
  • Neutral reporting — We present findings as reported in the literature without advocacy for or against any supplement
  • Transparent sourcing — Every claim is traceable to its original citation via DOI and/or PMID links
  • Regular updates — The database is updated as new significant research is published, with update dates tracked per supplement
  • Conflict of interest disclosure — SupplementDB does not sell supplements, accept advertising from supplement manufacturers, or receive funding from the supplement industry

In plain language: We don't sell anything, we don't accept industry money, and we only include supplements that have been studied in published research. Our goal is to be the most transparent and unbiased supplement reference available.

7. Limitations & Disclaimers

While we strive for accuracy and comprehensiveness, important limitations apply:

  • Not medical advice — SupplementDB is an informational resource, not a healthcare provider. Always consult a qualified healthcare professional before starting any supplement regimen. See our full medical disclaimer
  • Research lag — There is an inherent delay between when research is published and when it is incorporated into the database
  • Publication bias — Published research may over-represent positive findings. We note this limitation where relevant
  • Individual variation — Research findings reflect population averages. Individual responses to supplements vary based on genetics, health status, medications, and other factors
  • Regulatory status — Dietary supplements are not FDA-approved for the treatment of any disease. Our evidence ratings reflect research quality, not regulatory approval
  • Coverage scope — Not all commercially available supplements are included. We prioritize those with meaningful published research
