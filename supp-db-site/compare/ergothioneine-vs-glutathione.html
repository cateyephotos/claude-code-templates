Summary

Quick Verdict

Glutathione (Tier 2) is the most abundant endogenous cellular antioxidant with extensive clinical literature, but oral glutathione has historically poor bioavailability — most studied formulations are liposomal, sublingual or precursor-based (NAC, GlyNAC). Ergothioneine (Tier 3) has the opposite profile: it is highly bioavailable orally and retained in tissues for weeks via the OCTN1/SLC22A4 transporter, but the human evidence base is thinner (one large prospective cohort + one small pilot RCT). They occupy complementary positions in the cellular antioxidant network: glutathione is the workhorse intracellular reducing agent, ergothioneine acts as a backup scavenger that accumulates specifically in oxidatively-stressed tissues and helps recycle oxidised glutathione.

Pharmacology

Mechanisms of Action

Ergothioneine
  1. OCTN1/SLC22A4-mediated tissue accumulation
  2. Hydroxyl radical scavenging (histidine betaine)
  3. Singlet oxygen quenching
  4. Direct activation of mitochondrial 3-mercaptopyruvate sulfurtransferase (MPST)
  5. Metal chelation (Fe2+, Cu2+)
  6. Nrf2 pathway modulation
  7. Reduced glutathione regeneration cycle support
Glutathione
  1. Direct scavenging of reactive oxygen species (ROS) and reactive nitrogen species (RNS)
  2. Glutathione peroxidase (GPx) cofactor for hydrogen peroxide and lipid peroxide detoxification
  3. Phase II detoxification via glutathione S-transferase (GST) conjugation reactions
  4. Maintenance of thiol redox balance (GSH/GSSG ratio) in cells
  5. Regeneration of other antioxidants including vitamins C and E
  6. Regulation of immune cell proliferation and NK cell cytotoxicity
  7. Inhibition of tyrosinase activity reducing melanin synthesis in skin
  8. Mitochondrial membrane integrity protection against oxidative damage

Decision Guide

Who Should Choose Which?

Cellular detoxification support
Glutathione
Glutathione is the central substrate for hepatic Phase II conjugation reactions and the workhorse intracellular reductant.
Reliable oral bioavailability and tissue retention
Ergothioneine
OCTN1-mediated active uptake gives ergothioneine weeks-to-months tissue half-life; oral glutathione is rapidly degraded and poorly absorbed.
Long-term mortality / cardiovascular signal
Ergothioneine
Smith 2020 (Heart, n=3236) shows long-term inverse association with cardiovascular and all-cause mortality — no equivalent prospective dataset for plasma glutathione.
Acute oxidative-stress events (ischaemia, paracetamol toxicity)
Glutathione (or NAC precursor)
Glutathione repletion via NAC has emergency-medicine indications; no equivalent acute role for ergothioneine.
Targeting immune-cell antioxidant defense (neutrophils, monocytes)
Ergothioneine
OCTN1 expression concentrates ergothioneine in neutrophils, monocytes and macrophages — cells with high oxidative burst.
Budget and convenience
Ergothioneine
Effective doses are 5–30 mg/day and tissue retention allows intermittent dosing; clinically meaningful glutathione products are typically more expensive per effective dose.

Combination Protocol

Can You Stack Ergothioneine & Glutathione?

Stacking Verdict

Yes — Ergothioneine and Glutathione (or its precursors NAC/GlyNAC) are highly compatible. Ergothioneine concentrates inside oxidatively-stressed tissues and is reported to support the reduced glutathione regeneration cycle, so the two can act as a coordinated network rather than compete. Typical stacking: Ergothioneine 5–25 mg/day plus liposomal glutathione 250–1000 mg/day or GlyNAC at trial-validated doses. No reported adverse interactions. The stack is reasonable for users prioritising cellular antioxidant breadth.
