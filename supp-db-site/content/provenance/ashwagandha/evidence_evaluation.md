# Evidence Evaluation: Ashwagandha (Withania somnifera)
## Pipeline Run: 2026-03-05

### Evaluation Framework
- **Primary:** GRADE (Grading of Recommendations Assessment, Development and Evaluation)
- **Quality scoring:** Custom composite (see tier_assignment.md)

### Per-Study Evaluation

#### Study: alsanie_2026_mental_health_dose_response -- Effects on Mental Health: Dose-Response Meta-Analysis
- **Citation:** Alsanie SA et al. 2026, Complementary Therapies in Medicine 97:103325
- **PMID:** 41644067
- **DOI:** 10.1016/j.ctim.2026.103325
- **Study type:** Dose-response meta-analysis (22 RCTs)
- **Key finding:** Large effect sizes across all three mental health domains: stress SMD=-5.88, anxiety SMD=-6.87, depression SMD=-5.68. Dose-response relationships established for each outcome.
- **GRADE domains:**
  - Risk of bias: Some concerns -- while the meta-analysis itself is well-conducted with dose-response modeling, the underlying 22 RCTs have variable quality. Many ashwagandha RCTs are industry-funded and conducted in India, raising questions about independence and generalizability. However, the large study count (22) dilutes the influence of any individual low-quality trial
  - Inconsistency: Not serious -- consistent direction of effect across all three domains (stress, anxiety, depression). The extremely large SMD values suggest potential scale-related inflation rather than true inconsistency
  - Indirectness: Not serious -- directly assesses oral ashwagandha (Withania somnifera) root extract supplementation for mental health outcomes in adults
  - Imprecision: Not serious -- 22 RCTs provide adequate statistical power for pooled estimates and dose-response modeling
  - Publication bias: Possible concern -- the extremely large SMD values (5.68-6.87) are unusual and may reflect selective reporting of positive results, small study effects, or scale-specific inflation. These values are substantially larger than what is typically seen in supplement meta-analyses
- **Overall GRADE certainty:** Moderate
- **Assigned evidence level:** Level 1
- **Quality score contribution:** 8/10
- **Notes:** This is the largest meta-analysis of ashwagandha for mental health outcomes (22 RCTs) and the first to establish dose-response relationships. The dose-response analysis is a critical advancement: previous meta-analyses only reported pooled effects without quantifying how outcomes change across dosing ranges. However, the extremely large SMD values (5.88-6.87) warrant cautious interpretation. SMD values above 2.0 are uncommon in supplement research and may be inflated by (1) use of non-standardized outcome scales across studies, (2) small individual study sample sizes amplifying effect estimates, or (3) heterogeneity in ashwagandha extract types and dosing protocols. The dose-response data itself is the primary value of this paper, regardless of the absolute pooled effect sizes.

#### Study: marchi_2025_mental_disorders -- Effect on Mental Health Symptoms in Mental Disorders
- **Citation:** Marchi M et al. 2025, BJPsych Open 11(6):e260
- **PMID:** 41140145
- **DOI:** 10.1192/bjo.2025.10885
- **Study type:** Meta-analysis (14 studies, 713 participants)
- **Key finding:** Anxiety SMD=-1.13 (95% CI: significant), depression SMD=-1.28, stress SMD=-0.95, sleep SMD=-1.35. All four outcomes showed statistically significant improvements with ashwagandha supplementation.
- **GRADE domains:**
  - Risk of bias: Some concerns -- 14 studies is adequate for a meta-analysis, but included studies span heterogeneous mental disorder diagnoses. The population heterogeneity (anxiety disorders, depression, stress-related conditions) may introduce confounding
  - Inconsistency: Not serious -- consistent direction and magnitude of effect across anxiety, depression, stress, and sleep domains. SMD values range from -0.95 to -1.35, all in the large-effect range, showing internal consistency
  - Indirectness: Not serious -- directly assesses oral ashwagandha supplementation in adults with mental health conditions. The focus on clinical populations (mental disorders) adds clinical relevance beyond healthy volunteer studies
  - Imprecision: Some concerns -- 713 participants across 14 studies is adequate but on the lower end for a meta-analysis. Individual study sample sizes may be small, widening confidence intervals
  - Publication bias: Possible concern -- funnel plot symmetry with only 14 studies is difficult to reliably assess. Small study effects are possible
- **Overall GRADE certainty:** Moderate
- **Assigned evidence level:** Level 1
- **Quality score contribution:** 8/10
- **Notes:** Published in BJPsych Open, a Q1 psychiatry journal from the Royal College of Psychiatrists, which adds substantial clinical credibility. The focus on mental health disorder populations (rather than healthy adults reporting subjective stress) distinguishes this from most ashwagandha meta-analyses and makes the findings more clinically relevant. The sleep outcome (SMD=-1.35) is particularly valuable as it adds to the sleep evidence domain with a large effect size. The SMD values in this study (0.95-1.35) are more conservative than Alsanie 2026 (5.68-6.87), which may reflect better-controlled studies or more clinically validated outcome measures. The moderate SMD range (-0.95 to -1.35) represents clinically meaningful large effects that are more plausible than the extreme values in Alsanie 2026.

#### Study: fornalik_2026_hormonal -- Hormonal Modulation with Withania somnifera
- **Citation:** Fornalik M et al. 2026, Planta Medica
- **PMID:** 41740946
- **DOI:** 10.1055/a-2802-8363
- **Study type:** Meta-analysis (23 trials, 1706 participants)
- **Key finding:** Cortisol SMD=-1.18 (significant reduction), testosterone in men MD=57.43 ng/dl (significant increase), serotonin MD=31.75 ng/ml (significant increase). Comprehensive hormonal endpoint coverage.
- **GRADE domains:**
  - Risk of bias: Low -- 23 trials with 1706 participants provide robust pooled estimates. Planta Medica (Georg Thieme Verlag) is a well-established Q1 phytomedicine journal with rigorous peer review
  - Inconsistency: Not serious -- cortisol reduction is consistent with ashwagandha's established adaptogenic mechanism. Testosterone increase in men is biologically plausible and consistent with prior individual RCTs. Serotonin increase provides mechanistic support for anxiolytic and mood effects
  - Indirectness: Not serious -- directly assesses oral ashwagandha supplementation and serum hormonal endpoints. Cortisol, testosterone, and serotonin are clinically relevant biomarkers
  - Imprecision: Not serious -- 1706 participants across 23 trials provide adequate statistical power. This is one of the largest ashwagandha meta-analyses by participant count
  - Publication bias: Low concern -- 23 trials provide reasonable funnel plot assessment. The inclusion of both positive and null findings across different hormonal endpoints suggests balanced reporting
- **Overall GRADE certainty:** Moderate-High
- **Assigned evidence level:** Level 1
- **Quality score contribution:** 9/10
- **Notes:** This is the first comprehensive hormonal modulation meta-analysis for ashwagandha, covering three major hormonal pathways. The cortisol reduction (SMD=-1.18) provides quantitative support for ashwagandha's adaptogenic mechanism -- the primary biological rationale for its stress-reducing effects. The testosterone increase in men (MD=57.43 ng/dl) is clinically meaningful: typical normal testosterone ranges are 300-1000 ng/dl, so a 57.43 ng/dl increase represents roughly a 5-19% improvement depending on baseline, which may have practical significance for men with low-normal testosterone. The serotonin finding (MD=31.75 ng/ml) provides a mechanistic link between ashwagandha supplementation and its anxiolytic/antidepressant effects, as serotonin is the primary neurotransmitter targeted by SSRIs. This study is the most important mechanistic contribution in this update, bridging the gap between clinical outcome data (anxiety, stress reduction) and biological mechanism (cortisol, serotonin, testosterone modulation).

#### Study: arumugam_2024_stress_anxiety -- Effects on Stress and Anxiety: Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis
- **Citation:** Arumugam V et al. 2024, Explore 20(6):103062
- **PMID:** 39348746
- **DOI:** 10.1016/j.explore.2024.103062
- **Study type:** Systematic review and meta-analysis
- **Key finding:** Significant reductions in stress and anxiety measures with ashwagandha supplementation.
- **GRADE domains:**
  - Risk of bias: Some concerns -- standard ashwagandha meta-analysis limitations (variable RCT quality, some industry funding)
  - Inconsistency: Not serious -- findings consistent with other meta-analyses in this domain
  - Indirectness: Not serious -- directly assesses oral ashwagandha for stress and anxiety
  - Imprecision: Not serious -- adequate sample for pooled estimates
  - Publication bias: Possible concern -- small study effects possible
- **Overall GRADE certainty:** Moderate
- **Assigned evidence level:** Level 2
- **Quality score contribution:** 7/10
- **Notes:** Already in database. Confirmed as still relevant and not superseded by the newer meta-analyses. While Alsanie 2026 and Marchi 2025 provide larger and more recent pooled estimates, Arumugam 2024 contributes an independent confirmation from a different research group. Retained for replication value.

#### Study: fatima_2024_anxiety_insomnia -- Safety and Efficacy for Anxiety and Insomnia
- **Citation:** Fatima K et al. 2024, Human Psychopharmacology 39(6):e2911
- **PMID:** 39083548
- **DOI:** 10.1002/hup.2911
- **Study type:** Systematic review and meta-analysis
- **Key finding:** Ashwagandha is both safe and efficacious for anxiety and insomnia. Safety profile favorable with no serious adverse events reported.
- **GRADE domains:**
  - Risk of bias: Low -- published in Human Psychopharmacology (respected psychopharmacology journal). Dual focus on both safety and efficacy provides balanced assessment
  - Inconsistency: Not serious -- safety and efficacy findings consistent with broader literature
  - Indirectness: Not serious -- directly assesses oral ashwagandha for anxiety and insomnia with explicit safety evaluation
  - Imprecision: Not serious -- adequate sample for safety and efficacy conclusions
  - Publication bias: Low concern -- safety-focused studies are less susceptible to positive publication bias since null safety findings (no adverse events) are the desired outcome
- **Overall GRADE certainty:** Moderate
- **Assigned evidence level:** Level 2
- **Quality score contribution:** 7/10
- **Notes:** Already in database. Particularly valuable for its explicit safety evaluation, which remains current. The combined safety + efficacy design provides balanced evidence. Retained for safety data continuity -- the favorable safety profile documented here is reinforced by the hormonal data in Fornalik 2026 (no concerning hormonal disruptions at recommended doses).

### Aggregate Quality Metrics
- **Average study quality:** 7.8/10
- **Evidence level distribution:** Level 1: 3 papers (all new), Level 2: 2 papers (both existing)
- **Replication assessment:** Strong -- five independent meta-analyses from different research groups across multiple countries converge on consistent anxiolytic, stress-reducing, and sleep-improving effects
- **Geographic diversity:** Saudi Arabia (Alsanie), Italy (Marchi), Poland (Fornalik), India (Arumugam), Pakistan (Fatima)
- **Positive/negative balance:** All 5 studies report positive findings for ashwagandha. No negative or null meta-analyses were identified in the 2024-2026 literature. This uniformly positive profile is notable but also raises the question of whether null findings are underreported in the ashwagandha literature
- **Effect size calibration note:** SMD values vary substantially across studies (Alsanie: 5.68-6.87 vs. Marchi: 0.95-1.35). The Marchi 2025 values are more clinically plausible and should be used as the primary reference for effect size communication. The Alsanie 2026 values may be inflated by methodological factors and should be cited primarily for dose-response relationships rather than absolute effect sizes
