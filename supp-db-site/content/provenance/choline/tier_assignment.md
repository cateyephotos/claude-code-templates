## Tier Decision Tree

### Step 1 — Screen for Tier 1 (Strong consistent large-N RCT evidence)

**Criterion:** Multiple large-N (≥200 participants) RCTs with consistent results, strong effect sizes, and Cochrane or equivalent systematic review with High GRADE certainty across primary benefit domain.

**Evaluation:**

- **Cochrane review:** No Cochrane systematic review exists for choline supplementation benefits. Wallace & Fulgoni 2017 (Nutrition Reviews) is a systematic review but NOT Cochrane methodology and does not include a GRADE assessment.
- **Large-N RCT evidence:** Caudill et al. 2018 (Nutrients) is an RCT for maternal supplementation but is a single moderate-N study. No multiple large-N (≥200) RCTs with consistent results for any single benefit domain.
- **GRADE certainty for positive benefit:** Not assessed in existing systematic reviews; would likely be Moderate given single RCT for maternal outcomes and observational data for cognitive outcomes.
- **IOM Adequate Intake (AI):** While the IOM 1998 reference establishes choline as an essential nutrient with AI values, an AI represents the estimated average intake level assumed to be adequate based on approximation — it is not the same as High GRADE Cochrane RCT evidence for a supplementation benefit.

**Decision: Tier 1 NOT met** — No Cochrane review; no multiple large-N RCTs demonstrating consistent High GRADE benefit across a primary domain; IOM AI classification does not substitute for clinical intervention evidence at Tier 1 level → Proceed to Step 2

---

### Step 2 — Screen for Tier 2 (Moderate multi-RCT or systematic review evidence)

**Criterion:** Systematic review or meta-analysis with ≥3 RCTs pooled OR equivalent authoritative evidence base, statistically significant effect, GRADE Moderate or above, effect size sufficient for clinical relevance, no major safety concerns.

**Evaluation:**

- ✅ **Systematic review exists:** Wallace & Fulgoni 2017 (Nutrition Reviews) — comprehensive systematic review covering lifespan benefits including cognitive, developmental, and hepatic outcomes; published in top nutrition journal; multi-domain coverage
- ✅ **RCT evidence in primary benefit domain:** Caudill et al. 2018 (Nutrients) — controlled supplementation trial demonstrating improved infant cognitive outcomes from maternal choline supplementation; statistically significant; clinically relevant outcome (information processing speed)
- ✅ **Authoritative dietary reference:** IOM 1998 Adequate Intake establishes 425–550 mg/day as appropriate; 3.5g/day Upper Tolerable Intake Level; this is authoritative consensus evidence supporting the dosage framework
- ✅ **Population-level epidemiological evidence:** Poly et al. 2011 (Framingham cohort, AJCN) and Fischer et al. 2007 (NHANES III, AJCN) provide large-N observational evidence for cognitive and hepatic benefits respectively
- ✅ **Clinical deficiency evidence:** Buchman et al. 1995 (Hepatology) — clinical demonstration of choline deficiency causing reversible NAFLD; provides causal evidence (not merely associational) for the liver health claim
- ✅ **Well-established mechanistic basis:** Zeisel 2009, Blusztajn 2017, Li 2008 provide comprehensive mechanistic coverage across acetylcholine synthesis, phospholipid membrane biosynthesis, methylation pathways, and epigenetic regulation
- ✅ **No major safety concerns:** Safety well-characterized through IOM 1998 UL, Conlay 1992, Taylor 2017; no serious adverse events at therapeutic doses; mild fishy odor at high doses is the primary concern

**Decision: Tier 2 CONFIRMED** — Systematic review (Wallace 2017) with comprehensive coverage; RCT evidence for maternal supplementation (Caudill 2018); authoritative AI reference (IOM 1998); large-N observational evidence for cognitive and hepatic domains; clinical deficiency causation demonstrated; robust mechanistic basis; no major safety concerns

## Mandatory Notes

### No Cochrane Review Available

No Cochrane systematic review exists for choline supplementation benefits as of the pipeline run date (2026-03-06). The Wallace & Fulgoni 2017 systematic review in Nutrition Reviews is the closest equivalent but does not use Cochrane methodology and does not report GRADE ratings. This absence is the primary ceiling on evidence quality and the primary reason Tier 1 cannot be assigned.

### Population Inadequacy Note

The >90% inadequate intake among pregnant women (Wallace et al. 2018) is a public health finding that strengthens the case for choline supplementation during pregnancy but does not itself constitute clinical intervention evidence. Consumer-facing content should clearly distinguish between deficiency prevention (where the case is strong) and cognitive enhancement in non-deficient populations (where the case is Moderate-observational).

### Choline Form Note

This file covers choline bitartrate specifically. The clinical trial literature includes phosphatidylcholine, CDP-choline (citicoline), and alpha-GPC forms as well, which may have different bioavailability and efficacy profiles. The IOM AI and UL values apply to total choline intake regardless of form. The dosage calculation (choline bitartrate provides ~41% choline by weight) is confirmed by standard nutritional chemistry.

## Tier Assignment Confidence Statement

**Confidence: High that Tier 2 is correct.**

Choline meets Tier 2 criteria:
1. **Systematic review (Wallace 2017, Nutr Rev):** Comprehensive lifespan benefits coverage; multi-domain
2. **RCT evidence in maternal domain (Caudill 2018, Nutrients):** Controlled supplementation demonstrating improved infant cognitive outcomes
3. **Authoritative essential nutrient classification (IOM 1998):** AI and UL established; evidence base for dietary requirements is mature
4. **Clinical deficiency causation (Buchman 1995, Hepatology):** Reversible NAFLD in choline-deficient patients; causal evidence
5. **Robust mechanistic basis (Zeisel 2009, Blusztajn 2017, Li 2008):** Acetylcholine synthesis, phospholipid membranes, methylation all well-characterized
6. **Safety:** Well-established; no serious AEs at therapeutic doses; UL set at 3.5g/day

**Conditions for Tier 1 elevation:**
- Publication of a Cochrane systematic review of choline supplementation achieving GRADE High certainty for a positive benefit domain (most likely: maternal supplementation for offspring cognitive outcomes), OR
- Multiple (≥2) large-N (≥200) independent RCTs demonstrating consistent cognitive benefit in aging adults or consistent maternal supplementation → offspring outcome benefit, with GRADE High certainty

**Conditions that would require Tier downgrade:**
- Evidence that the Wallace 2017 systematic review contains major methodological flaws reducing its weight to case-series level
- Major safety signal at therapeutic doses (no current indication this is likely)
- Discovery that Caudill 2018's cognitive outcome improvements are not replicated in independent trials (single RCT replication concern)
