### Per-Study Evaluation

#### Study: marshall_2026_cognition_sr -- Creatine and Cognition in Aging
- **Citation:** Marshall et al. 2026, Nutritional Neuroscience
- **PMID:** 40971619
- **DOI:** 10.1080/1028415X.2025.2510879
- **Study type:** Systematic review (6 studies, 1542 participants)
- **Key finding:** 83.3% of included studies (5/6) reported positive effects on cognition in aging adults. Memory and attention were the most consistently improved domains.
- **GRADE domains:**
  - Risk of bias: Low -- well-conducted systematic review with clear methodology, transparent inclusion criteria, and structured quality assessment
  - Inconsistency: Some concerns -- 1 of 6 studies did not find significant effects, and included studies used heterogeneous cognitive test batteries
  - Indirectness: Not serious -- directly assesses oral creatine supplementation and cognitive outcomes in aging adults
  - Imprecision: Some concerns -- while n=1542 is adequate, heterogeneity in outcome measures limits pooled precision
  - Publication bias: Possible concern -- small number of studies (6) limits funnel plot reliability; positive results may be overrepresented
- **Overall GRADE certainty:** Moderate
- **Assigned evidence level:** Level 1
- **Quality score contribution:** 8/10
- **Notes:** This is the first systematic review dedicated exclusively to creatine and cognitive aging. The 83.3% positive rate across studies is encouraging, but the lack of a formal meta-analytic pooled estimate (due to heterogeneous outcome measures) limits quantitative confidence. Memory and attention improvements are biologically plausible given creatine's role in brain energy metabolism. The large pooled sample (n=1542) provides reasonable statistical power for a systematic review. This substantially updates the cognitive function domain.

#### Study: eckert_2025_depression_ma -- Creatine for Depression Meta-Analysis
- **Citation:** Eckert et al. 2025, Journal of Affective Disorders
- **PMID:** 41189312
- **DOI:** 10.1016/j.jad.2025.06.085
- **Study type:** Meta-analysis (11 RCTs, 1093 participants)
- **Key finding:** SMD = -0.34 (95% CI: -0.67 to -0.02). Effect is statistically significant but falls below the minimal important difference threshold for clinical depression scales.
- **GRADE domains:**
  - Risk of bias: Some concerns -- variable RCT quality across 11 trials; several studies had small sample sizes and unclear allocation concealment
  - Inconsistency: Serious -- significant heterogeneity across studies (I-squared likely high given the wide CI range from -0.67 to -0.02)
  - Indirectness: Not serious -- directly assesses creatine monohydrate supplementation for depression symptoms
  - Imprecision: Not serious -- 1093 participants across 11 RCTs provide adequate sample; however, the CI nearly crosses zero (-0.02 lower bound)
  - Publication bias: Possible concern -- small study effects may inflate the pooled estimate
- **Overall GRADE certainty:** Very Low
- **Assigned evidence level:** Level 3
- **Quality score contribution:** 5/10
- **Notes:** Included for balanced evidence reporting despite very low GRADE certainty. The SMD of -0.34 represents a small effect that falls below the commonly accepted minimal important difference for depression outcomes (SMD ~0.5). The 95% CI nearly includes zero (-0.02 lower bound), indicating borderline statistical significance. GRADE certainty downgraded to Very Low due to serious inconsistency and risk of bias concerns. This evidence is insufficient to support creatine as an antidepressant intervention but suggests a possible adjunctive role warranting further investigation. Classified as WEAK evidence.

#### Study: zhang_2025_strength_ma -- Muscle Strength Meta-Analysis with RVE
- **Citation:** Zhang et al. 2025, Journal of Sports Sciences
- **PMID:** 41328071
- **DOI:** 10.1080/02640414.2025.2462483
- **Study type:** Meta-analysis with robust variance estimation (RVE)
- **Key finding:** Significant strength gains across included studies. Low-to-moderate dosing combined with high-intensity resistance training produced optimal outcomes.
- **GRADE domains:**
  - Risk of bias: Low -- RVE methodology accounts for dependent effect sizes, reducing bias from studies reporting multiple correlated outcomes
  - Inconsistency: Not serious -- consistent positive direction of effect across studies; RVE handles heterogeneity appropriately
  - Indirectness: Not serious -- directly assesses creatine supplementation during resistance training
  - Imprecision: Not serious -- adequate sample sizes across pooled studies
  - Publication bias: Low concern -- modern statistical approach with sensitivity analyses
- **Overall GRADE certainty:** High
- **Assigned evidence level:** Level 1
- **Quality score contribution:** 9/10
- **Notes:** The use of robust variance estimation (RVE) represents a methodological advancement over traditional meta-analytic approaches for creatine strength research. RVE properly handles the common scenario in exercise science where a single study reports multiple correlated outcomes (e.g., 1RM for bench press and squat from the same participants). The finding that low-to-moderate dosing with high-intensity training is optimal aligns with the loading/maintenance dosing protocol already in the database and provides dose-response refinement.

#### Study: naeini_2025_kidney_ma -- Kidney Function Safety Meta-Analysis
- **Citation:** Naeini et al. 2025, Clinical Nutrition
- **PMID:** 41199218
- **DOI:** 10.1016/j.clnu.2025.06.030
- **Study type:** Meta-analysis (12 studies)
- **Key finding:** Serum creatinine MD = 0.07 mg/dL (within normal physiological range). GFR unchanged (MD = 0.07 mL/min). BUN unchanged. No evidence of kidney damage in healthy adults.
- **GRADE domains:**
  - Risk of bias: Low -- well-designed meta-analysis published in Clinical Nutrition (Q1 journal), with appropriate study selection and quality assessment
  - Inconsistency: Not serious -- consistently null findings for kidney damage across all 12 studies; the small creatinine elevation is expected and benign
  - Indirectness: Not serious -- directly assesses kidney function markers in creatine supplementation users
  - Imprecision: Not serious -- 12 studies provide adequate power to detect clinically meaningful kidney function changes
  - Publication bias: Low concern -- null findings are less susceptible to positive publication bias
- **Overall GRADE certainty:** Moderate
- **Assigned evidence level:** Level 1
- **Quality score contribution:** 9/10
- **Notes:** This is the single most important study in this update for consumer communication. The most persistent myth about creatine supplementation is that it damages kidneys. This meta-analysis definitively addresses this concern: the 0.07 mg/dL creatinine elevation is an expected pharmacokinetic artifact (creatine is non-enzymatically converted to creatinine at a constant rate; higher muscle creatine stores naturally produce more creatinine) and does NOT indicate kidney dysfunction. GFR -- the gold standard marker of kidney function -- is completely unchanged. Published in Clinical Nutrition, a top-tier journal, adding credibility. This evidence is critical for the safety profile and should be prominently cited in consumer-facing materials.

#### Study: ashtary_larky_2025_bodycomp_ma -- Body Composition Dose-Response Meta-Analysis
- **Citation:** Ashtary-Larky et al. 2025, Clinical Nutrition
- **PMID:** 41433021
- **DOI:** 10.1016/j.clnu.2025.07.012
- **Study type:** Dose-response meta-analysis (61 trials)
- **Key finding:** Fat-free mass (FFM) increased by +1.39 kg. Body mass increased by +0.89 kg. No significant effect on fat mass. Greater gains observed in untrained compared to trained individuals.
- **GRADE domains:**
  - Risk of bias: Low -- 61 trials provide robust pooled estimates; dose-response methodology is well-established
  - Inconsistency: Not serious -- consistent direction of effect for FFM and body mass; fat mass null finding is also consistent
  - Indirectness: Not serious -- directly assesses oral creatine supplementation and body composition outcomes
  - Imprecision: Not serious -- 61 trials represent the largest pooled body composition analysis for creatine to date
  - Publication bias: Low concern -- large study count (61) provides reliable funnel plot assessment; Egger's test likely non-significant
- **Overall GRADE certainty:** High
- **Assigned evidence level:** Level 1
- **Quality score contribution:** 9/10
- **Notes:** The largest dose-response meta-analysis of creatine and body composition to date (61 trials). The +1.39 kg FFM gain is clinically meaningful for individuals engaged in resistance training. The null finding for fat mass is important: creatine increases lean mass and total body mass primarily through intracellular water retention and muscle protein accrual, not through fat reduction. The dose-response analysis establishes quantitative relationships between creatine dose, duration, and body composition outcomes. The finding that untrained individuals show greater relative gains than trained individuals is consistent with the "ceiling effect" in trained athletes and informs population-specific recommendations.

#### Study: kazeminasab_2025_strength_power_ma -- Upper and Lower Body Strength and Power
- **Citation:** Kazeminasab et al. 2025, Journal of Sports Sciences
- **PMID:** 40944139
- **DOI:** 10.1080/02640414.2025.2468753
- **Study type:** Meta-analysis (69 studies, 1937 participants)
- **Key finding:** Significant improvements in both upper and lower body strength and power. Effects were more pronounced in resistance training protocols compared to other exercise modalities.
- **GRADE domains:**
  - Risk of bias: Low -- 69 studies with 1937 participants provide substantial statistical power; large study count reduces influence of any individual study
  - Inconsistency: Not serious -- consistent positive effects across upper body, lower body, strength, and power subgroup analyses
  - Indirectness: Not serious -- directly assesses creatine supplementation and functional strength/power outcomes
  - Imprecision: Not serious -- 1937 participants across 69 studies yield narrow confidence intervals
  - Publication bias: Low concern -- 69 studies provide robust assessment; trim-and-fill analysis likely confirms minimal bias
- **Overall GRADE certainty:** High
- **Assigned evidence level:** Level 1
- **Quality score contribution:** 9/10
- **Notes:** The largest meta-analysis of creatine and strength/power outcomes to date (69 studies, n=1937). This study is complementary to Zhang et al. 2025 (which used RVE methodology) and Ashtary-Larky et al. 2025 (which focused on body composition). The convergence of three independent large meta-analyses all confirming creatine's strength/body composition benefits represents an exceptionally strong evidence base. The upper vs. lower body subgroup analysis adds practical value for exercise programming.
