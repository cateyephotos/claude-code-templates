# Research Synthesis: Creatine
## Pipeline Run: 2026-03-04

### Synthesis Methodology
- **Approach:** Narrative synthesis incorporating new meta-analytic evidence into existing 18-citation database
- **Effect size metrics:** Standardized mean difference (SMD), mean difference (MD), fat-free mass (FFM) in kg
- **Confidence assessment:** GRADE certainty of evidence
- **Balance principle:** Both strong and weak findings included to maintain database integrity

### Key Evidence Updates

#### Update 1: Strength and Power -- Three Converging Meta-Analyses
- **Sources:** zhang_2025_strength_ma (PMID 41328071), kazeminasab_2025_strength_power_ma (PMID 40944139)
- **Previous state:** Strength benefits well-documented by individual RCTs and earlier meta-analyses, but older syntheses did not use modern statistical methods like robust variance estimation
- **New evidence:**
  - Zhang 2025: Significant strength gains confirmed using robust variance estimation (RVE). RVE properly accounts for dependent effect sizes from studies reporting multiple correlated outcomes. Low-to-moderate dosing with high-intensity training identified as optimal.
  - Kazeminasab 2025: 69 studies, n=1937. Significant improvements in both upper body and lower body strength and power. Effects more pronounced in resistance training protocols.
- **Evidence strength change:** Strong -> Very Strong
- **Synthesis notes:** The convergence of two independent large meta-analyses on the same conclusion -- that creatine significantly improves muscle strength and power -- represents one of the strongest evidence bases in all of sports nutrition. Zhang et al.'s use of robust variance estimation addresses a methodological limitation that has plagued exercise science meta-analyses for years (non-independent effect sizes from multi-outcome studies). Kazeminasab et al.'s 69-study pool is the largest meta-analysis specifically addressing creatine strength and power. Together, these studies eliminate any reasonable doubt about creatine's ergogenic efficacy for resistance training outcomes.
- **Convergent evidence:** Both meta-analyses, conducted independently by different research groups in different countries, arrive at the same conclusion. Combined with the 61-trial body composition meta-analysis (Ashtary-Larky 2025), three independent 2025 publications collectively synthesize 130+ studies confirming creatine's efficacy for musculoskeletal outcomes.
- **Database impact:** Strength and power evidence sections upgraded to Very Strong. Dosing recommendations refined based on dose-response data.

#### Update 2: Body Composition -- Dose-Response Meta-Analysis
- **Source:** ashtary_larky_2025_bodycomp_ma (PMID 41433021)
- **Previous state:** Body composition benefits documented but dose-response relationships not quantified at the meta-analytic level
- **New evidence:** 61 trials. Key findings:
  - Fat-free mass: +1.39 kg (statistically significant)
  - Body mass: +0.89 kg (statistically significant)
  - Fat mass: No significant effect (important negative finding within a positive study)
  - Greater FFM gains in untrained vs. trained individuals (ceiling effect)
  - Dose-response relationships established across creatine dosing ranges
- **Evidence strength change:** Moderate -> Strong
- **Synthesis notes:** This is the most comprehensive dose-response analysis of creatine and body composition ever conducted (61 trials). The +1.39 kg FFM gain is clinically meaningful for individuals engaged in resistance training and represents a realistic expectation for consumers. The null finding for fat mass is critically important for consumer communication: creatine does not reduce body fat, and claims to that effect are unsupported. The body mass increase (+0.89 kg) is lower than the FFM increase, suggesting that the modest overall weight gain is primarily lean tissue. The finding that untrained individuals show greater relative gains is consistent with the "newbie gains" phenomenon and suggests creatine may be particularly beneficial for individuals beginning a training program.
- **Database impact:** Body composition evidence section upgraded. Dose-response framework added. Consumer guidance updated to set realistic expectations (FFM gain, not fat loss).

#### Update 3: Cognitive Function -- Systematic Review in Aging
- **Source:** marshall_2026_cognition_sr (PMID 40971619)
- **Previous state:** Cognitive benefits supported by a small number of individual studies, primarily in sleep-deprived or stressed populations. No dedicated systematic review for aging.
- **New evidence:** 6 studies, n=1542. 83.3% of studies (5/6) reported positive effects on cognition in aging adults. Memory and attention were the most consistently improved domains.
- **Evidence strength change:** Low-Moderate -> Moderate
- **Synthesis notes:** Creatine's cognitive effects represent an emerging evidence domain that is distinct from its established musculoskeletal benefits. The biological mechanism is well-understood: creatine serves as an energy buffer in the brain via the phosphocreatine/creatine kinase system, maintaining ATP levels during periods of high metabolic demand. Aging is associated with declining brain creatine levels and reduced efficiency of this energy system, making older adults a biologically plausible target population. The 83.3% positive rate across 6 studies is encouraging, but several limitations remain: (1) heterogeneous cognitive test batteries make pooling difficult, (2) the one negative study creates uncertainty, and (3) the systematic review did not produce a pooled quantitative estimate. Memory and attention improvements are the most consistently reported, which aligns with the energy-intensive nature of these cognitive domains. The Consensus API sources (Kaplinska, Tomczyk, Fabiano & Candow) were excluded as narrative reviews but confirm growing scientific interest in creatine's cognitive applications.
- **Database impact:** Cognitive function evidence upgraded from Low-Moderate to Moderate. Aging-specific benefit noted. Brain energy metabolism mechanism documented.

#### Update 4: Depression/Mood -- Weak but Documented Evidence
- **Source:** eckert_2025_depression_ma (PMID 41189312)
- **Previous state:** Depression/mood not previously covered in the database
- **New evidence:** 11 RCTs, n=1093. SMD = -0.34 (95% CI: -0.67 to -0.02). Statistically significant but below the minimal important difference for clinical depression scales. GRADE certainty: Very Low.
- **Evidence strength:** Weak
- **Synthesis notes:** This meta-analysis establishes creatine as having a statistically significant but clinically sub-threshold effect on depression symptoms. The SMD of -0.34 is classified as a small effect and falls below the commonly accepted minimal important difference of SMD ~0.5 for depression outcomes. The 95% CI nearly includes zero (lower bound -0.02), further weakening confidence. The mechanism by which creatine might affect mood is plausible (brain energy metabolism, potential effects on serotonergic and dopaminergic pathways), but the clinical evidence does not support a standalone benefit claim. This finding is included for completeness and balanced evidence reporting, with a clear WEAK evidence classification. The database should NOT claim creatine as effective for depression based on this evidence.
- **Convergent evidence:** The depression evidence stands alone and does not converge with other findings. It does not undermine the established benefits in strength, body composition, or cognition, but it also does not support expansion into the mental health benefit domain.
- **Database impact:** Depression/mood section added with WEAK evidence classification. No benefit claim made. Evidence documented for transparency and future tracking as the literature matures.

#### Update 5: Kidney Safety -- Definitive Safety Meta-Analysis
- **Source:** naeini_2025_kidney_ma (PMID 41199218)
- **Previous state:** Kidney safety generally noted as safe based on individual studies and position stands, but no dedicated meta-analysis specifically addressing kidney function markers
- **New evidence:** 12 studies. Key findings:
  - Serum creatinine: MD = 0.07 mg/dL (within normal physiological range, expected pharmacokinetic artifact)
  - Glomerular filtration rate (GFR): MD = 0.07 mL/min (unchanged -- the gold standard for kidney function)
  - Blood urea nitrogen (BUN): No change
  - No evidence of kidney damage in healthy adults
- **Evidence strength change:** Moderate -> Strong
- **Synthesis notes:** This meta-analysis is arguably the most important study in this entire update for consumer communication and safety profiling. The persistent myth that creatine damages kidneys is the single most common barrier to consumer adoption. This meta-analysis directly and definitively refutes that claim with pooled data from 12 studies showing:

  1. **Creatinine elevation is expected and benign.** Creatine is non-enzymatically converted to creatinine at a constant rate (~1.7% per day). When muscle creatine stores are elevated through supplementation, more creatinine is produced. This is a pharmacokinetic artifact, not a sign of kidney damage. The 0.07 mg/dL elevation is well within the normal reference range (0.7-1.3 mg/dL for men, 0.6-1.1 mg/dL for women).

  2. **GFR is unchanged.** GFR is the gold standard functional assessment of kidney health. The fact that GFR is completely unchanged (MD = 0.07 mL/min, effectively zero) while creatinine is slightly elevated confirms that the creatinine increase is a measurement artifact, not kidney dysfunction.

  3. **BUN is unchanged.** BUN provides an independent confirmation of normal kidney function.

  The clinical implication is clear: healthcare providers who see elevated creatinine in creatine users should not reflexively diagnose kidney disease. The elevation is expected and benign. This finding should be prominently cited in safety sections and consumer guidance.
- **Database impact:** Safety section substantially upgraded. Kidney function subsection created with dedicated meta-analytic evidence. Consumer guidance updated to address the creatinine artifact explanation.

### Cross-Domain Integration
The 6 new studies collectively demonstrate that creatine monohydrate has an evidence profile spanning multiple physiological systems:

- **Musculoskeletal performance** (strength, power, body composition) is the core evidence domain with the strongest support. Three independent 2025 meta-analyses converge on consistent benefits, collectively synthesizing 130+ studies. This convergence across independent research groups, countries, and statistical methodologies provides one of the strongest evidence bases in dietary supplement science.

- **Cognitive function** in aging is an emerging domain with moderate evidence. The biological mechanism (brain phosphocreatine energy buffering) is well-established, and 83.3% of studies show positive effects on memory and attention. This domain is expected to strengthen as more dedicated RCTs are conducted.

- **Depression/mood** has weak evidence that is below clinical significance thresholds. The SMD of -0.34 is statistically significant but clinically insufficient. This domain requires substantially more research before any benefit claim can be made.

- **Kidney safety** is now definitively established. The most common consumer concern about creatine has been addressed by a dedicated meta-analysis showing no kidney damage. The creatinine elevation is an expected pharmacokinetic artifact, not pathology.

### Limitations of This Synthesis
1. Marshall 2026 cognition systematic review did not produce a pooled quantitative estimate due to heterogeneous outcome measures -- limits quantitative confidence in cognitive effect size
2. Eckert 2025 depression meta-analysis has Very Low GRADE certainty -- the SMD of -0.34 should not be used to support a benefit claim
3. The body composition dose-response (Ashtary-Larky 2025) primarily represents creatine monohydrate -- other creatine forms (HCl, ethyl ester, buffered) may not produce equivalent results
4. Female-specific outcomes remain underrepresented across all 6 new studies -- the meta-analyses are predominantly male-participant-derived
5. Long-term (>2 year) supplementation data is sparse across all domains
6. The kidney safety meta-analysis addresses healthy adults only -- individuals with pre-existing kidney disease were not included and should consult healthcare providers before supplementation
