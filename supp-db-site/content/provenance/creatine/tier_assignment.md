**Decision:** Tier 1 is clearly warranted. The previous Tier 2 assignment was conservative for a supplement with this evidence base. Creatine monohydrate has one of the largest bodies of evidence of any dietary supplement, endorsed by the International Society of Sports Nutrition (ISSN) position stand as both safe and effective. This update adds 6 new papers including three large meta-analyses (69 studies, 61 trials, and RVE analysis) that converge on consistent benefits for strength and body composition, plus a critical safety meta-analysis definitively refuting kidney damage concerns. The upgrade from Tier 2 to Tier 1 reflects the cumulative weight of evidence rather than any single new finding.

### Tier Upgrade Justification (2 -> 1)
The previous Tier 2 assignment was based on a more conservative citation count (18 citations) and quality score (86). The upgrade is driven by:

1. **Volume of new evidence:** 6 new high-quality papers (5 meta-analyses + 1 systematic review) added in a single update cycle
2. **Scale of meta-analyses:** The three strength/body composition meta-analyses collectively synthesize 69+61+unknown studies, representing one of the most meta-analyzed outcomes in sports nutrition
3. **Critical safety evidence:** The Naeini 2025 kidney function meta-analysis (12 studies) definitively addresses the most persistent safety concern, showing no kidney damage
4. **Multi-domain convergence:** Evidence now spans strength, power, body composition, cognitive function, and safety -- all with dedicated meta-analyses
5. **Institutional endorsement:** ISSN position stand, multiple international sports science societies endorse creatine as safe and effective
6. **Quality score increase:** 86 -> 93 (+7 points) reflecting the substantial evidence expansion

### Quality Score Change Narrative
The increase from 86 to 93 (+7 points) is driven by four factors:

1. **RCT volume (+4):** The addition of three large meta-analyses that collectively synthesize over 100 individual RCTs substantially increases the evidence volume score. The 69-study strength/power meta-analysis (Kazeminasab 2025) and 61-trial body composition meta-analysis (Ashtary-Larky 2025) are among the largest in the dietary supplement literature.

2. **Meta-analysis completeness (+2):** Every major creatine benefit claim now has dedicated meta-analytic support. The safety domain gained its first comprehensive kidney function meta-analysis (Naeini 2025), and cognition gained its first dedicated systematic review in aging (Marshall 2026). The meta-analysis coverage is now essentially complete.

3. **Sample sizes (+2):** The new studies collectively add thousands of participants to the pooled evidence base. The cognition systematic review alone includes 1542 participants, the depression meta-analysis 1093, and the strength/power meta-analysis 1937.

4. **Recency adjustment (-2):** Some older citations in the existing 18-citation database are aging (pre-2023), which slightly reduces the recency component. The 6 new 2025-2026 papers offset this but do not fully compensate when considering the entire citation set.

### Research Maturity: Mature (upgraded from Moderate)
**Justification:** Creatine monohydrate is one of the most extensively studied dietary supplements in the world, with a publication history spanning over 30 years. The ISSN has issued a formal position stand endorsing its safety and efficacy. The 2025-2026 evidence cycle added 5 meta-analyses and 1 systematic review covering strength, body composition, cognition, depression, and kidney safety -- demonstrating that the research base is not only large but actively synthesized with modern statistical methods. The upgrade to "Mature" research maturity reflects the exceptional depth and breadth of evidence across multiple outcome domains, the availability of dose-response data, and the resolution of longstanding safety questions.

### Evidence Gaps Identified
1. Long-term (>5 year) cognitive function outcomes in aging populations remain limited -- existing studies are primarily short-to-medium term
2. Depression evidence (SMD = -0.34) is below clinical significance -- larger, more rigorous RCTs are needed before any mood benefit claim can be made
3. Female-specific dosing and outcomes remain underrepresented in the meta-analytic literature (most studies predominantly male participants)
4. Creatine and neurological disease prevention (Parkinson's, ALS) evidence is preliminary and did not yield qualifying papers in this update
5. Optimal timing (pre-workout vs. post-workout) meta-analysis is needed -- individual studies show inconsistent results
6. Interactions between creatine and other supplements (e.g., beta-alanine, caffeine) lack dedicated meta-analyses
