### Per-Study Evaluation

#### Study: clegg_2006_gait — GAIT Trial (NIH-funded RCT)
- **PMID/DOI:** 16495392 / 10.1056/NEJMoa052771
- **Study type:** Multi-center RCT, n=1583, 24 weeks
- **GRADE domains:**
  - Risk of bias: **Low** — NIH-funded, pre-registered, blinded; positive control (celecoxib) included; allocation concealment adequate
  - Inconsistency: **Not serious** — single trial, internal consistency good
  - Indirectness: **Not serious** — knee OA patients, representative population
  - Imprecision: **Not serious** — large N (1583); adequate power
  - Publication bias: **Undetected** — government funding, pre-registered
- **Overall GRADE certainty:** **High** (single trial)
- **Assigned evidence level:** Level 2
- **Key limitation:** Primary outcome (WOMAC pain) null for overall sample; positive in severe-pain subgroup (n=354) only. Celecoxib (positive control) was effective. Glucosamine HCl used (not sulfate) — disputed form issue.
- **Quality score contribution:** 8/10

### Summary Judgment

The glucosamine evidence base is **methodologically mature but internally contested**. The highest-quality independent evidence (Runhaar 2017 IPD MA, Knapik 2018 funding stratification, GAIT trial) consistently shows null to minimal symptomatic effects. The positive signals come predominantly from industry-funded meta-analyses. The structural benefit (joint space narrowing) is more consistent across funding sources and is the most defensible claim. OARSI's "uncertain appropriateness" classification accurately reflects this evidence state.

**Evidence Level Designation: Tier 2** is appropriate for the research volume, not for consistent efficacy. The volume of research has not resolved the fundamental contested efficacy question — it has merely documented it more rigorously.
