# Evidence Evaluation: L-Theanine (gamma-Glutamylethylamide)
## Pipeline Run: 2026-03-05

### Evaluation Framework
- **Primary:** GRADE (Grading of Recommendations Assessment, Development and Evaluation)
- **Quality scoring:** Custom composite (see tier_assignment.md)

### Per-Study Evaluation

#### Study: bulman_2025_sleep -- Impact of L-Theanine on Sleep Quality: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis
- **Citation:** Bulman CL et al. 2025, Sleep Medicine Reviews 80:102070
- **PMID:** 40056718
- **DOI:** 10.1016/j.smrv.2024.102070
- **Study type:** Systematic review and meta-analysis (19 articles, N=897)
- **Key finding:** Sleep quality SMD=0.43 (significant improvement), daytime dysfunction SMD=0.33 (significant improvement), sleep onset latency SMD=0.15 (small but positive effect). Overall sleep benefit confirmed across multiple validated instruments.
- **GRADE domains:**
  - Risk of bias: Low -- published in Sleep Medicine Reviews (IF>9), the leading sleep-specific review journal. Rigorous PRISMA methodology. 19 articles with N=897 provides substantial pooled sample. Validated sleep instruments (PSQI, actigraphy, polysomnography) used across included studies
  - Inconsistency: Not serious -- consistent direction of effect across all three sleep sub-domains (quality, onset latency, daytime dysfunction). Effect sizes vary in magnitude (0.15 to 0.43) but this is expected given the different sub-domain measures
  - Indirectness: Not serious -- directly assesses oral L-theanine supplementation for sleep outcomes in adults. Sleep is a primary benefit domain for L-theanine
  - Imprecision: Not serious -- 897 participants across 19 articles provides adequate statistical power for pooled estimates. Confidence intervals reported for all SMDs
  - Publication bias: Low concern -- 19 articles provides reasonable funnel plot assessment. The inclusion of both large (SMD=0.43) and small (SMD=0.15) effects across sub-domains suggests balanced reporting rather than selective positive publication
- **Overall GRADE certainty:** High
- **Assigned evidence level:** Level 1
- **Quality score contribution:** 9/10
- **Notes:** This is the definitive sleep meta-analysis for L-theanine. Sleep Medicine Reviews is the top-ranked journal in sleep research, and publication there confers substantial credibility. The effect sizes are modest but clinically meaningful: SMD=0.43 for sleep quality represents a small-to-medium effect that is comparable to some behavioral sleep interventions and exceeds placebo response rates. The sleep onset latency finding (SMD=0.15) is smaller, suggesting L-theanine is more beneficial for overall sleep quality and daytime function than for falling asleep faster. The N=897 pooled sample is the largest dedicated sleep analysis for L-theanine. This study substantially strengthens the sleep domain, which was previously based on smaller individual studies.

#### Study: payne_2025_comprehensive -- Tea, L-Theanine, and Caffeine: Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis on Cognition, Sleep, and Mood
- **Citation:** Payne JK et al. 2025, Nutrition Reviews, nuaf002
- **PMID:** 40314930
- **DOI:** 10.1093/nutrit/nuaf002
- **Study type:** Systematic review and meta-analysis (50 RCTs)
- **Key finding:** L-theanine+caffeine combination: choice reaction time at hour 1 SMD=-0.48 (medium effect, faster), attention switching at hour 2 SMD=0.33 (small-to-medium effect, improved). L-theanine alone: choice reaction time at hour 1 SMD=-0.35 (small-to-medium effect, faster), mood at hour 2 SMD=0.26 (small effect, improved). Timepoint-stratified analysis reveals acute temporal effects.
- **GRADE domains:**
  - Risk of bias: Low -- published in Nutrition Reviews (IF>7), a highly respected review journal. 50 RCTs is an exceptionally large evidence base for a single-ingredient supplement meta-analysis. The methodological innovation of separating L-theanine-alone from L-theanine+caffeine effects and stratifying by timepoint (h1, h2) represents best practice in the field
  - Inconsistency: Some concerns -- effect sizes for L-theanine alone are smaller than for L-theanine+caffeine combination, indicating that some previously attributed L-theanine benefits may actually require caffeine co-administration. The theanine-alone mood effect (SMD=0.26) is small and close to the threshold of clinical significance
  - Indirectness: Not serious -- directly assesses oral L-theanine supplementation. The separation of theanine-alone from theanine+caffeine effects makes this the most directly relevant meta-analysis for evaluating L-theanine as a standalone supplement
  - Imprecision: Not serious -- 50 RCTs provide exceptional statistical power. The large study count enables reliable subgroup analyses (theanine-alone vs. combination) and timepoint stratification
  - Publication bias: Low concern -- with 50 RCTs, funnel plot assessment is highly reliable. The presence of both significant (choice RT) and non-significant findings within the same meta-analysis suggests balanced reporting
- **Overall GRADE certainty:** High
- **Assigned evidence level:** Level 1
- **Quality score contribution:** 9/10
- **Notes:** This is the largest meta-analysis of L-theanine effects published to date (50 RCTs). Its primary contribution is methodological: by separating L-theanine-alone effects from L-theanine+caffeine combination effects, it provides the cleanest estimate of what L-theanine does independently. The finding that L-theanine alone has a smaller but still significant effect on choice reaction time (SMD=-0.35 vs. -0.48 with caffeine) is important for honest evidence communication. The timepoint stratification (h1 vs. h2 post-dose) reveals that L-theanine's effects are temporally dynamic, with some outcomes peaking at different times. The mood effect (SMD=0.26 at h2) suggests a subtle but real anxiolytic/calming property of standalone L-theanine. This study sets the benchmark for future L-theanine meta-analyses.

#### Study: matyus_2025_cognitive -- Effect of L-Theanine Supplementation on Cognitive Performance: Meta-Analysis of RCTs
- **Citation:** Matyus I et al. 2025, Journal of Clinical Medicine 14(13):4769
- **PMID:** 41227106
- **DOI:** 10.3390/jcm14134769
- **Study type:** Meta-analysis (5 RCTs, 148 adults)
- **Key finding:** Recognition visual reaction time MD=-15.20ms (statistically significant improvement). Simple reaction time: non-significant. Stroop test: non-significant. Dose-dependent subgroup analysis shows larger effects at higher doses.
- **GRADE domains:**
  - Risk of bias: Some concerns -- Journal of Clinical Medicine is a reputable MDPI journal but not top-tier. Only 5 RCTs with 148 total participants is a small evidence base for meta-analysis. Individual study quality may vary within this small pool
  - Inconsistency: Serious concern -- mixed results across cognitive domains. Recognition visual RT is significant but simple RT and Stroop are not. This inconsistency limits the generalizability of the cognitive benefit claim. However, the domain-specificity may also be interpreted as a genuine finding (L-theanine improves some cognitive domains but not others)
  - Indirectness: Not serious -- directly assesses oral L-theanine supplementation for cognitive performance in adults
  - Imprecision: Serious concern -- 148 participants across 5 RCTs is underpowered for reliable pooled estimates. Confidence intervals are likely wide, and the non-significant findings for simple RT and Stroop may reflect insufficient statistical power rather than true null effects
  - Publication bias: Possible concern -- with only 5 studies, funnel plot assessment is unreliable. The mixed positive/null results may partially mitigate positive publication bias
- **Overall GRADE certainty:** Moderate
- **Assigned evidence level:** Level 2
- **Quality score contribution:** 6/10
- **Notes:** Despite limited statistical power, this study provides a valuable nuanced perspective on L-theanine's cognitive effects. The finding that recognition visual reaction time improves (MD=-15.20ms) while simple reaction time and Stroop performance do not is informative for understanding the specificity of L-theanine's cognitive mechanism. L-theanine may enhance attentional processing of complex stimuli (recognition tasks) without affecting basic motor speed (simple RT) or executive function (Stroop). The dose-dependent subgroup analysis, showing larger effects at higher doses, adds pharmacological plausibility despite the small sample. This study's primary value lies in its specificity of findings rather than its statistical power. It should be cited as supporting evidence for domain-specific cognitive enhancement but should not be used as a standalone basis for broad cognitive benefit claims.

#### Study: moshfeghinia_2024_mental_disorders -- Effects of L-Theanine Supplementation on Outcomes of Patients with Mental Disorders: Systematic Review
- **Citation:** Moshfeghinia R et al. 2024, BMC Psychiatry 24(1):886
- **PMID:** 39633316
- **DOI:** 10.1186/s12888-024-06339-z
- **Study type:** Systematic review (11 RCTs, 6 countries)
- **Key finding:** Positive effects reported for schizophrenia (adjunctive therapy -- reduces positive symptoms and general psychopathology), anxiety disorders (significant anxiolytic effects), and ADHD (improvements in attention and hyperactivity). Cochrane Risk of Bias (RoB 2) assessment performed for all included studies.
- **GRADE domains:**
  - Risk of bias: Some concerns -- BMC Psychiatry is a reputable open-access psychiatry journal. 11 RCTs across 6 countries provides reasonable geographic diversity. Cochrane RoB 2 assessment performed, which is the gold standard for bias evaluation. However, the quality of individual RCTs varies: some schizophrenia trials have small samples and short durations
  - Inconsistency: Not serious -- consistent positive direction across three different psychiatric conditions (schizophrenia, anxiety, ADHD). The consistency across heterogeneous clinical populations suggests a genuine transdiagnostic effect
  - Indirectness: Some concerns -- the primary intervention in many included studies is L-theanine as an adjunct to existing psychiatric medications (antipsychotics for schizophrenia, stimulants for ADHD). The effects of L-theanine cannot be fully isolated from the pharmacological context. Additionally, the psychiatric population may not generalize to healthy supplement users
  - Imprecision: Moderate concern -- this is a qualitative systematic review without quantitative meta-analytic pooling. Effect sizes are reported per study but not pooled, making it difficult to assess the overall magnitude of effect. Individual study sample sizes vary
  - Publication bias: Possible concern -- systematic reviews without meta-analysis cannot formally assess publication bias through funnel plots. The positive framing of results across all three conditions may reflect selective reporting
- **Overall GRADE certainty:** Moderate
- **Assigned evidence level:** Level 2
- **Quality score contribution:** 6/10
- **Notes:** This study opens a new evidence domain for L-theanine: psychiatric clinical populations. The most intriguing finding is for schizophrenia, where L-theanine as an adjunct to antipsychotics showed reductions in positive symptoms and general psychopathology. While this is unlikely to become a primary consumer benefit claim for L-theanine supplements, it provides mechanistic insight: if L-theanine can modulate dopaminergic and glutamatergic neurotransmission sufficiently to improve schizophrenia symptoms, its effects on anxiety and attention in healthy populations are biologically plausible. The ADHD findings add another clinical domain, though the evidence is preliminary. The main limitation is the absence of quantitative pooling -- without meta-analytic synthesis, the magnitude of L-theanine's effects in psychiatric populations cannot be precisely quantified. Retained for domain expansion and mechanistic insight.

### Aggregate Quality Metrics
- **Average study quality:** 7.5/10
- **Evidence level distribution:** Level 1: 2 papers (Bulman 2025, Payne 2025), Level 2: 2 papers (Matyus 2025, Moshfeghinia 2024)
- **Replication assessment:** Moderate -- cognitive effects are confirmed by both Payne 2025 (50 RCTs) and Matyus 2025 (5 RCTs), though with different specific outcomes. Sleep effects confirmed by Bulman 2025 as a standalone domain. Psychiatric effects are a new domain with a single systematic review
- **Geographic diversity:** Multi-country representation across included studies. Moshfeghinia 2024 explicitly reports 6 countries across its 11 RCTs. Payne 2025 spans the global L-theanine literature (50 RCTs from multiple countries)
- **Positive/negative balance:** Results are generally positive but with important nuances. L-theanine-alone effects are smaller than L-theanine+caffeine effects (Payne 2025). Some cognitive domains show non-significant effects (Matyus 2025: simple RT, Stroop). This mixed profile is actually a strength of the evidence base -- it suggests genuine effect specificity rather than uniformly positive bias
- **Effect size calibration note:** L-theanine effect sizes for standalone use are generally in the small-to-moderate range (SMD 0.15-0.43 for sleep, SMD 0.26-0.35 for cognition/mood alone). The larger effects (SMD=-0.48) are seen in the L-theanine+caffeine combination. These modest standalone effect sizes are important context for tier assignment and consumer communication
