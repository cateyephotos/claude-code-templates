### Key Evidence Updates

#### Update 1: Sleep Domain Substantially Strengthened
- **Source:** bulman_2025_sleep (PMID 40056718)
- **Previous state:** Sleep improvement noted in individual RCTs and smaller reviews. Sleep was a recognized but not robustly quantified benefit domain for L-theanine.
- **New evidence:**
  - Bulman 2025: Systematic review and meta-analysis of 19 articles with N=897. Three sleep sub-domains quantified:
    - Sleep quality: SMD=0.43 (significant -- small-to-medium effect)
    - Daytime dysfunction: SMD=0.33 (significant -- small effect)
    - Sleep onset latency: SMD=0.15 (small, positive but modest)
  - Published in Sleep Medicine Reviews (IF>9, top sleep journal)
- **Evidence strength change:** Moderate -> Strong
- **Synthesis notes:** Sleep is now L-theanine's strongest standalone evidence domain. The SMD=0.43 for sleep quality, while modest in absolute terms, represents a clinically meaningful improvement confirmed across 19 studies with nearly 900 participants. The hierarchical pattern of effects (quality > daytime dysfunction > onset latency) suggests that L-theanine primarily improves subjective sleep quality and next-day function rather than reducing the time to fall asleep. This aligns with L-theanine's proposed mechanism of promoting relaxation (alpha brain wave activity) without sedation -- it facilitates better sleep quality through anxiolysis rather than through direct sedative action. The publication in Sleep Medicine Reviews, the top sleep-specific review journal, confers substantial credibility to this domain.
- **Database impact:** Sleep evidence section upgraded to Strong. Quantitative SMDs incorporated. Sleep quality is now the primary standalone benefit claim for L-theanine.

#### Update 2: Comprehensive Cognitive/Mood MA Confirms Modest Standalone Effects
- **Source:** payne_2025_comprehensive (PMID 40314930)
- **Previous state:** Cognitive and mood benefits attributed to L-theanine based on mixed literature that often did not separate standalone effects from L-theanine+caffeine combination effects.
- **New evidence:**
  - Payne 2025: The largest L-theanine meta-analysis to date (50 RCTs). Key innovation: separates standalone from combination effects and stratifies by timepoint (hour 1, hour 2 post-dose).
  - L-theanine + caffeine (combination): Choice RT h1 SMD=-0.48 (medium), attention switching h2 SMD=0.33 (small-to-medium)
  - L-theanine alone: Choice RT h1 SMD=-0.35 (small-to-medium), mood h2 SMD=0.26 (small)
  - Published in Nutrition Reviews (IF>7)
- **Evidence strength change:** Cognition (standalone): Moderate maintained; Cognition (combination): Moderate -> Strong; Mood: Moderate maintained
- **Synthesis notes:** This meta-analysis fundamentally recalibrates the L-theanine evidence narrative. By separating standalone from combination effects across 50 RCTs, Payne 2025 reveals that: (1) L-theanine+caffeine synergy is the strongest cognitive intervention, confirming what many practitioners have long suspected; (2) standalone L-theanine has real but modest cognitive effects -- primarily faster choice reaction time (SMD=-0.35), suggesting improved attentional processing; (3) standalone mood/anxiolytic effects are small (SMD=0.26) and manifest primarily at the second hour post-dose. The temporal dynamics (h1 vs. h2 effects) are a novel finding: L-theanine's mood effects peak later than its cognitive effects, possibly reflecting the time course of alpha brain wave modulation. The honest communication of these modest standalone effect sizes is essential for evidence-based supplement recommendations. L-theanine should be positioned as a mild anxiolytic and cognitive modulator, not a potent nootropic -- unless combined with caffeine, where the synergy produces medium-sized effects.
- **Database impact:** Cognitive and mood evidence sections updated with separated standalone vs. combination effect sizes. L-theanine+caffeine synergy confirmed as the strongest intervention. Consumer-facing messaging should distinguish standalone from combination benefits.

#### Update 3: Pure L-Theanine Cognitive Effects Remain Domain-Specific and Modest
- **Source:** matyus_2025_cognitive (PMID 41227106)
- **Previous state:** Cognitive enhancement was described broadly without specifying which cognitive domains respond to L-theanine and which do not.
- **New evidence:**
  - Matyus 2025: Meta-analysis of 5 RCTs with 148 adults. Domain-specific cognitive assessment:
    - Recognition visual reaction time: MD=-15.20ms (significant improvement)
    - Simple reaction time: Non-significant
    - Stroop test: Non-significant
  - Dose-dependent subgroup analysis shows larger effects at higher doses
- **Evidence strength change:** Maintained at Moderate (cognitive domain overall)
- **Synthesis notes:** Matyus 2025 provides an important reality check on L-theanine's cognitive profile. The finding that L-theanine improves recognition visual RT (a complex attentional task) but not simple RT (a basic motor task) or Stroop performance (an executive function task) reveals meaningful domain specificity. L-theanine appears to enhance attentional processing of complex stimuli rather than broadly improving all cognitive functions. The 15.20ms improvement in recognition visual RT is small in absolute terms but potentially meaningful for tasks requiring sustained visual attention (e.g., driving, screen-based work). The non-significant Stroop result is notable -- L-theanine does not appear to enhance executive function/inhibitory control, which is a common claim in marketing materials. The dose-dependent finding adds pharmacological plausibility. However, the small sample (148 adults across 5 RCTs) limits the strength of these conclusions, and the non-significant findings may partly reflect insufficient statistical power. Future meta-analyses with larger samples may resolve the ambiguity.
- **Database impact:** Cognitive benefit claims refined. Domain-specific effects documented. Marketing claims about broad cognitive enhancement should be moderated to reflect the evidence.

#### Update 4: New Psychiatric Domain Emerging
- **Source:** moshfeghinia_2024_mental_disorders (PMID 39633316)
- **Previous state:** L-theanine's effects in psychiatric populations were documented in individual case studies and small trials but not systematically reviewed.
- **New evidence:**
  - Moshfeghinia 2024: Systematic review of 11 RCTs across 6 countries.
  - Schizophrenia: Positive findings as adjunctive therapy -- reduces positive symptoms and general psychopathology
  - Anxiety disorders: Significant anxiolytic effects confirmed in clinical anxiety populations
  - ADHD: Improvements in attention and hyperactivity measures
  - Cochrane Risk of Bias (RoB 2) assessment performed for all included studies
- **Evidence strength change:** New domain -- rated Low-Moderate
- **Synthesis notes:** The psychiatric domain represents a meaningful expansion of L-theanine's evidence base. The most surprising finding is the positive effect in schizophrenia -- L-theanine's glutamate receptor modulation (it is structurally similar to glutamate) may explain its ability to reduce positive symptoms when used adjunctively with antipsychotics. This is consistent with the glutamate hypothesis of schizophrenia and provides mechanistic insight that goes beyond the traditional "calming amino acid" narrative. The anxiety disorder findings align with the mood/anxiolytic evidence from Payne 2025 but extend it to clinical populations with diagnosed anxiety. The ADHD findings are preliminary but intriguing -- L-theanine's alpha wave promotion may counteract the attentional deficits in ADHD without the stimulant side effects. However, all of these findings are qualitative (no meta-analytic pooling) and the evidence base is limited (11 RCTs total across three different conditions). This domain should be monitored for quantitative meta-analyses in future updates.
- **Database impact:** New psychiatric domain section created. Positioned as emerging/preliminary evidence. Appropriate caveats about adjunctive (not standalone) psychiatric use maintained.

### Cross-Domain Integration
The 4 included studies collectively demonstrate that L-theanine has an evidence profile spanning multiple physiological domains, with important distinctions between standalone and combination effects:

- **Sleep quality** is now L-theanine's strongest standalone evidence domain (Bulman 2025: SMD=0.43, N=897, published in the top sleep journal). The mechanism is likely anxiolysis-mediated: L-theanine promotes alpha brain wave activity, reducing pre-sleep anxiety and hyperarousal without direct sedation. This positions L-theanine as a gentle, non-sedating sleep aid -- different from melatonin (which is a direct chronobiotic) or valerian (which has GABAergic sedative properties).

- **Cognitive enhancement** is confirmed but nuanced. The key insight from Payne 2025 is that L-theanine+caffeine is the optimal cognitive intervention, with medium effect sizes. Standalone L-theanine provides smaller cognitive benefits primarily in attentional processing (choice RT, recognition visual RT). Executive function (Stroop) does not appear to improve. This domain specificity should inform evidence-based recommendations.

- **Mood and anxiolytic effects** are real but small for standalone L-theanine (SMD=0.26). The psychiatric SR (Moshfeghinia 2024) suggests stronger effects in clinical populations, but without quantitative pooling. L-theanine should be positioned as a mild anxiolytic supplement, not as a treatment for clinical anxiety.

- **L-theanine+caffeine synergy** is consistently the strongest cognitive intervention in the L-theanine literature. This synergy is now robustly confirmed by the largest meta-analysis to date (50 RCTs, Payne 2025). The practical implication is that L-theanine may be most effective as a caffeine companion -- moderating caffeine's jitteriness while enhancing its cognitive benefits. Many consumers already use this combination informally through green tea consumption.

### Mechanistic Framework
The new evidence supports the following mechanistic model for L-theanine:

```
L-Theanine (gamma-Glutamylethylamide)
    |
    +-> Alpha Brain Wave Promotion (EEG evidence from multiple RCTs)
    |       |
    |       +-> Relaxation Without Sedation
    |       +-> Sleep Quality Improvement (SMD=0.43, Bulman 2025)
    |       +-> Mild Anxiolysis (SMD=0.26, Payne 2025)
    |
    +-> Glutamate Receptor Modulation (structural analog of glutamate)
    |       |
    |       +-> Attentional Processing Enhancement (choice RT SMD=-0.35)
    |       +-> Recognition Visual RT Improvement (MD=-15.20ms, Matyus 2025)
    |       +-> Possible Schizophrenia Symptom Reduction (Moshfeghinia 2024)
    |
    +-> Caffeine Interaction (when co-administered)
            |
            +-> Enhanced Cognitive Effects (choice RT SMD=-0.48 vs. -0.35 alone)
            +-> Attention Switching Improvement (SMD=0.33)
            +-> Reduced Caffeine-Induced Jitteriness (qualitative evidence)
```

This framework highlights that L-theanine operates through two primary pathways (alpha wave promotion and glutamate modulation) with an important third interaction pathway (caffeine synergy). The standalone effects through pathways 1 and 2 are small to moderate, while the combination effects through pathway 3 are medium-sized.

### Tier Impact Assessment
- **Previous tier:** Tier 2
- **Updated tier:** Tier 2 (maintained, no change)
- **Score change:** 84 -> 87 (+3)
- **New citation count:** Existing citations + 4 new = expanded evidence set
- **Rationale for no tier change:** Standalone L-theanine effects remain small-to-moderate across most domains. The strongest evidence is for sleep quality (SMD=0.43), which is a meaningful but not large effect. Cognitive benefits are domain-specific and modest without caffeine. Psychiatric evidence lacks quantitative pooling. The evidence does not yet meet the "consistent strong findings across multiple domains with multiple independent MAs" threshold required for Tier 1.

### Limitations of This Synthesis
1. The separation of L-theanine-alone vs. L-theanine+caffeine effects (Payne 2025) may reduce the perceived strength of L-theanine's evidence, but this reflects genuine scientific rigor -- previously, combination effects were often attributed to L-theanine alone
2. The cognitive meta-analysis (Matyus 2025) has only 5 RCTs and 148 participants, limiting confidence in the domain-specific findings. The non-significant results may reflect low power rather than true null effects
3. The psychiatric SR (Moshfeghinia 2024) lacks quantitative pooling, making it impossible to compare psychiatric effect sizes to other L-theanine domains
4. No new dose-response meta-analysis was identified for L-theanine. Optimal dosing remains based on individual study data rather than pooled analysis
5. The distinction between Suntheanine (pure L-isomer) and generic L-theanine preparations is not addressed in any included meta-analysis. Form-specific effects may exist but are not quantifiable with current evidence
6. Most RCTs in the L-theanine literature are acute (single dose) or short-term (1-8 weeks). Chronic supplementation evidence is minimal
7. The sleep meta-analysis (Bulman 2025) pools studies using different sleep measures (PSQI, actigraphy, polysomnography), which introduces methodological heterogeneity
