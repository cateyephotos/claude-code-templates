# Synthesis Notes: L-Tyrosine (ID: 33)
## Pipeline Run: 2026-03-06

---

### Mechanism Synthesis

#### Catecholamine Precursor Hypothesis (Primary Mechanism)
**Evidence rating: Moderate**

L-tyrosine is the direct biosynthetic precursor to dopamine and norepinephrine via the rate-limiting enzyme tyrosine hydroxylase. The mechanistic hypothesis is that under conditions of acute stress — cold, altitude, sleep deprivation, sustained cognitive demand — the brain's demand for catecholamine synthesis exceeds supply capacity, depleting dopamine and norepinephrine in prefrontal cortical circuits. Supplemental L-tyrosine restores synthesis substrate availability, reversing stress-induced catecholamine depletion and thereby restoring cognitive function.

**Supporting evidence:**
- Banderet & Lieberman (1989) established the environmental stress depletion model in humans
- Nobre 2015 review identifies catecholamine depletion reversal as the consensus mechanism across 15 controlled studies
- Jongkees 2020 provides the strongest mechanistic support via the inverted-U relationship: individuals with lower baseline dopaminergic function show greater response, consistent with restoration-not-enhancement pharmacology
- Lieberman 2015 synthesizes the "pharmacokinetic" basis: peak plasma tyrosine levels at 1–2 hours post-ingestion correlate with cognitive benefit windows

**Limitations:**
- No direct human in vivo measurement of central dopamine/norepinephrine depletion and restoration under tyrosine supplementation (cerebrospinal fluid studies absent)
- Inverted-U model is inferred from behavioral outcomes; direct neurotransmitter biomarker confirmation limited
- Cannot distinguish dopamine-mediated from norepinephrine-mediated cognitive effects in humans

**Rating "Moderate" rationale:** The mechanism is biologically plausible, consistent with pharmacology, and supported by indirect behavioral evidence across multiple independent laboratories. Not "Strong" because direct in vivo human mechanistic confirmation (CSF biomarkers, PET neuroimaging under supplementation) is absent.

---

#### Thyroid Hormone Precursor Pathway
**Evidence rating: Weak/Emerging**

L-tyrosine is also required for thyroid hormone (T3/T4) synthesis. Theoretical benefit for thyroid-depleted individuals exists. However, this pathway has minimal relevance for cognitive supplementation in healthy populations — thyroid hormone synthesis is not rate-limited by dietary tyrosine in euthyroid individuals. The "thyroid support" claim in supplements.js is a manufacturer-derived claim with minimal clinical trial support for the supplementation context.

### Clinical Benefit Synthesis

#### Cognitive Performance Under Acute Stress
**Evidence rating: Moderate**

The most consistent positive signal in the L-tyrosine literature. Multiple independent research groups (US Army Research Institute of Environmental Medicine — Banderet, Mahoney, Lieberman; Dutch Cognitive Psychology — Colzato, Jongkees; multiple civilian academic labs) have documented cognitive preservation effects under stress paradigms.

**Consistent findings:**
- Cold stress (Banderet 1989, Mahoney 2007): cognitive task performance preserved at 4°C / 4300m altitude vs placebo
- Psychological/occupational stress (Deijen 1999, Webster 2015): military cadet performance maintained during combat training
- Laboratory cognitive-stress paradigms (Jongkees 2020, Colzato 2015, Colzato 2018): working memory and executive function preserved under controlled demand
- Nobre 2015 identifies "most effective under demanding situational conditions" as the consensus conclusion across all reviewed studies

**Effect sizes:** d = 0.3–0.7 across stress paradigms (Nobre 2015); moderate for cognitive outcomes under stress

**Key conditional requirement:** The positive signal is consistently conditional — benefits emerge under stress or suboptimal dopaminergic baseline conditions. Studies in non-stressed populations show minimal or no benefit. This is the defining characteristic that limits L-tyrosine to Tier 3 (stress-specific efficacy without broad cognitive enhancement evidence).

---

#### Physical / Endurance Performance
**Evidence rating: Null (GRADE Very Low certainty)**

**KEY NULL FINDING — Fernandez-Sanchez 2024:** The most rigorous pooling of physical performance evidence. Meta-analysis of 8 RCTs (n=187) found:
- Pooled effect size: ES = -0.05 (95% CI: -0.34 to 0.24) — not significant, virtually zero effect
- GRADE certainty: Very Low for endurance outcomes
- Subgroup analyses: no benefit across exercise modalities, durations, or doses
- Conclusion: "Meta-analysis confirms limited efficacy for pure endurance performance"

This null finding is consistent with the mechanistic hypothesis: endurance exercise does not necessarily deplete central catecholamines to the degree required for tyrosine supplementation to provide benefit. The null is mechanistically coherent, not surprising.

**Practical implication:** L-tyrosine should NOT be marketed or recommended for physical performance enhancement. The cognitive-stress domain is its evidence-supported application.

---

#### Creative Thinking and Cognitive Flexibility
**Evidence rating: Preliminary**

Colzato 2015 (n=32) demonstrated improved convergent thinking (Remote Associates Test) with tyrosine vs. placebo. This is the only study specifically addressing creativity. Effect is plausible via dopaminergic cognitive flexibility mechanisms. Evidence is too limited (single small RCT) to form a clinical recommendation but is directionally consistent with the catecholamine precursor hypothesis.

---

#### Age-Related Cognitive Benefits
**Evidence rating: Preliminary**

Colzato 2018 (n=24, older adults 60–75) showed proactive response inhibition improvement with tyrosine in older adults. Mechanistically coherent with age-related dopaminergic decline. One small study — insufficient for specific age-group recommendation but consistent with the baseline-dependency model.

### Safety Synthesis

**Safety rating: Well-established** (for standard doses in healthy non-contraindicated populations)

**Consistent safety data across all included studies:**
- All individual RCTs (Banderet 1989, Deijen 1999, Mahoney 2007, Colzato 2015, 2018, Jongkees 2020, Harris 2024) report no serious adverse events
- Nobre 2015 review identifies "occasional nausea, headache at high doses" as the primary adverse effects
- Lieberman 2015 and Webster 2015 both confirm "well-tolerated" across military and civilian populations

**Key safety qualifications:**
1. **MAO inhibitor interaction (contraindicated):** L-tyrosine significantly increases catecholamine substrate availability; concurrent MAO inhibitor use can precipitate hypertensive crisis — this is a hard contraindication, not a precaution
2. **Hyperthyroidism:** Theoretical risk of exacerbating hyperthyroidism via thyroid hormone precursor pathway — exclude as a precaution
3. **Melanoma:** Melanin synthesis from tyrosine pathway — theoretical concern; most safety reviews include as precaution though clinical evidence of harm is limited
4. **Blood pressure:** Deijen 1999 documented BP reduction with chronic tyrosine supplementation — beneficial effect in cadets under stress but warrants monitoring in individuals with cardiovascular disease or hypotension

**Dose-dependent safety:** Doses used in RCTs range from 100–150 mg/kg (single dose, ~7–10g for 70kg individual) for acute protocols to 4g/day (2g twice daily) for chronic use. No organ toxicity or serious adverse effects reported at any studied dose in healthy populations.

---

### Dosage Synthesis

**Evidence-based dosage range:**

| Protocol | Dose | Timing | Source |
|----------|------|--------|--------|
| Acute cognitive stress | 100–150 mg/kg bodyweight (~7–10g for 70kg) | 1–2 hours before stressor | Lieberman 2015; Banderet 1989; Mahoney 2007 |
| Acute laboratory cognitive task | 2g fixed dose | 60–90 min before testing | Colzato 2015, 2018; Jongkees 2020; Harris 2024 |
| Chronic sustained stress | 2g twice daily (4g/day) | Daily during stress period | Deijen 1999 (1 week) |

**Critical dosage note:** N-acetyl tyrosine (NALT) — a commonly marketed alternative — has inferior bioavailability for central uptake compared to free-form L-tyrosine. Several reviews (Lieberman 2015; Webster 2015) note this. Free-form L-tyrosine is the evidence-based formulation.

### Cross-Study Patterns and Interpretive Themes

1. **Stress-conditionality is the defining characteristic:** L-tyrosine's cognitive effects are consistently conditional on depletion or stress states. This places it in a different category from supplements showing unconditional benefits. The "stress-specific catecholamine restoration" framing is the most accurate clinical description.

2. **Consistent independence of research groups:** Unlike chondroitin sulfate where manufacturer funding creates a specific COI risk, the L-tyrosine literature derives from military agencies, Dutch cognitive psychology labs, and academic medical centers — no single manufacturer has dominated the research agenda.

3. **Small N is systematic, not incidental:** No individual RCT has n>40. This is not a gap that later large RCTs have filled — there are no large pre-registered L-tyrosine trials. This systematic small-N characteristic is the primary reason for Tier 3 classification.

4. **Physical performance null is robust:** The Fernandez 2024 meta-analysis (GRADE very-low, ES -0.05) is consistent with Lieberman 2015 and Webster 2015 synthesizing physical performance null. Three independent sources (1 meta-analysis, 2 reviews) converge on null for physical outcomes. This is an established null, not a gap.

5. **Inverted-U model explains within-study variability:** The Jongkees 2020 baseline-dependency finding explains why some small studies show benefits and others do not — individuals with higher baseline dopaminergic function do not respond to tyrosine supplementation. This is mechanistically important and suggests that the positive cognitive-stress signal may be even more robust in properly selected populations (stressed, fatigued, suboptimal dopaminergic function).

---

### Evidence Limitations

1. **No large (n>100) pre-registered RCT exists** for any L-tyrosine outcome. This is the primary gap preventing Tier 2 classification.

2. **All positive cognitive evidence is from small N (n=8–40) or non-quantitative reviews.** While consistent, this evidence base cannot achieve the sample-size-based confidence of Tier 2 supplements.

3. **Acute protocols dominate:** With only one chronic study (Deijen 1999, 1 week), the long-term effects of L-tyrosine supplementation are essentially unknown from a controlled trial perspective.

4. **Female subjects underrepresented:** The majority of RCTs used male subjects (military cadets, male athletes). Hormonal interactions with catecholamine synthesis pathways in female athletes are not characterized.

5. **Combination supplement confound (Harris 2024):** The most recent and largest RCT (n=40) used L-theanine + L-tyrosine. Tyrosine-specific attribution cannot be isolated.
