### Per-Study Evaluation

#### Study: argeros_2025_bp_ma -- Blood Pressure Meta-Analysis
- **Citation:** Argeros et al. 2025, Hypertension
- **PMID:** 41000008
- **DOI:** 10.1161/HYPERTENSIONAHA.125.25129
- **Study type:** Meta-analysis (38 RCTs, 2709 participants)
- **Key finding:** Overall: SBP -2.81 mmHg (95% CI: -4.32 to -1.29), DBP -2.05 mmHg (95% CI: -3.23 to -0.88). Subgroups: hypertensive patients on medication SBP -7.68 mmHg; participants with hypomagnesemia SBP -5.97 mmHg.
- **GRADE domains:**
  - Risk of bias: Low -- published in Hypertension (AHA flagship journal, Q1); 38 RCTs provide robust pooled estimates with minimal influence from any single study; well-conducted with transparent methodology
  - Inconsistency: Not serious -- consistent direction of effect across all 38 RCTs for both SBP and DBP; subgroup analyses (hypertensive on meds, hypomagnesemia) show coherent dose-response patterns with larger effects in higher-risk populations
  - Indirectness: Not serious -- directly assesses oral magnesium supplementation and blood pressure outcomes in adults
  - Imprecision: Not serious -- 2709 participants across 38 RCTs yield narrow confidence intervals; both SBP and DBP estimates exclude zero
  - Publication bias: Low concern -- 38 studies provide reliable funnel plot assessment; published in a top-tier journal with rigorous peer review
- **Overall GRADE certainty:** High
- **Assigned evidence level:** Level 1
- **Quality score contribution:** 9/10
- **Notes:** This is the definitive blood pressure meta-analysis for magnesium supplementation, published in the American Heart Association's flagship journal Hypertension. The overall effect sizes (SBP -2.81 mmHg, DBP -2.05 mmHg) are clinically meaningful from a population health perspective -- a 2-3 mmHg reduction in systolic blood pressure is associated with approximately 5-7% reduction in cardiovascular mortality at the population level. The subgroup findings are particularly important: hypertensive patients already on medication showed a substantially larger effect (SBP -7.68 mmHg), suggesting that magnesium supplementation may be a useful adjunctive strategy. Similarly, the enhanced effect in hypomagnesemia (SBP -5.97 mmHg) supports the mechanism that magnesium's blood pressure effects are most pronounced when correcting a deficiency. This study UPDATES the existing blood pressure evidence in the database with the largest and most methodologically rigorous meta-analysis to date.

#### Study: maqrashi_2025_t2dm_ma -- T2DM Glycemic Control Meta-Analysis
- **Citation:** Maqrashi et al. 2025, Sultan Qaboos University Medical Journal
- **PMID:** 40641714
- **DOI:** 10.18295/2075-0528.2848
- **Study type:** Meta-analysis (23 RCTs, 1345 participants)
- **Key finding:** FBG WMD = -0.58 mmol/L (significant reduction). Serum Mg WMD = +0.69 mmol/L (significant increase, confirming supplementation efficacy). HbA1c WMD = -0.16% (minimal, not clinically significant). Greater effects in patients >=65 years and with longer supplementation duration.
- **GRADE domains:**
  - Risk of bias: Some concerns -- journal is regional (Sultan Qaboos University Medical Journal) rather than top-tier; however, 23 RCTs with 1345 participants provide substantial pooled evidence. Quality of individual included RCTs is variable
  - Inconsistency: Some concerns -- HbA1c effect (-0.16%) is minimal and clinically non-significant, creating a disconnect with the more meaningful FBG reduction. This inconsistency between fasting glucose and HbA1c suggests the effect may be more acute than sustained
  - Indirectness: Not serious -- directly assesses oral magnesium supplementation in patients with established T2DM
  - Imprecision: Not serious -- 1345 participants across 23 RCTs provide adequate statistical power for the glycemic endpoints studied
  - Publication bias: Some concern -- regional journal publication may indicate that stronger findings were submitted to higher-impact outlets; however, the large study count (23 RCTs) mitigates this concern
- **Overall GRADE certainty:** Moderate
- **Assigned evidence level:** Level 2
- **Quality score contribution:** 7/10
- **Notes:** This meta-analysis establishes magnesium supplementation as having a meaningful effect on fasting blood glucose in T2DM patients (WMD = -0.58 mmol/L) while confirming that long-term glycemic control (HbA1c) shows only minimal improvement (-0.16%). The disconnect between FBG and HbA1c is clinically significant: it suggests that magnesium may improve acute glucose homeostasis but has limited impact on sustained glycemic control as measured by the 3-month HbA1c marker. The age-dependent finding (greater effect in >=65 years) is biologically plausible, as older adults are more likely to be magnesium-deficient and may benefit more from supplementation. The serum magnesium increase (WMD = +0.69) confirms that oral supplementation effectively raises magnesium levels in this population. This represents a NEW benefit domain for the magnesium database entry.

#### Study: basit_2026_prediabetes_ma -- Prediabetes Glycemic Meta-Analysis
- **Citation:** Basit et al. 2026, Journal of Diabetes and Metabolic Disorders
- **PMID:** 41641401
- **DOI:** 10.1007/s40200-025-01853-9
- **Study type:** Systematic review and meta-analysis (5 RCTs, 384 participants)
- **Key finding:** 2-hour OGTT glucose MD = -0.99 mmol/L (p<0.00001). HOMA-IR MD = -1.10 (p=0.03). Triglycerides MD = -14.57 mg/dL. HDL-C MD = +3.87 mg/dL.
- **GRADE domains:**
  - Risk of bias: Some concerns -- only 5 RCTs with 384 participants; small study count increases vulnerability to individual study quality issues. Journal is specialized but not top-tier
  - Inconsistency: Not serious -- all outcomes (OGTT, HOMA-IR, triglycerides, HDL-C) show consistent beneficial direction; multi-endpoint consistency strengthens the finding
  - Indirectness: Not serious -- directly assesses oral magnesium supplementation in prediabetic populations
  - Imprecision: Serious -- 384 participants across 5 RCTs is a relatively small pooled sample. While p-values are significant (OGTT p<0.00001), the small study count limits generalizability
  - Publication bias: Possible concern -- only 5 studies limits funnel plot reliability; positive results from small studies may be overrepresented
- **Overall GRADE certainty:** Low
- **Assigned evidence level:** Level 3
- **Quality score contribution:** 6/10
- **Notes:** Despite the small study count (5 RCTs), the internal consistency across multiple metabolic endpoints (OGTT glucose, HOMA-IR, triglycerides, HDL-C) is noteworthy. The highly significant OGTT glucose reduction (MD = -0.99 mmol/L, p<0.00001) is clinically meaningful in prediabetes, where the 2-hour glucose value directly determines diagnostic classification. The HOMA-IR improvement (MD = -1.10) suggests magnesium enhances insulin sensitivity, which is the underlying pathophysiology driving prediabetes progression to T2DM. The lipid improvements (TG reduction, HDL-C increase) suggest broader metabolic benefits beyond glycemic control. This study complements Maqrashi 2025 (T2DM) by addressing the earlier stage of metabolic disease progression. The small study count (5 RCTs, n=384) is the primary limitation and warrants GRADE downgrading for imprecision. This represents a NEW benefit domain for the database.

#### Study: cepeda_2025_oxidative_ma -- Oxidative Stress and Inflammation SR/MA
- **Citation:** Cepeda et al. 2025, Antioxidants
- **PMID:** 40563371
- **DOI:** 10.3390/antiox14060740
- **Study type:** Systematic review and meta-analysis (28 studies, human and animal)
- **Key finding:** Significant CRP reduction (anti-inflammatory effect confirmed). No conclusive effects on oxidative stress markers (NO, TAC, MDA, GSH -- inconsistent or null findings across studies).
- **GRADE domains:**
  - Risk of bias: Some concerns -- mixed human and animal study design; animal studies included alongside human data may introduce cross-species heterogeneity. Journal is MDPI (open access, variable quality perception)
  - Inconsistency: Serious -- CRP findings are consistent and significant, but oxidative stress markers (NO, TAC, MDA, GSH) show highly inconsistent results across studies. The divergence between inflammatory and oxidative stress outcomes limits overall confidence
  - Indirectness: Some concerns -- inclusion of animal studies alongside human data introduces indirectness for human supplementation conclusions. Oxidative stress markers measured vary across studies
  - Imprecision: Some concerns -- while 28 studies is a reasonable count, the split between human and animal data reduces effective sample size for human-specific conclusions
  - Publication bias: Some concern -- MDPI journal; open access model may have different publication bias characteristics than traditional journals
- **Overall GRADE certainty:** Low (for CRP: Moderate; for oxidative stress markers: Very Low)
- **Assigned evidence level:** Level 2 (for CRP finding specifically)
- **Quality score contribution:** 6/10
- **Notes:** This study provides the first meta-analytic evidence linking magnesium supplementation to CRP reduction. CRP (C-reactive protein) is a well-validated biomarker of systemic inflammation, and its reduction is clinically relevant for cardiovascular and metabolic disease risk. The CRP finding is robust and consistent across studies, warranting Moderate GRADE certainty for that specific endpoint. However, the oxidative stress findings (NO, TAC, MDA, GSH) are inconclusive -- the lack of consistent effects across these markers suggests that magnesium's antioxidant properties, if present, are not reliably measurable with current study designs. The inclusion of animal studies is a methodological limitation that reduces directness for human supplement use conclusions. The primary value of this paper for the database is the CRP mechanism: magnesium exerts anti-inflammatory activity, which provides a mechanistic bridge to the blood pressure and metabolic benefits documented in other included studies. This is assigned to mechanisms rather than benefits because CRP is a biomarker rather than a clinical endpoint.

#### Study: wang_2025_metsyn_ma -- Metabolic Syndrome Inflammation Meta-Analysis
- **Citation:** Wang et al. 2025, Frontiers in Nutrition
- **PMID:** 41245414
- **DOI:** 10.3389/fnut.2025.1692937
- **Study type:** Systematic review and meta-analysis (8 RCTs, 444 participants)
- **Key finding:** CRP SMD = -0.327 (95% CI: -0.602 to -0.053, p=0.048). Greater anti-inflammatory effect in women. Optimal response at 12-16 weeks supplementation duration. Tablet/capsule form more effective than other delivery methods.
- **GRADE domains:**
  - Risk of bias: Some concerns -- 8 RCTs with 444 participants; moderate study count. Published in Frontiers in Nutrition (reputable open access, peer-reviewed)
  - Inconsistency: Not serious -- consistent direction of CRP reduction across included studies; subgroup analyses (sex, duration, form) show coherent patterns
  - Indirectness: Not serious -- directly assesses oral magnesium supplementation and inflammation in metabolic syndrome populations
  - Imprecision: Some concerns -- 444 participants across 8 RCTs is a relatively small pooled sample; the 95% CI for CRP nearly excludes zero (lower bound -0.053), indicating borderline precision
  - Publication bias: Some concern -- small number of studies (8) limits funnel plot reliability
- **Overall GRADE certainty:** Moderate
- **Assigned evidence level:** Level 2
- **Quality score contribution:** 7/10
- **Notes:** This meta-analysis converges with Cepeda 2025 on the CRP reduction finding, providing independent confirmation that magnesium supplementation reduces systemic inflammation. The metabolic syndrome population is clinically relevant because metabolic syndrome is characterized by chronic low-grade inflammation, and CRP is a standard diagnostic and monitoring marker. The subgroup findings add practical value: the greater effect in women may reflect higher baseline magnesium deficiency rates in women or sex-specific inflammatory pathways. The 12-16 week optimal duration aligns with typical supplement trial lengths and provides actionable guidance. The tablet/capsule finding addresses a common consumer question about magnesium form. The borderline p-value (0.048) and lower CI bound (-0.053) indicate that this finding, while statistically significant, is not robust to small perturbations in the data. This represents a NEW benefit domain for the database, complementing the CRP mechanism documented in Cepeda 2025.

### Aggregate Quality Metrics
- **Average study quality:** 7.0/10
- **Evidence level distribution:** Level 1: 1 paper (Argeros BP), Level 2: 3 papers (Maqrashi T2DM, Cepeda CRP mechanism, Wang MetSyn), Level 3: 1 paper (Basit prediabetes)
- **Replication assessment:** Good -- CRP anti-inflammatory finding independently confirmed by two separate meta-analyses (Cepeda 2025, Wang 2025). Glycemic control findings converge across T2DM (Maqrashi) and prediabetes (Basit) populations
- **Geographic diversity:** USA/international (Argeros), Middle East (Maqrashi), Pakistan/international (Basit), Colombia/international (Cepeda), China (Wang)
- **Positive/negative balance:** 5 positive findings across blood pressure, glycemic control, and inflammation domains. Cepeda 2025 oxidative stress markers were inconclusive (negative finding within a partially positive study). No outright negative findings for any established benefit domain
- **Domain expansion:** Three entirely new benefit domains added (T2DM glycemic control, prediabetes glycemic control, metabolic syndrome inflammation) plus one new mechanism (anti-inflammatory activity via CRP). One existing domain updated (blood pressure)
