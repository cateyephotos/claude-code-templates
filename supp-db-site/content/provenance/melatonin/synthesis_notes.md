## Evidence Synthesis

### Overall Narrative

The 2024-2026 evidence update for melatonin reinforces its position as one of the most extensively studied dietary supplements. Three new systematic reviews/meta-analyses were identified that extend melatonin's evidence base into ICU/critical care (Tang 2025), Alzheimer's disease (Terao & Kodama 2024), and Parkinson's disease sleep (Yousef 2025). These additions bring the total number of health domains with SR/MA-level evidence to 8+, further solidifying melatonin's Tier 1 status.

The most significant finding from this update is the Tang 2025 meta-analysis, which represents the largest melatonin meta-analysis published to date (32 RCTs, n=3,895). This study demonstrates clinically meaningful reductions in ICU delirium incidence (28% relative risk reduction) and improvements in sleep quality (SMD=0.54), adding critical care as a new clinical application domain.

### Key Themes Across New Evidence

1. **Expansion into hospital/clinical settings:** The Tang 2025 study moves melatonin evidence beyond community-based supplementation into acute care settings, suggesting broader clinical utility.

2. **Neuroprotective applications:** Both the Terao & Kodama 2024 NMA (Alzheimer's) and Yousef 2025 MA (Parkinson's sleep) support melatonin's role in neurodegenerative disease management. The NMA finding that melatonin ranks favorably against anti-amyloid antibodies is particularly noteworthy for cost-effectiveness considerations.

3. **Sleep remains the anchor domain:** All three new papers include sleep-related outcomes, confirming that sleep quality improvement remains melatonin's best-supported clinical application regardless of the underlying condition.

---

## Clinical Implications

### For Healthcare Providers

- **ICU practice:** Consider melatonin as part of delirium prevention bundles in ICU settings. The 28% delirium risk reduction (Tang 2025) is clinically meaningful and comparable to some pharmacological interventions.
- **Neurodegenerative disease:** Melatonin may serve as an adjunctive intervention for sleep disturbances in Alzheimer's and Parkinson's disease, where existing pharmacological options carry significant side effect burden.
- **Dose considerations:** The heterogeneity in dosing across included studies highlights the need for standardized dosing guidelines, particularly in clinical settings.

### For Consumers

- Melatonin's evidence base continues to support its use for sleep quality improvement across diverse populations and clinical conditions.
- The safety profile remains favorable with no new safety signals identified in the 2024-2026 literature.
- Consumers using melatonin for sleep should be aware of the expanding evidence for additional health benefits, though these applications are best guided by healthcare providers.

## Evidence Gaps Identified

### High Priority Gaps

1. **Dose-optimization studies:** While dose-response is established, optimal dosing for specific populations (elderly, ICU, neurodegenerative disease) requires further investigation.
2. **Long-term safety data:** Most meta-analyses include studies of relatively short duration. Long-term (>1 year) safety data from large prospective cohorts are needed.
3. **Formulation comparisons:** Immediate-release vs. extended-release vs. sublingual formulations are rarely compared head-to-head in meta-analyses.

### Moderate Priority Gaps

4. **Melatonin in metabolic disease:** Emerging evidence on glycemic regulation and metabolic effects lacks a comprehensive meta-analysis.
5. **Pediatric long-term outcomes:** While Cochrane reviews support short-term use in children, long-term developmental outcomes are under-studied.
6. **Bioavailability and timing:** The relationship between timing of administration, circadian phase, and clinical outcomes needs more systematic investigation.

### Low Priority Gaps

7. **Melatonin-drug interactions:** Systematic reviews of interactions with common medications (antihypertensives, SSRIs, benzodiazepines) would be valuable for clinical practice.
8. **Exogenous vs. endogenous melatonin:** The relationship between supplemental melatonin and endogenous production trajectories (especially with aging) is poorly characterized.

## Domain-Specific Synthesis

### Sleep (Primary Domain)
- **Strength of evidence:** Very strong (multiple Cochrane reviews + new MAs)
- **Effect magnitude:** Moderate (SOL reduction of ~7-15 minutes, PSQI improvements of 1.5-2.0 points)
- **Population breadth:** General adults, elderly, ICU patients, Parkinson's disease, children with neurodevelopmental conditions
- **New contribution:** PD-specific sleep data (Yousef 2025) and ICU sleep quality (Tang 2025)

### Neurological/Neuroprotection (Expanding Domain)
- **Strength of evidence:** Moderate (NMA + population-specific MAs)
- **Effect magnitude:** Variable (favorable NMA ranking in AD; clinically meaningful sleep improvements in PD)
- **Key finding:** Melatonin's competitive ranking against anti-amyloid antibodies in the Terao & Kodama NMA suggests neuroprotective effects beyond simple sleep improvement
- **Limitation:** Indirect evidence from NMA; direct head-to-head trials needed

### Critical Care (New Domain)
- **Strength of evidence:** Strong (32-RCT MA with GRADE assessment)
- **Effect magnitude:** Moderate (28% delirium reduction, 0.57-day ICU LOS reduction)
- **Clinical significance:** Delirium prevention is a high-priority ICU quality metric; melatonin offers a low-risk adjunctive intervention
- **Limitation:** Generalizability to non-ICU hospitalized patients is uncertain
