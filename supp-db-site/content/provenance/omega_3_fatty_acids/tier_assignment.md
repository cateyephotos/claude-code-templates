### Quality Score Change Narrative
The increase from 92 to 94 is driven primarily by two factors:
1. **Meta-analysis coverage (+2):** The AMD meta-analysis and metabolic syndrome meta-analysis fill gaps where previous evidence relied on individual RCTs or narrative reviews. Omega-3 now has dedicated meta-analyses across virtually all claimed benefit domains.
2. **RCT power (+1):** The metabolic syndrome meta-analysis alone contributes 21 RCTs with ~1,950 participants, and the preeclampsia meta-analysis pools 16,237 participants — substantially increasing the total evidence base.
3. **Recency adjustment (-1):** While 6 new papers are added, some older citations (pre-2023) are aging. This minor offset reflects the natural evidence lifecycle.

### Research Maturity: Mature (unchanged)
**Justification:** Omega-3 fatty acids remain one of the most extensively studied dietary supplements. The publication span now extends continuously from the early 2000s through 2026 with active meta-analytic synthesis across multiple domains. New research is expanding into novel benefit domains (liver health, exercise synergy) while consolidating established ones (cardiovascular, eye health, pregnancy).

### Evidence Gaps Identified
1. Long-term (>5 year) supplementation outcomes remain limited outside of cardiovascular endpoints
2. Optimal EPA:DHA ratio for specific conditions requires head-to-head comparison meta-analysis
3. Exercise synergy evidence is preliminary — need larger RCTs with standardized protocols
4. MASLD/liver health evidence limited to 7 RCTs with 439 participants — need confirmation
5. PAD finding highlights that omega-3 cardiovascular benefits are domain-specific, not universal
6. Cognitive function evidence from new period did not yield qualifying papers — existing coverage remains from prior update
