## Paper 1: Zeng et al. 2024 — Cognitive Function SR+MA

### Identification

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| **PMID** | 39474788 |
| **DOI** | 10.1002/ptr.8359 |
| **Title** | Systematic review and meta-analysis of ginseng effects on cognitive function |
| **Authors** | Zeng Y, et al. |
| **Journal** | Phytotherapy Research |
| **Year** | 2024 |
| **Quality Score** | **7/10** |

### Study Design and Scale

| Parameter | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Design** | Systematic review with meta-analysis |
| **Methodology** | Random-effects model, subgroup analysis by cognitive domain and dose |
| **Studies Included** | 15 RCTs |
| **Total Participants** | n = 671 |
| **Registration** | PROSPERO CRD42024514231 |
| **Follow-up Range** | Variable (weeks to months) |

### Key Findings

| Cognitive Domain | Effect Estimate | 95% CI | p-value | Significance |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Memory | SMD = 0.19 | 0.02 to 0.36 | < 0.05 | Significant (small effect) |
| High-dose memory | SMD = 0.33 | 0.04 to 0.61 | < 0.05 | Significant (small-moderate effect) |
| Overall cognition | — | — | 0.86 | Not significant |
| Attention | — | — | 0.54 | Not significant |
| Executive function | — | — | 0.79 | Not significant |

### PICO Assessment

- **Population:** Adults across health statuses (healthy, MCI, and cognitive decline). Mixed age ranges. Both Asian and Western populations included.
- **Intervention:** Ginseng extract (primarily P. ginseng, some studies mixed Panax species). Various dosages and extract preparations.
- **Comparator:** Placebo in all included RCTs.
- **Outcomes:** Cognitive function assessed by domain — memory (primary positive finding), attention, executive function, overall cognition composite scores.

### Strengths

1. PROSPERO registered (CRD42024514231) — methodological transparency and pre-specified protocol
2. Domain-specific subgroup analysis reveals the selective nature of ginseng's cognitive effects, avoiding the misleading conclusion of "cognitive enhancement" when only memory is affected
3. Dose-response subgroup analysis (high-dose SMD = 0.33 vs. overall SMD = 0.19) provides clinically actionable dosing information
4. Clear reporting of null findings (attention, executive function) with p-values, avoiding publication bias toward positive results
5. First comprehensive ginseng-specific cognitive meta-analysis with this level of domain granularity

### Weaknesses

1. **Small total sample size** (n = 671) — limits statistical power and generalizability. Many individual RCTs had fewer than 50 participants.
2. **Memory effect is small** (SMD = 0.19) — while statistically significant, the clinical meaningfulness of a 0.19 SMD is debatable. Even the high-dose subgroup (SMD = 0.33) is a small-to-moderate effect.
3. **Species heterogeneity** — not all 15 RCTs used pure P. ginseng; some included mixed Panax preparations, diluting the species-specific evidence.
4. **Preparation heterogeneity** — included studies used different extract types (standardized, whole root, specific ginsenoside formulations), making dosing comparisons imprecise.
5. **Predominantly Asian populations** — limits generalizability to Western supplement consumers.
6. **No long-term follow-up assessment** — all included RCTs were relatively short-term. Long-term cognitive preservation effects are unknown.

### Impact on Evidence File

- **Citation category:** Benefits — Cognitive function (memory specific)
- **Evidence weight:** Moderate — establishes a specific, limited cognitive benefit
- **Updates:** Provides first domain-specific cognitive effect estimates for the supplement profile
- **Claim calibration:** Memory claims can be supported; general "cognitive enhancement" claims cannot

## Paper 2: Kim et al. 2025 — MMSE/ADAS-Cog SR+MA

### Identification

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| **PMID** | 40774237 |
| **DOI** | 10.1159/000547543 |
| **Title** | Effects of ginseng on MMSE and ADAS-Cog scores: A systematic review and meta-analysis |
| **Authors** | Kim J, et al. |
| **Journal** | Dementia and Geriatric Cognitive Disorders |
| **Year** | 2025 |
| **Quality Score** | **7/10** |

### Study Design and Scale

| Parameter | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Design** | Systematic review with meta-analysis |
| **Methodology** | Random-effects model, heterogeneity assessment (I-squared) |
| **Studies Included** | 8 RCTs |
| **Total Participants** | Not explicitly reported (estimated ~400-600 from included studies) |
| **Registration** | Not reported |
| **Outcome Measures** | MMSE (Mini-Mental State Examination), ADAS-Cog (Alzheimer's Disease Assessment Scale — Cognitive) |

### Key Findings

| Outcome Measure | Effect Estimate | 95% CI | p-value | I-squared | Interpretation |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| MMSE | MD = 0.68 | 0.03 to 1.32 | 0.04 | 66% | Significant, substantial heterogeneity |
| ADAS-Cog | MD = -1.10 | -1.82 to -0.38 | 0.003 | 0% | Significant, no heterogeneity |

### PICO Assessment

- **Population:** Adults with cognitive impairment or dementia (predominantly Alzheimer's disease and vascular dementia). The use of MMSE and ADAS-Cog as outcome measures indicates a clinically impaired population rather than healthy adults.
- **Intervention:** Ginseng extract (P. ginseng). Various doses and preparations across the 8 included RCTs.
- **Comparator:** Placebo.
- **Outcomes:** MMSE score change from baseline (higher = better). ADAS-Cog score change from baseline (lower = better, as ADAS-Cog is an error/deficit scale).

### Strengths

1. **ADAS-Cog finding with I-squared = 0%** — Zero heterogeneity is exceptionally rare in supplement meta-analyses and indicates highly consistent treatment effects across all included studies. This is the strongest single finding in the ginseng cognitive evidence base.
2. **Uses gold-standard cognitive assessment instruments** — MMSE and ADAS-Cog are the most widely validated and clinically accepted cognitive scales, enabling direct comparison with pharmaceutical interventions (e.g., donepezil, memantine).
3. **ADAS-Cog effect size is clinically meaningful** — MD = -1.10 points on ADAS-Cog is comparable to the lower bound of cholinesterase inhibitor effects in mild AD (donepezil: ~-2.5 to -3.0 ADAS-Cog points).
4. **Complements Zeng 2024** — While Zeng focused on domain-specific cognition (memory, attention, executive function), Kim focuses on validated global cognitive scales, providing a different analytical lens on the same evidence base.

### Weaknesses

1. **Small number of RCTs** (n = 8) — limited statistical power for meta-analysis. Sensitivity analyses are constrained.
2. **MMSE heterogeneity is high** (I-squared = 66%) — the MMSE finding is less reliable than the ADAS-Cog finding due to substantial between-study variability. The pooled effect may not accurately represent the true treatment effect.
3. **MMSE effect size is borderline** — MD = 0.68 with a 95% CI that barely excludes zero (lower bound 0.03). This is a statistically fragile finding that could shift with the addition of a single negative study.
4. **No PROSPERO registration reported** — raises concerns about post-hoc analytical decisions and outcome reporting bias.
5. **Population limited to cognitive impairment** — findings apply to clinically impaired populations, not healthy adults seeking cognitive enhancement.
6. **Overlap with Zeng 2024 studies** — some RCTs may appear in both meta-analyses, meaning the two papers are not fully independent evidence sources.

### Impact on Evidence File

- **Citation category:** Benefits — Cognitive function (validated scales)
- **Evidence weight:** Moderate — ADAS-Cog finding is strong; MMSE finding is borderline
- **Updates:** Provides effect estimates in clinically meaningful units (MMSE points, ADAS-Cog points) that enable pharmaceutical comparison
- **Claim calibration:** Supports modest cognitive benefit claims in impaired populations; does not support claims in healthy adults

## Paper 3: Jafari et al. 2025 — CVD Dose-Response Meta-Analysis

### Identification

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| **PMID** | 40923100 |
| **DOI** | 10.1017/S0007114525103607 |
| **Title** | Dose-response meta-analysis of Panax ginseng effects on cardiovascular disease risk factors |
| **Authors** | Jafari A, et al. |
| **Journal** | British Journal of Nutrition |
| **Year** | 2025 |
| **Quality Score** | **8/10** |

### Study Design and Scale

| Parameter | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Design** | Dose-response meta-analysis |
| **Methodology** | Random-effects model, non-linear dose-response modeling, subgroup analyses |
| **Studies Included** | 70 RCTs |
| **Total Participants** | n = 4,506 |
| **Registration** | Not reported |
| **Biomarkers Assessed** | Inflammatory (hs-CRP, IL-6), oxidative stress (ROS, SOD, MDA), glycemic (FBG, fasting insulin, HbA1c, HOMA-IR), lipid (TC, LDL, HDL, TG), blood pressure (SBP, DBP), anthropometric (BMI, body weight) |

### Key Findings

#### Significant Effects (positive findings)

| Biomarker | Effect Estimate (SMD) | Direction | Clinical Significance |
|---|---|---|---|
| hs-CRP | SMD = -0.23 | Reduction (beneficial) | Small anti-inflammatory effect |
| ROS | SMD = -0.94 | Reduction (beneficial) | Large antioxidant effect |
| SOD | SMD = 0.48 | Increase (beneficial) | Moderate antioxidant enhancement |
| FBG | Dose-response significant | Reduction | Dose-dependent glucose lowering |
| IL-6 | Dose-response significant | Reduction | Dose-dependent anti-inflammatory |
| DBP | Dose-response significant | Reduction | Dose-dependent diastolic BP lowering |
| Fasting insulin | Dose-response significant | Reduction | Dose-dependent insulin reduction |

#### Null Effects (no significant impact)

| Biomarker Category | Finding |
|---|---|
| Lipids (TC, LDL, HDL, TG) | No significant effects |
| Blood pressure (SBP) | No significant effect on systolic BP |
| Glycemia (HbA1c, HOMA-IR) | No significant effects |
| Anthropometrics (BMI, body weight) | No significant effects |

### PICO Assessment

- **Population:** Adults across health statuses. Includes healthy adults, adults with metabolic syndrome, type 2 diabetes, hypertension, and other cardiovascular risk factors.
- **Intervention:** Panax ginseng extract, oral supplementation, various doses (dose-response analysis covers a wide dosing range).
- **Comparator:** Placebo in all included RCTs.
- **Outcomes:** Comprehensive panel of cardiovascular disease risk biomarkers organized by pathophysiological category.

### Strengths

1. **Largest ginseng CVD meta-analysis** — 70 RCTs with 4,506 participants represents the most comprehensive CVD evidence synthesis for ginseng to date.
2. **Dose-response methodology** — Non-linear dose-response modeling provides clinically actionable dosing information beyond simple "supplement vs. placebo" comparisons.
3. **Comprehensive biomarker panel** — Assesses inflammatory, oxidative stress, glycemic, lipid, blood pressure, and anthropometric markers in a single analysis, enabling a holistic cardiovascular risk assessment.
4. **Clear mechanistic profile** — Establishes anti-inflammatory/antioxidant activity (hs-CRP, ROS, SOD) as the primary cardiovascular mechanism, with secondary dose-dependent effects on glucose metabolism and diastolic blood pressure.
5. **Honest null reporting** — Transparently reports null effects on lipids, blood pressure, and anthropometrics, resisting the temptation to over-claim cardiovascular benefits.
6. **Published in British Journal of Nutrition** — High-impact nutrition science journal with rigorous peer review.

### Weaknesses

1. **No PROSPERO registration reported** — For a 70-RCT meta-analysis, pre-registration would significantly strengthen the methodological confidence.
2. **hs-CRP effect is small** (SMD = -0.23) — While statistically significant, the clinical meaningfulness of this small anti-inflammatory effect is debatable for individual patient outcomes.
3. **ROS effect may be inflated** (SMD = -0.94) — Very large effect sizes in oxidative stress biomarkers often reflect measurement variability and assay heterogeneity rather than true biological effects.
4. **Dose-response for DBP but not SBP** — The selective diastolic-only blood pressure finding is unusual and may reflect statistical noise rather than a true pharmacological effect.
5. **Heterogeneity across 70 RCTs** — The wide range of ginseng preparations, doses, treatment durations, and populations introduces substantial clinical heterogeneity that may limit the precision of pooled estimates.
6. **Biomarker-level evidence only** — All outcomes are intermediate biomarkers, not hard clinical endpoints (e.g., myocardial infarction, stroke, cardiovascular mortality). Biomarker improvements do not guarantee clinical benefit.

### Impact on Evidence File

- **Citation category:** Benefits — Cardiovascular / Anti-inflammatory
- **Evidence weight:** High — largest CVD evidence synthesis with dose-response data
- **Updates:** Establishes anti-inflammatory/antioxidant profile as primary CVD mechanism; refutes lipid and blood pressure claims
- **Claim calibration:** Anti-inflammatory claims supported; lipid-lowering and blood pressure claims NOT supported

## Cross-Paper Synthesis

### Evidence Convergence

| Theme | Papers Supporting | Confidence |
|---|---|---|
| Memory improvement (small effect) | Zeng 2024 | Moderate (small SMD, small n) |
| ADAS-Cog improvement (consistent) | Kim 2025 | Moderate-High (I-squared = 0%) |
| No effect on attention/executive function | Zeng 2024 | Moderate (clear null, adequate power) |
| Anti-inflammatory effect (hs-CRP) | Jafari 2025 | Moderate (small effect, large n) |
| Antioxidant effect (ROS, SOD) | Jafari 2025 | Moderate (large effect, potential measurement issues) |
| No lipid effects | Jafari 2025 | High (70 RCTs, null) |
| No blood pressure effects (SBP) | Jafari 2025 | High (70 RCTs, null) |
| Dose-response relationships | Zeng 2024, Jafari 2025 | Moderate (both show dose dependency) |

### Evidence Gaps Identified

1. No Cochrane review for Panax ginseng cognitive effects (contrast with Ginkgo biloba which has Wieland 2026)
2. No evidence synthesis for ginseng effects on fatigue/energy — a primary consumer use case
3. No meta-analysis of ginseng effects on exercise performance
4. No head-to-head NMA comparing ginseng to other adaptogens (Rhodiola, Ashwagandha)
5. No evidence synthesis for ginseng effects on sexual function/erectile dysfunction
6. No long-term safety meta-analysis specific to P. ginseng
7. Limited evidence in healthy adult populations for cognitive outcomes
8. No evidence synthesis for ginseng effects on immune function
