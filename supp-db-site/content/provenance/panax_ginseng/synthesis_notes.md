## Executive Summary

Panax ginseng has a broad but shallow evidence profile. The updated evidence from three meta-analyses (totaling 93 RCTs across cognitive and cardiovascular endpoints) reveals a supplement with selective cognitive effects limited to memory (SMD = 0.19, high-dose SMD = 0.33) and no benefit for attention, executive function, or overall cognition. Global cognitive scales (MMSE, ADAS-Cog) show modest but statistically significant improvements, with the ADAS-Cog finding showing zero heterogeneity across 8 RCTs. The cardiovascular evidence from 70 RCTs establishes anti-inflammatory and antioxidant activity (hs-CRP, ROS, SOD) as the primary cardiovascular mechanism, with dose-response relationships for fasting glucose, inflammation, and diastolic blood pressure, but no effects on lipids, systolic blood pressure, or body composition. Data integrity issues were identified including three fabricated safety citations, a species mismatch (Vuksan 2000 is P. quinquefolius), and a citation with missing identifiers. Tier 2 is maintained with quality score adjustment from 81 to 83. The tier is constrained by the absence of a current Cochrane review, the domain-selective nature of cognitive effects, small effect sizes, and the evidence breadth exceeding evidence depth pattern.

## Key Clinical Findings

### 1. Selective Cognitive Enhancement: Memory Only

The most important finding from this evidence update is the clarification that ginseng's cognitive effects are domain-selective. Zeng 2024 provides the first comprehensive domain-specific analysis:

| Cognitive Domain | Effect | Evidence Quality |
|---|---|---|
| Memory | Positive (SMD = 0.19) | Moderate — significant, PROSPERO registered |
| Memory (high-dose) | Positive (SMD = 0.33) | Moderate — dose-response supports biological plausibility |
| Attention | Null (p = 0.54) | Moderate — adequate power to detect, clearly null |
| Executive function | Null (p = 0.79) | Moderate — adequate power to detect, clearly null |
| Overall cognition | Null (p = 0.86) | Moderate — clearly null |

This pattern has important implications for how ginseng is marketed and positioned:

- **"Cognitive enhancement" claims are NOT supported** — The null finding for overall cognition (p = 0.86) directly contradicts broad cognitive enhancement marketing.
- **"Memory support" claims ARE supported** — With appropriate caveats about effect size (small) and population (predominantly impaired adults).
- **Dose-dependency strengthens biological plausibility** — The high-dose subgroup effect (SMD = 0.33) is nearly double the overall effect (SMD = 0.19), suggesting a genuine pharmacological dose-response rather than placebo effects.

### 2. Global Cognitive Scale Evidence: ADAS-Cog Zero Heterogeneity

Kim 2025 provides a complementary perspective using the two most widely validated global cognitive assessment instruments:

- **ADAS-Cog improvement (MD = -1.10, p = 0.003, I-squared = 0%)** — This is the single strongest finding in the ginseng cognitive evidence base. Zero heterogeneity across 8 RCTs means every study found essentially the same treatment effect, which is exceptionally rare in supplement research. For context:
  - Donepezil typically shows ADAS-Cog improvements of -2.5 to -3.0 points
  - Ginseng's -1.10 points is roughly 35-45% of the donepezil effect
  - This positions ginseng as a potential complementary therapy, not a replacement for pharmaceutical cognitive interventions

- **MMSE improvement (MD = 0.68, p = 0.04, I-squared = 66%)** — This is a weaker finding due to:
  - High heterogeneity (I-squared = 66%) indicating inconsistent effects across studies
  - Borderline significance (lower CI = 0.03, nearly touching zero)
  - A 0.68-point MMSE improvement may not be clinically detectable in individual patients

### 3. Cardiovascular Mechanism: Anti-Inflammatory, Not Lipid-Lowering

Jafari 2025 clarifies ginseng's cardiovascular mechanism profile with 70 RCTs:

```
GINSENG CVD MECHANISM MAP (Jafari 2025, 70 RCTs, n=4,506)

PRIMARY MECHANISM (Significant):
  Anti-inflammatory ───── hs-CRP: SMD = -0.23 (small effect)
                          IL-6: dose-response significant
  Antioxidant ─────────── ROS: SMD = -0.94 (large effect)
                          SOD: SMD = +0.48 (moderate effect)

SECONDARY EFFECTS (Dose-response only):
  Glucose metabolism ──── FBG: dose-response significant
                          Fasting insulin: dose-response significant
  Blood pressure ──────── DBP: dose-response significant (diastolic only)

NULL EFFECTS (Not supported):
  Lipids ──────────────── TC, LDL, HDL, TG: all null
  Blood pressure ──────── SBP: null (systolic)
  Glycemia ────────────── HbA1c, HOMA-IR: null
  Anthropometrics ─────── BMI, body weight: null
```

This mechanistic map has direct implications for claim calibration:

- **Anti-inflammatory claims:** Supported, but with small effect size (hs-CRP SMD = -0.23). Should be positioned as mild anti-inflammatory rather than potent.
- **Antioxidant claims:** Supported with larger effect sizes (ROS SMD = -0.94). However, the clinical significance of ROS reduction measured in vitro assays is uncertain.
- **Cholesterol/lipid claims:** NOT supported. 70 RCTs showing null lipid effects is definitive evidence of no effect.
- **Blood pressure claims:** NOT supported (SBP null). The DBP-only dose-response finding is unusual and insufficient to support broad BP claims.
- **Blood sugar claims:** Partially supported (FBG and fasting insulin dose-response), but null HbA1c means no long-term glycemic control benefit.

### 4. Dose-Response Evidence Strengthens Biological Plausibility

Both Zeng 2024 and Jafari 2025 demonstrate dose-dependent effects:

| Source | Low Dose | High Dose | Interpretation |
|---|---|---|---|
| Zeng 2024 — Memory | SMD = 0.19 (pooled) | SMD = 0.33 (high-dose subgroup) | Higher doses show stronger memory effects |
| Jafari 2025 — FBG | Not reported (linear) | Dose-response curve significant | Dose-dependent glucose lowering |
| Jafari 2025 — hs-CRP | Not reported (linear) | Dose-response curve significant | Dose-dependent anti-inflammatory |
| Jafari 2025 — DBP | Not reported (linear) | Dose-response curve significant | Dose-dependent diastolic BP reduction |

Dose-response relationships are one of the Bradford Hill criteria for causal inference and significantly strengthen the case that ginseng's observed effects are genuine pharmacological activity rather than placebo or study artifact.

## Data Integrity Assessment

### Critical Issues Requiring Resolution

This evidence update identified significant data integrity issues in the existing Panax Ginseng evidence file that must be addressed independently of the evidence update:

#### 1. Fabricated Safety Citations (Severity: HIGH)

Three safety citations show a consistent fabrication pattern:

| Pattern Element | Value | Assessment |
|---|---|---|
| Authors | "Research team" | Not a valid academic author attribution. No real researcher uses this as a byline. Hallmark of AI-generated fabrication. |
| Year | 2023 | Generic recent year, consistent with fabrication. |
| Journal | "Clinical review" | Not a real journal title. No ISSN exists for "Clinical review" as a standalone journal name. |
| Identifiers | None | No PMID, no DOI, no verifiable link to any publication database. |

**Recommendation:** Remove all three fabricated citations immediately. Replace with legitimate safety data from:
- Jafari 2025 (70 RCTs reporting adverse event data)
- The WHO monograph on Panax ginseng
- The European Medicines Agency (EMA) assessment report

#### 2. Species Mismatch — Vuksan 2000 (Severity: MODERATE)

Vuksan 2000 (benefit[4]) studied P. quinquefolius (American ginseng) for glycemic control in type 2 diabetes. This citation:
- Provides evidence for the WRONG species
- Inflates the apparent P. ginseng evidence base
- May mislead consumers into expecting P. ginseng effects based on P. quinquefolius data
- Should be removed from the P. ginseng entry or clearly flagged as a cross-species reference

#### 3. Missing Identifiers — Ellis 2002 (Severity: LOW-MODERATE)

Ellis 2002 (benefit[3]) lacks both PMID and DOI. Without these identifiers:
- The citation cannot be independently verified
- The full text cannot be retrieved for content validation
- The citation's continued inclusion depends on whether the study can be identified through other bibliographic searches

#### 4. Duplicate isEnhanced Keys (Severity: LOW)

Three instances of duplicate `isEnhanced` key declarations in supplements.js cause the later value to silently overwrite the earlier one per JavaScript object rules. This is a code-level issue that should be fixed during the next supplements.js maintenance pass.

## Comparative Context

### Ginseng vs. Other Herbal Nootropics

| Supplement | Tier | Cognitive Evidence Quality | Key Advantage | Key Limitation |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Panax Ginseng (ID 15)** | **2** | Memory MA + ADAS-Cog MA | Dose-response, broad CVD evidence | Domain-selective, no Cochrane |
| Ginkgo Biloba (ID 14) | 2 | Cochrane 82 RCTs + NMA | Gold-standard Cochrane, VCI niche | Low GRADE certainty, MCI null |
| Bacopa Monnieri | 2 | NMA (SUCRA rankings) | Best-in-class working memory | Gastrointestinal AEs |
| Lion's Mane | 3 | Limited RCTs | Novel NGF mechanism | Very small evidence base |
| Rhodiola Rosea | 2 | Moderate RCT base | Fatigue/stress evidence | Limited cognitive MA |

### Key Competitive Insights

1. **Ginseng lacks a Cochrane review** — Both Ginkgo biloba (Wieland 2026) and several pharmaceutical cognitive interventions have current Cochrane reviews. The absence of a Cochrane review for P. ginseng is a significant evidence gap.
2. **Ginseng's CVD evidence base is unusually large** — The 70-RCT Jafari 2025 MA is larger than CVD meta-analyses for most other herbal supplements, providing a unique competitive advantage in the cardiovascular claim space.
3. **Dose-response evidence differentiates ginseng** — Few herbal supplements have documented dose-response relationships across multiple endpoints. This distinguishes ginseng from supplements with flat dose-response curves.
4. **Species confusion is a unique liability** — No other major herbal supplement faces the same level of species-level taxonomic confusion as the Panax genus. This creates a persistent evidence integrity risk.

## Implications for Supplement Database

### Claims Updates Needed

1. **Cognitive function claim:** Revise to specify "memory support" rather than broad "cognitive enhancement." Include the null findings for attention and executive function as evidence-based limitations.
2. **ADAS-Cog claim:** Consider adding a specific claim for cognitive scale improvement in impaired populations, with the caveat that the effect is approximately 35-45% of the donepezil benchmark.
3. **Anti-inflammatory claim:** Supported by 70-RCT MA (hs-CRP SMD = -0.23). Position as mild anti-inflammatory activity, not potent.
4. **Antioxidant claim:** Supported (ROS SMD = -0.94, SOD SMD = 0.48). Note that these are biomarker-level findings.
5. **Lipid claim:** REMOVE if present. 70-RCT null finding is definitive.
6. **Blood pressure claim:** REMOVE or significantly caveat. SBP null; DBP dose-response only.
7. **Blood sugar claim:** Partially supported (FBG, fasting insulin dose-response) but HbA1c null limits the strength of this claim.

### Dosage Updates Needed

- Cognitive: Consider specifying that higher doses show stronger memory effects (Zeng 2024 dose-response)
- CVD: Dose-response curves from Jafari 2025 should inform dosing recommendations for anti-inflammatory/antioxidant effects
- Specify that the evidence applies to P. ginseng (Asian/Korean ginseng) specifically, not P. quinquefolius or P. notoginseng

### Safety Updates Needed

- Remove 3 fabricated safety citations
- Replace with legitimate safety data from Jafari 2025 (70 RCTs, no significant AEs) and authoritative monographs
- Note drug interactions (anticoagulants, diabetes medications, MAO inhibitors) from established pharmacological references

## Future Research Directions

### High Priority

1. **Cochrane systematic review for P. ginseng cognitive effects** — The most critical evidence gap. A current Cochrane review would potentially enable a tier upgrade and provide GRADE certainty assessments that are currently missing from the ginseng cognitive evidence base.
2. **Healthy adult cognitive RCTs** — Large RCTs (n > 200) in healthy adults aged 40-65 are needed to determine whether ginseng benefits the primary supplement consumer population for cognitive outcomes.
3. **Fatigue and energy meta-analysis** — Fatigue reduction and energy enhancement are primary consumer use cases for ginseng supplementation, but no current meta-analysis addresses these outcomes. This is a major evidence-claim mismatch.

### Medium Priority

4. **Head-to-head adaptogen NMA** — A network meta-analysis comparing ginseng, Rhodiola, Ashwagandha, and Eleuthero for stress response and fatigue outcomes would provide critical competitive landscape data.
5. **Exercise performance meta-analysis** — Ginseng is marketed for athletic performance, but no current evidence synthesis supports or refutes this claim.
6. **Sexual function meta-analysis** — Ginseng has traditional use for sexual vitality, and some RCTs have been conducted, but no comprehensive MA exists.
7. **Species-specific cognitive MA** — A meta-analysis that rigorously separates P. ginseng, P. quinquefolius, and P. notoginseng evidence would resolve the persistent species confusion in the literature.

### Lower Priority

8. **Immune function evidence synthesis** — Ginseng has immunomodulatory claims that lack systematic evidence review.
9. **Long-term safety analysis** — While Jafari 2025 provides short-term safety data, long-term safety (> 1 year continuous use) has not been systematically assessed.
10. **Ginsenoside-specific dose-response** — The active compounds (ginsenosides Rg1, Rb1, Rg3, etc.) have different pharmacological profiles. A ginsenoside-specific analysis would enable more precise formulation recommendations.
11. **Clinical endpoint CVD trial** — The biomarker-level CVD evidence (Jafari 2025) needs to be connected to clinical outcomes through either a long-term cardiovascular endpoint trial or a Mendelian randomization study.
