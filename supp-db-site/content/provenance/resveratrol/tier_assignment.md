**Step 5: Are findings consistent across reviews?**
→ PARTIALLY — Consistent for anti-inflammatory effects (CRP/hs-CRP) across multiple independent meta-analyses

→ Moderate consistency for: body composition, endothelial function, cognitive/CBF

**Decision:**

> **Tier 2 — Strong Evidence (with caveats)**

**Rationale:** Multiple high-quality meta-analyses exist for resveratrol's anti-inflammatory effects (Gorabi 2021: 35 RCTs; Molani-Gol 2024: 81 RCTs umbrella), supported by mechanistic RCTs. The umbrella meta-analysis represents the highest-level synthesis available. However, Tier 1 is not reached because:
1. Findings are inconsistent across all claimed domains (SIRT1 null, bone null, lipid null)
2. The most replicated benefits (CRP reduction, body composition) are modest in effect size (SMD ~0.3-0.5 range)
3. Cognitive effects, while replicated in 3 RCTs, lack independent meta-analytic confirmation with large samples
4. Clinical significance of anti-inflammatory effects remains debated (CRP reductions clinically meaningful?)
5. No Tier 1-level cognitive evidence (comparable to Bacopa Monnieri's memory meta-analyses)

**Previous Tier 3 justification review:** The database had evidenceTier: 3 with only 1 keyCitation (Tseilikman 2024 — anxiety RCT). This underrepresented the actual evidence base. The tier upgrade to 2 reflects the true weight of evidence from 16 synthesis papers and 4 direct RCTs.

### Research Maturity: Developing

**Justification:**
Resveratrol has a substantial clinical research base (81+ RCTs in umbrella meta-analysis), but the field is still clarifying:
1. Which mechanisms actually drive the observed effects (SIRT1 hypothesis falsified in humans)
2. Whether anti-inflammatory effects are clinically meaningful vs. statistically significant only
3. Optimal dosing for different outcomes (cognitive vs. metabolic vs. vascular)
4. Long-term safety at higher doses (>500mg/day for extended periods)
5. Population specificity (T2D, obese, postmenopausal may respond differently)

Not "Mature" because mechanistic understanding was recently revised (SIRT1 null 2025) and clinical significance remains under active debate. Not "Emerging" because the evidence base is substantial enough to support Tier 2 classification.

---

### Evidence Gaps Identified

1. **Cognitive outcomes:** Only 3 individual RCTs; no dedicated meta-analysis of resveratrol-specific cognitive outcomes (de Vries 2022 is polyphenol-class SR); Thaung Zaw 2021 is the only long-term cognitive RCT
2. **Mechanism clarification post-SIRT1:** If not SIRT1, what is the primary mechanism in humans? NF-κB and eNOS evidence indirect; no well-powered RCT measuring these biomarkers as primary endpoints
3. **Dose-response characterization:** No published dose-response meta-analysis for cognitive or inflammatory outcomes; Kennedy 2010 acute dose-response small (n=22)
4. **Long-term safety >12 months:** No published RCT or meta-analysis specifically addressing >12-month safety; Thaung Zaw 2021 (24 months) is the longest but not safety-powered
5. **Male populations:** Many meta-analyses disproportionately include female or mixed populations; male-specific effects understudied
6. **ALP elevation clarification:** Soltani 2023 identifies ALP elevation at >1000mg/day but specific threshold and mechanism unclear
7. **Bioavailability enhancement:** Impact of formulation (micronized, liposomal, pterostilbene combination) on efficacy not systematically reviewed in the clinical literature

---

### Tier Stability Assessment

**Current Tier 2 is stable** given the existing evidence base. Movement to Tier 1 would require:
- A dedicated cognitive meta-analysis with consistent, large-effect results (≥5 RCTs, n>500)
- Resolution of the dose-response question with a well-powered RCT
- Mechanistic clarity post-SIRT1 null finding

Movement back to Tier 3 would require:
- A large high-quality RCT directly contradicting anti-inflammatory meta-analytic results
- Evidence of systematic methodological flaws in the 35-RCT meta-analysis (Gorabi 2021)

Neither is likely in the near term.
