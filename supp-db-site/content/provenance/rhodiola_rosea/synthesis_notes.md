### Key Evidence Updates

#### Update 1: Physical Performance -- Domain Upgrade Driven by 26-RCT Meta-Analysis
- **Source:** wang_2025_endurance_performance (PMID 41080184)
- **Previous state:** Physical performance effects documented by individual RCTs (De Bock 2004, Noreen 2013, Parisi 2010) and smaller reviews. Evidence rated as Moderate with no comprehensive meta-analytic quantification.
- **New evidence:**
  - Wang 2025: Systematic review + meta-analysis of 26 RCTs (668 participants) across multiple exercise performance domains:
    - VO2 (aerobic capacity): ES=0.32 (p<0.01) -- small-to-moderate improvement
    - Time to exhaustion (TTE): ES=0.38 -- moderate improvement in endurance capacity
    - Time trial performance (TTP): ES=-0.40 -- moderate improvement in paced exercise output
    - Total antioxidant capacity (TAC): ES=0.59 -- moderate-to-large increase
    - Superoxide dismutase (SOD): ES=1.16 -- large upregulation of endogenous antioxidant enzyme
    - Creatine kinase (CK): ES=-0.84 -- large reduction in muscle damage marker
    - Blood lactate: ES=-0.87 -- large reduction in metabolic stress marker
  - Dose-response: >600mg/day shows greater VO2 improvement compared to lower doses
- **Evidence strength change:** Moderate -> Strong
- **Synthesis notes:** This is the most significant evidence advancement for Rhodiola rosea in this update cycle. The Wang 2025 meta-analysis transforms the exercise performance evidence from a collection of individual RCTs into a quantified, meta-analytically supported evidence domain. The effect size pattern reveals an important mechanistic insight: while direct performance metrics show modest improvements (VO2 ES=0.32, TTE ES=0.38, TTP ES=-0.40), the recovery and antioxidant markers show substantially larger effects (SOD ES=1.16, CK ES=-0.84, lactate ES=-0.87). This asymmetry suggests that R. rosea's primary exercise benefit pathway may be through enhanced antioxidant defense and accelerated recovery rather than acute ergogenic performance enhancement. The dose-response finding (>600mg/day for greater VO2 improvement) is clinically actionable: most commercial R. rosea extracts are dosed at 200-600mg/day, suggesting that the upper end of the dosing range may be needed for optimal exercise performance benefits. This meta-analysis provides the strongest individual piece of evidence for R. rosea in the current update and is the primary driver of the quality score improvement from 87 to 89.
- **Database impact:** Physical Performance evidence upgraded from Moderate to Strong. Antioxidant and Recovery domains upgraded to Moderate. Dose-response framework (>600mg/day) incorporated for exercise performance claims.

#### Update 2: Depression -- Strengthened by Network Meta-Analysis
- **Source:** urata_2025_milder_depression (PMID 40014460)
- **Previous state:** Depression effects documented in individual RCTs (Darbinyan 2007, Mao 2015) but without meta-analytic synthesis. Evidence rated as Low.
- **New evidence:**
  - Urata 2025: Network meta-analysis of 8 RCTs (1049 participants total across multiple interventions) evaluating pharmacological interventions for milder depression. R. rosea demonstrated significant improvements in depressive symptoms vs placebo within the network comparison.
- **Evidence strength change:** Low -> Low-Moderate
- **Synthesis notes:** The Urata 2025 NMA provides the first meta-analytic-level evidence for R. rosea's antidepressant effects. The NMA design enables indirect comparisons between R. rosea and other interventions (including pharmaceutical agents and other supplements), providing relative efficacy context that individual RCTs cannot offer. However, the upgrade is conservative (Low to Low-Moderate rather than Moderate) because: (1) R. rosea is one of several agents in the NMA, not the primary focus of investigation; (2) the R. rosea-specific sample within the NMA is smaller than the total 1049-participant pool; and (3) a dedicated R. rosea pairwise meta-analysis for depression would provide stronger evidence than the NMA approach. The finding is nonetheless important because it corroborates the prior RCT-level evidence (Darbinyan 2007, Mao 2015) and places R. rosea within the broader pharmacological landscape for milder depression. The monoaminergic mechanism (MAO inhibition, serotonin modulation) provides biological plausibility for the antidepressant effect.
- **Database impact:** Depression/Mood evidence upgraded from Low to Low-Moderate. NMA context (relative comparison to other interventions) noted. Appropriate caveats about multi-supplement NMA design maintained.

#### Update 3: Salidroside Mechanism -- Clarified by Isolated Compound RCT
- **Source:** schwarz_2024_salidroside_exercise (PMID 39601362)
- **Previous state:** Salidroside identified as a key bioactive glycoside in R. rosea along with rosavin, but mechanism not isolated from whole-extract effects. The relative contributions of salidroside vs. rosavin vs. other constituents were unclear.
- **New evidence:**
  - Schwarz 2024: Double-blind RCT (n=50) using pure biosynthetic salidroside at 60mg/day. Enhanced O2 utilization during HIIT (p<0.01) and reduced muscle damage markers. Demonstrates that salidroside alone can produce the exercise performance and recovery effects observed with whole R. rosea extracts.
- **Evidence strength change:** Low -> Moderate (mechanism domain)
- **Synthesis notes:** This study provides critical mechanistic evidence by isolating salidroside from the complex phytochemical matrix of R. rosea whole extract. The use of biosynthetic salidroside (not extracted from R. rosea) eliminates confounding from other R. rosea constituents (rosavin, rosarin, rosin, tyrosol, flavonoids), demonstrating that salidroside alone is sufficient to enhance O2 utilization and reduce muscle damage. This has two implications: (1) salidroside is confirmed as a (if not the) primary active compound for exercise performance effects, and (2) salidroside content should be a key quality marker for R. rosea extract standardization. However, the dose indirectness is a significant limitation: 60mg/day of pure salidroside is 10-30x higher than what typical R. rosea supplements deliver (standard extracts contain 1-3% salidroside, yielding ~2-18mg salidroside per 200-600mg dose). Whether the effects observed at 60mg translate to the lower salidroside concentrations in whole extracts is not established by this study. The Wang 2025 meta-analysis (which evaluates whole R. rosea extract) provides indirect evidence that lower salidroside doses are pharmacologically active, but a formal dose-bridging study would strengthen this connection.
- **Database impact:** Salidroside mechanism section updated. Key bioactive compound identification confirmed. Dose indirectness caveat documented. Cross-reference with Wang 2025 for whole-extract validation noted.

### Cross-Domain Integration
The 3 included studies collectively illuminate R. rosea's evidence profile across interconnected physiological systems:

- **Stress -> Cognitive -> Physical Performance -> Recovery pathway:** R. rosea's adaptogenic classification rests on its ability to modulate the stress response, which in turn affects cognitive function, exercise capacity, and post-exercise recovery. The Wang 2025 meta-analysis provides the strongest evidence for the downstream end of this pathway (physical performance and recovery), while the stress and cognitive components remain supported by older individual RCTs. The Urata 2025 NMA adds depression as a parallel domain, connecting R. rosea's mood effects to its broader adaptogenic activity.

- **Exercise performance as the strongest evidence domain:** The Wang 2025 meta-analysis (26 RCTs, 668 participants) makes physical performance R. rosea's best-supported benefit domain. The effect size pattern (modest performance gains + large recovery/antioxidant effects) suggests that R. rosea may be better characterized as a "recovery enhancer with modest ergogenic properties" rather than a pure performance booster. This reframing has implications for how R. rosea is positioned relative to other exercise supplements.

- **Depression as an emerging evidence domain:** The Urata 2025 NMA positions R. rosea within the pharmacological landscape for milder depression. While the evidence is preliminary (NMA, not pairwise MA), it corroborates prior RCT-level findings and is consistent with R. rosea's known monoaminergic activity (MAO-A/MAO-B inhibition, serotonin and dopamine modulation).

- **Salidroside as the mechanistic anchor:** Schwarz 2024 clarifies that salidroside is a (if not the) primary active compound for exercise performance effects. This has implications for extract standardization: R. rosea products should ideally be standardized to salidroside content (alongside the traditional rosavin standardization). The dual standardization approach (rosavin + salidroside) would better predict clinical efficacy.

### Mechanistic Integration Model
The new evidence allows refinement of R. rosea's integrated mechanistic framework:

```
Rhodiola rosea Extract (salidroside + rosavin + phenylpropanoids)
    |
    +-> HPA Axis Modulation (cortisol regulation, stress response normalization)
    |       |
    |       +-> Stress Reduction (supported by prior RCTs; no new MA)
    |       +-> Fatigue Resistance (supported by prior RCTs; no new MA)
    |       +-> Cognitive Function under stress (supported by prior RCTs)
    |
    +-> Salidroside-Mediated Pathways (Schwarz 2024 mechanism confirmed)
    |       |
    |       +-> Enhanced O2 Utilization (VO2 ES=0.32, Wang 2025 MA)
    |       +-> Mitochondrial ATP Production (preclinical evidence)
    |       +-> Antioxidant Enzyme Upregulation (SOD ES=1.16, Wang 2025 MA)
    |       +-> Muscle Damage Reduction (CK ES=-0.84, Wang 2025 MA)
    |       +-> Lactate Clearance (ES=-0.87, Wang 2025 MA)
    |
    +-> Monoamine Regulation (MAO-A/MAO-B inhibition)
    |       |
    |       +-> Serotonin / Dopamine Modulation
    |       +-> Depression Improvement (Urata 2025 NMA)
    |       +-> Mood Stabilization
    |
    +-> Rosavin-Mediated Pathways
            |
            +-> Adaptogenic Activity (synergy with salidroside)
            +-> Anxiolytic Effects (mechanism less characterized than salidroside)
```

This model demonstrates that R. rosea's clinical effects emerge from at least three upstream mechanisms: HPA axis modulation (stress/fatigue), salidroside-mediated antioxidant/mitochondrial activity (exercise performance/recovery), and monoamine regulation (mood/depression). The salidroside pathway is now the best-characterized thanks to Schwarz 2024 and Wang 2025, while the HPA axis and monoamine pathways remain supported primarily by older evidence.

### Limitations of This Synthesis
1. The physical performance domain is well-supported by Wang 2025 (26 RCTs), but stress and fatigue -- R. rosea's primary consumer use cases -- lack meta-analytic quantification in the 2024-2026 window. This creates an evidence asymmetry where the strongest evidence exists for a secondary use case
2. Urata 2025 NMA provides depression evidence but R. rosea is embedded within a multi-supplement network. R. rosea-specific effect sizes cannot be cleanly extracted from the NMA design
3. Schwarz 2024 uses pure biosynthetic salidroside at 60mg/day (10-30x typical R. rosea extract salidroside content). The dose indirectness limits extrapolation to whole-extract supplementation, though Wang 2025 provides indirect validation that whole-extract doses are pharmacologically active
4. R. rosea extract standardization varies across studies and products: some standardize to 3% rosavin + 1% salidroside, others to different ratios. This standardization heterogeneity introduces uncertainty in comparing across studies
5. The overall evidence volume for R. rosea (26 RCTs in the largest MA) is smaller than for heavily researched adaptogens like ashwagandha (50+ RCTs). The smaller evidence base increases vulnerability to future negative findings shifting the evidence balance
6. No safety-specific meta-analysis was identified. Safety data is derived from adverse event reporting in efficacy studies, which may undercount mild adverse effects
7. Long-term use data (>12 weeks continuous supplementation) remains limited. Most RCTs evaluate acute or short-term supplementation protocols
