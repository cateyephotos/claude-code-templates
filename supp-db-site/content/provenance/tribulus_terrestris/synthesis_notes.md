# Synthesis Notes: Tribulus Terrestris (ID: 35)
## Pipeline Run: 2026-03-06 | Mode: Evidence Update (Mode 2)

---

## 1. Mechanism Synthesis

### 1.1 Steroidal Saponin Activity (Protodioscin-Mediated)

**Source studies:** Zhang 2022 (PMID 35212473)
**GRADE Certainty: Moderate** (pharmacological mechanism well-documented; supplementation translation uncertain)

Tribulus terrestris contains steroidal saponins, primarily protodioscin, which is proposed as the primary bioactive compound. The saponin fraction is thought to modulate luteinizing hormone (LH) secretion and downstream steroidogenic enzyme activity, providing the mechanistic basis for testosterone-related marketing claims. Zhang 2022 (comprehensive phytochemistry review, Chemistry & Biodiversity) documents saponin content, isolation, and pharmacological activity.

**Synthesis judgment:** The saponin mechanism is biochemically plausible and is supported by in vitro and animal model data. However, the critical translational finding — from saponin pharmacology to clinically meaningful testosterone elevation in healthy human males — is **refuted by the highest-quality clinical evidence** (Chhatre 2014 meta-analysis, 12 studies, no significant effect in healthy men). The mechanism exists; the clinical translation does not hold for the primary marketed claim.

**Confidence:** Moderate for the mechanism itself. Very Low for translation to testosterone benefits in healthy adults.

---

### 1.2 Nitric Oxide and Endothelial Function (eNOS Pathway)

**Source studies:** Santos 2021 (PMID 34101274)
**GRADE Certainty: Moderate** (eNOS pathway biologically established; clinical benefit for vascular health and erectile function uncertain)

Santos 2021 (Phytotherapy Research) documents enhanced endothelial nitric oxide synthase (eNOS) activity and improved vascular relaxation in mechanistic study conditions. This pathway provides a plausible mechanism for potential cardiovascular and erectile function benefits — the nitric oxide pathway is central to vascular health and is the target of established pharmaceutical interventions (PDE5 inhibitors).

**Synthesis judgment:** The eNOS mechanism is credible and biologically relevant. This is a stronger mechanistic rationale for sexual function and cardiovascular benefits than the androgen pathway, but clinical validation in human RCTs is limited. The cardiovascular review (Adaikan 2005) is dated (2005) and relies on mixed experimental/clinical evidence.

**Confidence:** Moderate for the eNOS mechanism. Low for clinical cardiovascular and sexual function benefits attributed specifically to this pathway.

---

### 1.3 Antioxidant and Anti-Inflammatory Activity

**Source studies:** Kianbakht 2020 (PMID 32504801)
**GRADE Certainty: Moderate** (antioxidant activity is broad and consistent; supplement-specific clinical significance uncertain)

Kianbakht 2020 (Journal of Ethnopharmacology) documents antioxidant and hepatoprotective effects with cytokine modulation. This is the most general mechanism and applies broadly to many botanical extracts.

**Synthesis judgment:** Antioxidant activity is well-documented but is not a supplement-specific differentiator. The clinical significance for any specific health outcome claim is limited by its non-specificity.

**Confidence:** Moderate for antioxidant mechanism. Low for specific clinical outcomes attributable to this mechanism alone.

---

### Mechanism Synthesis Summary

| Mechanism | GRADE | Plausibility | Clinical Translation | Notes |
|-----------|-------|--------------|---------------------|-------|
| Saponin/LH/testosterone pathway | Moderate | High | **Refuted (null meta-analysis)** | Best mechanism evidence; worst clinical translation |
| eNOS/nitric oxide/vascular | Moderate | High | Possible (limited clinical data) | Relevant to sexual function and cardiovascular |
| Antioxidant/anti-inflammatory | Moderate | High | Non-specific; general botanical effect | Not a differentiating claim |

**Overall mechanism GRADE: Moderate** — mechanisms are plausible and documented, but the critical translational gap between the primary marketed mechanism (saponin → testosterone) and clinical outcomes is the defining feature of the Tribulus evidence base.

## 2. Clinical Benefit Synthesis

### 2.1 Testosterone Enhancement and Hormonal Effects in Healthy Men

**Source studies:** Chhatre 2014 (PMID 24480665) — meta-analysis, 12 studies
**GRADE Certainty: Moderate** (for the null finding — no testosterone effect in healthy men)
**Indication strength: Absent/Refuted**

Chhatre 2014 (International Journal of Sport Nutrition and Exercise Metabolism) conducted a meta-analysis of 12 studies examining testosterone levels, body composition, and exercise performance. The primary finding was **no significant effects on testosterone levels in healthy men**, with inconsistent results for strength and body composition.

**This is the highest-quality positive evidence in the portfolio — and it is null.** The meta-analytic design (12 studies), peer-reviewed publication in the field's leading journal, consistent null results (low inconsistency GRADE), and direct relevance to the primary marketed claim combine to make this the anchor evidence for Tier 3 assignment.

**Critical synthesis note:** This finding does not preclude effects in populations with clinical hypogonadism or hormonal deficiency, but in the healthy adult population representing the primary supplement market, testosterone enhancement is not supported.

**Confidence for claim "Tribulus raises testosterone in healthy men": Very Low** (claim is directly contradicted by best available evidence)

---

### 2.2 Sexual Function and Libido

**Source studies:** Kamenov 2017 (PMID 28364864) — systematic review, 11 studies, n=516
**GRADE Certainty: Low** (systematic methodology; heterogeneous populations; modest effect sizes; publication bias suspected)
**Indication strength: Limited (Modest)**

Kamenov 2017 (Maturitas) — the best available positive clinical evidence — found modest improvements in sexual desire and arousal, with effects more pronounced in women than men. The review included 11 studies with 516 participants across heterogeneous populations and indications.

**Synthesis judgment:** This is the strongest positive clinical claim that survives evidence scrutiny, but it is modest in magnitude, inconsistent across populations, and benefits women more than men. The female population finding (menopausal women, sexual dysfunction) has somewhat better evidence than the male population finding. The effect size for men with normal testosterone is limited.

**Evidence limitations:**
- Heterogeneous study populations (different patient types, ages, conditions)
- Effect more pronounced in women → generic male testosterone supplement marketing is inappropriate
- Sample sizes modest (n=516 across 11 studies)
- Publication bias suspected in herbal supplement systematic reviews
- No large pre-registered RCT

**Confidence for claim "Tribulus modestly improves sexual function (especially in women)": Low** (best available positive evidence; methodological limitations)

---

### 2.3 Sports Performance and Body Composition

**Source studies:** Chhatre 2014 (PMID 24480665) — meta-analysis, 12 studies (same source as 2.1)
**GRADE Certainty: Moderate** (null finding, well-established)
**Indication strength: Absent/Refuted**

Consistent with the testosterone null finding, Chhatre 2014 found limited improvements in strength and no significant effects on body composition across 12 studies.

**Synthesis judgment:** Sports performance benefits are not supported by meta-analytic evidence. This is consistent with the testosterone null finding — if testosterone is not elevated, the primary proposed mechanism for ergogenic effects is absent.

**Confidence for claim "Tribulus improves sports performance": Very Low** (null meta-analysis)

---

### 2.4 Cardiovascular Health

**Source studies:** Adaikan 2005 (PMID 16252298) — review
**GRADE Certainty: Low** (dated 2005 review; mixed experimental/clinical evidence; animal data included)
**Indication strength: Limited**

The cardiovascular evidence is largely mechanistic (eNOS pathway) and supported by a 2005 review relying on mixed experimental and clinical data. No post-2010 high-quality human clinical trial specifically addresses cardiovascular outcomes.

**Synthesis judgment:** The vascular mechanism (eNOS/nitric oxide) is credible, but the clinical evidence for cardiovascular health benefits in supplementation populations is insufficient and dated.

**Confidence for cardiovascular claims: Low** (mechanistic plausibility; no modern RCT)

---

### 2.5 Urinary Health

**Source studies:** Al-Ali 2003 (PMID 12639749) — preclinical study
**GRADE Certainty: Very Low** (preclinical/animal model; no human RCT)
**Indication strength: Very Limited (traditional use only)**

The only evidence for urinary health benefits (nephroprotective, diuretic effects) comes from a 2003 preclinical study. This claim is supported by traditional Ayurvedic use (Gokshura = urinary tonic in traditional Indian medicine) and preclinical pharmacology but lacks human clinical validation.

**Synthesis judgment:** Traditional use documentation only. Not sufficient for clinical efficacy claims.

**Confidence for urinary health claims: Very Low** (preclinical evidence only)

---

### 2.6 Metabolic Health (Glucose and Lipid Effects)

**Source studies:** Li 2012 (PMID 22006891) — animal study (diabetic rats)
**GRADE Certainty: Very Low** (animal study; two very-serious downgrade factors)
**Indication strength: Absent (no human evidence)**

The only metabolic evidence is an animal study in diabetic rats. This is retained in the evidence base as documentation of the evidence gap, not as positive clinical evidence.

**Synthesis judgment:** Metabolic claims have no human clinical support. The animal model finding may justify future clinical investigation but does not support marketing claims.

**Confidence for metabolic claims: Very Low** (animal study only)

## 3. Safety Synthesis

### 3.1 General Tolerability

**Source studies:** Neychev 2016 (PMID 27823629) — systematic safety review
**GRADE Certainty: Moderate** (systematic methodology; consistent tolerability profile)

Neychev 2016 (Phytomedicine) reviewed available clinical studies and found Tribulus to be generally well tolerated in healthy adults, with rare reports of stomach upset (typically at higher doses) and occasional sleep disturbances. No serious adverse events were documented in short-term studies.

**Synthesis judgment:** The tolerability profile is well-characterized and provides reasonable confidence in short-term safety for healthy adults.

---

### 3.2 Hormonal Safety and Contraindications

**Source studies:** Rogerson 2017 (PMID 28669767) — clinical review
**GRADE Certainty: Moderate** (hormonal safety signal well-characterized)

Rogerson 2017 (Journal of Ethnopharmacology) identified potential interactions with hormone-sensitive conditions and drug-drug interactions with diabetes medications and blood pressure medications. Caution is warranted in pregnant and breastfeeding women, persons with hormone-sensitive conditions (hormone-dependent cancers, endocrine disorders), and those taking relevant medications.

**Synthesis judgment:** The hormonal caution flag is the most important safety consideration for Tribulus beyond general tolerability. The mechanism (saponin → steroidogenic enzyme modulation) that underpins the testosterone claim simultaneously creates the hormonal caution.

---

### 3.3 Long-Term Safety

**Source studies:** Deepak 2004 (PMID 14741877) — toxicology study
**GRADE Certainty: Moderate** (acute safety established; long-term data gap documented)

Deepak 2004 confirmed no acute toxicity in their toxicological evaluation but noted the limitation of limited long-term human safety data. This gap has not been filled by subsequent studies.

**Synthesis judgment:** Acute and short-term safety is reasonably well-established. Long-term safety data remain limited, supporting the cycling approach (6–8 weeks on, 2–4 weeks off) as a prudent precautionary protocol.

---

### Safety Synthesis Summary

## 4. Dosage Synthesis

**Basis:** Synthesized from doses used in the clinical trials reviewed by Kamenov 2017, Chhatre 2014, and related studies. No dedicated pharmacokinetic RCT met inclusion criteria.

### Clinical Dosing Evidence

| Indication | Dose Range Used in Studies | Administration | Notes |
|-----------|---------------------------|----------------|-------|
| Sexual function | 250–750mg/day | Divided 2–3 doses with meals | Kamenov 2017 pooled from 11 studies |
| Sports performance (failed) | 750–1500mg/day | Various | Chhatre 2014; no efficacy regardless of dose |
| General | 250–750mg/day (standardized extract, 40–45% saponins) | With meals, morning/afternoon preferred | Avoid evening dosing (sleep disturbance reports) |

### Standardization Note
Saponin content (40–45% steroidal saponins, expressed as protodioscin equivalents) is the primary quality marker. Raw powder doses without standardization are not comparable to standardized extract doses.

### Cycling Recommendation
Given the limited long-term safety data and the mechanism involving potential steroidogenic modulation, a cycling protocol (6–8 weeks on, 2–4 weeks off) is the standard clinical recommendation documented across safety-oriented reviews.

**Dosage GRADE: Evidence-based** (derived from pooled clinical trial doses; no dedicated PK study)

## 6. Evidence Gaps

1. **No large pre-registered RCT for any indication:** The evidence base consists of older studies (many pre-2010), heterogeneous small trials, and meta-analyses of these small trials. No single gold-standard study exists.

2. **Testosterone null finding not stratified by subpopulation:** Chhatre 2014 examined healthy men. Effects in men with clinical hypogonadism, older men with declining androgen levels, or other specific populations are inadequately studied.

3. **Female sexual dysfunction evidence gap:** Kamenov 2017 shows more pronounced effects in women, but the effect is derived from heterogeneous studies. No dedicated large RCT in female sexual dysfunction exists.

4. **Long-term safety beyond 8 weeks:** All clinical studies are short-term (typically 4–8 weeks). No long-term safety data for continuous use.

5. **Metabolic and urinary claims have no human clinical data:** Both Li 2012 (metabolic) and Al-Ali 2003 (urinary) are preclinical studies. Human RCTs for these indications are entirely absent.

6. **Dose-response characterization is absent:** No pharmacokinetic study characterizing bioavailability, half-life, or dose-response relationships for protodioscin in humans exists in the included evidence base.

7. **Biogeographic source variation:** Saponin content varies significantly by plant source (Bulgarian, Indian, Chinese origin material differs in protodioscin concentration). No included study adequately addresses this confound.

## 7. Overall Synthesis Confidence Statement

**Synthesis Confidence: Moderate** (for the synthesis itself — the evidence base is adequately characterized)
**Evidence Quality: Low–Moderate overall** (average study quality 6.0/10; GRADE Low–Moderate)

The Tribulus terrestris evidence base presents an unusual profile: the highest-quality evidence (Chhatre 2014 meta-analysis, Moderate GRADE, 8/10 quality) is a null finding that directly refutes the primary marketed claim. This creates an evidence base where marketing claims exceed the evidentiary support by the widest margin among supplements in this database cohort.

The supplement is not without documented activity — the saponin mechanism is pharmacologically real, modest sexual function benefits (primarily in women) survive scrutiny at a Low GRADE level, and the safety profile is relatively well-characterized. However, the primary claims (testosterone enhancement, sports performance) are unsupported by the best available evidence.

**Assigned overall GRADE: Low (clinical benefits) / Moderate (mechanisms and safety)**
**Assigned Tier: 3 (Limited Evidence)**
**ResearchQualityScore: 65/100**

The 65/100 score reflects: moderate mechanism evidence (saponin pharmacology real but clinically non-translating for primary claim), limited clinical benefit evidence (null finding dominant, modest secondary findings), well-characterized safety (reduces uncertainty but doesn't increase efficacy evidence), and good evidence recency for mechanism studies (2020–2022) but dated clinical studies (primary trials 2014–2017, no post-2020 efficacy data).
