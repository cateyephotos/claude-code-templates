## Tier Decision Tree

### Step 1: Does strong, consistent evidence from large-N RCTs or meta-analyses of high-quality RCTs support the primary claimed benefit?

**Primary claimed benefit:** Testosterone enhancement and sports performance in healthy adults.

**Answer: NO — the evidence base is dominated by a null finding.**

- Chhatre 2014 (PMID 24480665): Meta-analysis of 12 studies — **no significant effects on testosterone levels in healthy men**; limited improvements in strength or body composition; inconsistent results
- Kamenov 2017 (PMID 28364864): Systematic review confirms "limited evidence for testosterone enhancement in healthy individuals"
- No large pre-registered RCT exists demonstrating positive testosterone effects in healthy adults

**→ Tier 1 excluded at Step 1.** The primary marketed claim is refuted by the best available evidence.

---

### Step 2: Does a Cochrane review or high-quality systematic review conclude evidence is insufficient or mixed?

**Answer: YES — multiple systematic reviews identify insufficient or null evidence for primary claims.**

- Chhatre 2014 (meta-analysis): No significant testosterone or performance effects — null finding
- Kamenov 2017 (systematic review): Mixed results; modest sexual function benefit only in some populations; inconsistent across indications
- No Cochrane review specifically for Tribulus testosterone claims, but the meta-analytic evidence is functionally equivalent

**→ Tier 1 further excluded at Step 2. Tier 2 consideration proceeds.**

---

### Step 3: Is there a substantial body of multiple RCTs demonstrating consistent moderate-sized effects for at least one important indication?

**Answer: NO — the positive clinical evidence is too limited for Tier 2.**

**Evidence assessment:**
- Sexual function (Kamenov 2017): One systematic review, 11 small studies, n=516, modest and heterogeneous effects — insufficient volume and consistency for Tier 2
- No indication has multiple large independent RCTs demonstrating consistent moderate effects
- The most consistent finding across the portfolio is the null finding (testosterone/sports performance)
- No dedicated pharmacokinetic study; no dose-response characterization; no large pre-registered RCT

**Tier 2 requires:** Multiple RCTs with consistent results for at least one well-defined indication at adequate statistical power.

**→ Tier 2 excluded at Step 3.**

---

### Step 4: Is there plausible mechanism AND at least some preliminary human clinical evidence for at least one indication?

**Answer: YES — meets Tier 3 criteria.**

**Positive Tier 3 criteria met:**
1. ✅ Plausible pharmacological mechanism: Steroidal saponins (protodioscin) — documented in peer-reviewed phytochemistry review (Zhang 2022); eNOS pathway documented (Santos 2021); antioxidant activity (Kianbakht 2020)
2. ✅ Some preliminary human clinical evidence: Kamenov 2017 systematic review (11 studies, n=516) — modest sexual function benefits, especially in women
3. ✅ Well-characterized safety profile: Neychev 2016 systematic safety review; Rogerson 2017 hormonal safety; Deepak 2004 toxicology
4. ✅ Established research history: 2002–2022 publication span; academic research base; multiple review articles

**→ Tier 3 assigned.**

## Mandatory Safety Disclosure

**Required for all supplements with hormonal mechanism:**

Tribulus terrestris has a saponin-mediated mechanism with potential steroidogenic enzyme modulation. The following safety considerations must be prominently communicated:

1. **Hormone-sensitive populations:** Persons with hormone-sensitive conditions (hormone-dependent cancers, PCOS, endocrine disorders) should not use Tribulus without medical supervision
2. **Drug interactions:** May interact with diabetes medications (blood glucose effects) and blood pressure medications (hypotensive effects); physician consultation required
3. **Pregnancy and breastfeeding:** Use is contraindicated
4. **Long-term use:** Long-term safety data beyond 8 weeks are lacking; cycling approach recommended (6–8 weeks on, 2–4 weeks off)
