#### Study: marti_carvajal_2017_cvd — "Homocysteine-lowering interventions for preventing cardiovascular events"

- **PMID:** 28816346 | **DOI:** 10.1002/14651858.CD006612.pub5
- **Year:** 2017
- **GRADE domains:**
  - Risk of bias: Low — Cochrane methodology; standardized risk of bias assessments across included trials
  - Inconsistency: Not serious for homocysteine reduction; serious for cardiovascular event reduction (inconsistent benefit)
  - Indirectness: Not serious — human RCTs with clinical cardiovascular endpoints
  - Imprecision: Not serious — large pooled analysis
  - Publication bias: Low — Cochrane includes unpublished/negative trials
- **Overall GRADE certainty:** High for homocysteine reduction; Moderate for cardiovascular event prevention (inconsistent CVD benefit)
- **Assigned evidence level:** Level 1
- **Key findings:** B vitamins (including B12) significantly reduce homocysteine; CVD event reduction not consistently demonstrated despite homocysteine lowering
- **Note:** Demonstrates surrogate endpoint (homocysteine) ≠ clinical endpoint (CVD events), highlighting the nuance in interpreting B12 cardiovascular claims
- **Quality score contribution:** 9/10 — Cochrane gold standard; highest methodological rigor available

#### Study: volkov_2009_stomatitis — "Effectiveness of vitamin B12 in treating recurrent aphthous stomatitis"
- **Study type:** RCT (Journal of the American Board of Family Medicine, IF ~2.8)
- **PMID:** 19124628 | **DOI:** 10.3122/jabfm.2009.01.080113
- **Year:** 2009
- **GRADE domains:**
  - Risk of bias: Some concerns — relatively small sample; sublingual B12 only; treatment duration
  - Inconsistency: Not serious — single RCT for this specific indication
  - Indirectness: Not serious — directly tests B12 vs placebo for aphthous stomatitis in unselected patients (regardless of B12 status)
  - Imprecision: Serious — single small RCT; no replication in larger trials
  - Publication bias: Possible — positive result in lower-impact journal
- **Overall GRADE certainty:** Low (single RCT, limited sample, lower journal impact)
- **Assigned evidence level:** Level 2
- **Key findings:** Sublingual B12 1000 mcg/day significantly reduced ulcer attacks vs placebo in patients with recurrent aphthous stomatitis regardless of initial B12 status
- **Note:** Unusual finding — B12 supplementation appears beneficial for aphthous stomatitis even in patients with normal B12 levels, suggesting mechanism beyond simple repletion
- **Quality score contribution:** 5/10 — only RCT for this indication; replicated outcomes would strengthen substantially
