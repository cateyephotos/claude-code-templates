#### Study: wyatt_1999_pms — "Efficacy of vitamin B6 in the treatment of premenstrual syndrome: systematic review"
- **Study type:** Systematic review and meta-analysis (BMJ, IF ~105+)
- **PMID:** 10334745 | **DOI:** 10.1136/bmj.318.7195.1375
- **Year:** 1999
- **GRADE domains:**
  - Risk of bias: Some concerns — included RCTs varied in quality (1970s–1990s); publication bias possible; pooled 9 trials with heterogeneous populations and dosing
  - Inconsistency: Moderate — some heterogeneity across included trials; overall direction consistent (benefit)
  - Indirectness: Not serious — women with PMS receiving B6 supplementation; directly applicable
  - Imprecision: Not serious — pooled analysis with CIs reported; n sufficient for primary conclusion
  - Publication bias: Possible — predominantly positive trials published in the 1980s–90s
- **Overall GRADE certainty:** Moderate (meta-analysis of 9 RCTs; BMJ; some heterogeneity)
- **Assigned evidence level:** Level 1
- **Key findings:** Vitamin B6 (at doses up to 100 mg/day) significantly more likely to relieve overall PMS symptoms vs placebo; OR 2.32 (95% CI 1.95–2.54); premenstrual depression specific benefit also shown
- **Note:** Methodological quality of included RCTs moderate; authors note trial quality limitations; still the primary meta-analytic evidence for this indication
- **Quality score contribution:** 8/10 — BMJ high impact; systematic methodology; largest available evidence synthesis for PMS indication; some limitations from older trial quality

#### Study: schaumburg_1983_neuropathy — "Sensory neuropathy from pyridoxine abuse: a new megavitamin syndrome"
- **Study type:** Case series / original research (New England Journal of Medicine, IF ~176+)
- **PMID:** 6308447 | **DOI:** 10.1056/NEJM198308253090801
- **Year:** 1983
- **GRADE domains:**
  - Risk of bias: Some concerns — case series design; ascertainment bias possible; observational
  - Inconsistency: Not serious — consistent with subsequent reports of the same syndrome
  - Indirectness: Not serious — directly documents peripheral neuropathy in humans taking high-dose B6
  - Imprecision: Not serious — clinical findings clear; dose-response relationship documented
- **Overall GRADE certainty:** Moderate (case series; NEJM; landmark status; has not been formally refuted)
- **Assigned evidence level:** Level 2
- **Key findings:** Seven patients developed severe sensory neuropathy after taking 2–6 grams of pyridoxine daily for months to years; all had unsteady gait, numb extremities, and abnormal nerve conduction; recovery on withdrawal; dose-response relationship suggested
- **Note:** This is the foundational safety paper establishing the high-dose neuropathy risk; published in NEJM; despite being a case series, its findings have been widely replicated in subsequent reports and accepted as established clinical knowledge
- **Quality score contribution:** 7/10 — NEJM landmark publication; foundational safety evidence; case series design limits score
