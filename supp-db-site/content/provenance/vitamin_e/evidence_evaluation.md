### Per-Study Evaluation

#### Study: stampfer_1993_cohort — PMID 8479463
- **Citation:** Stampfer MJ et al. N Engl J Med. 1993;328(20):1444-9
- **Study type:** Prospective cohort, n=87,245 female nurses, 8 years follow-up
- **GRADE domains:**
  - Risk of bias: **Serious** — observational design; confounding by healthy user bias (supplement users are healthier overall); no randomization
  - Inconsistency: **Not applicable** — single cohort
  - Indirectness: **Serious** — association ≠ causation; RCTs subsequently demonstrated no CVD benefit
  - Imprecision: **Not serious** — large n; narrow confidence intervals
  - Publication bias: **Low** — NEJM publication; NIH funded
- **Overall GRADE certainty:** **Very Low** (for causal inference)
- **Assigned evidence level:** Level 3 (cohort — observational)
- **Key finding:** 41% lower risk of major coronary disease in highest vs. lowest quintile of vitamin E intake (RR 0.59, 95% CI 0.38–0.91 for supplements ≥100 IU/day)
- **Quality score contribution:** 4/10 (high n, but observational; hypothesis-generating only given subsequent RCTs)
- **Funding status:** NIH (fully independent)

#### Study: rimm_1993_cohort — PMID 8479464
- **Citation:** Rimm EB et al. N Engl J Med. 1993;328(20):1450-6
- **Study type:** Prospective cohort, n=39,910 male health professionals, 4 years follow-up
- **GRADE domains:**

  - Indirectness: **Serious** — association; no causal inference given subsequent null RCTs

  - Publication bias: **Low** — NEJM, NIH funded

- **Note:** Published as companion to Stampfer 1993 in the same NEJM issue; represents the observational peak before the RCT era

#### Study: meydani_1997_immune — PMID 9134944
- **Citation:** Meydani SN et al. JAMA. 1997;277(17):1380-6
- **Study type:** Randomized controlled trial, n=88 elderly (≥65 years), 235-day follow-up; double-blind, placebo-controlled; dose-response design (60, 200, or 800 IU/day)
- **GRADE domains:**
  - Risk of bias: **Low** — well-designed RCT; double-blind; allocation concealment documented; JAMA publication
  - Inconsistency: **Not applicable** — single RCT
  - Indirectness: **Not serious** — directly tests immune outcome in target elderly population
  - Imprecision: **Moderate** — n=88 across 4 groups (~22/group); relatively small for some secondary endpoints
  - Publication bias: **Low** — JAMA; NIH/USDA-funded
- **Overall GRADE certainty:** **Moderate** (for immune outcomes in elderly)
- **Assigned evidence level:** Level 2 (RCT)
- **Key finding:** 200 IU/day produced greatest immune benefit: 65% increase in delayed-type hypersensitivity (DTH) response; 6-fold increase in antibody response to hepatitis B; reduced infection rate (23% vs. 50% in placebo)
- **Quality score contribution:** 7/10
- **Funding status:** NIH/USDA (fully independent)

#### Study: hope_2000_cvd — PMID 10639540
- **Citation:** Lonn E, Bosch J, Yusuf S et al. (HOPE study investigators). N Engl J Med. 2000;342(3):154-60
- **Study type:** RCT (HOPE trial — Heart Outcomes Prevention Evaluation), n=9,541 high-risk patients, 4.5 years follow-up; double-blind, 2×2 factorial (vitamin E 400 IU/day vs. ramipril)
- **GRADE domains:**
  - Risk of bias: **Low** — large, well-designed RCT; double-blind; major cardiovascular outcomes trial; independent DSMB
  - Inconsistency: **Not applicable** — single RCT (though replicated by HOPE-TOO and PHS II)
  - Indirectness: **Not serious** — directly tested the observational CVD hypothesis in high-risk population
  - Imprecision: **Not serious** — n=9,541; well-powered for primary CVD endpoint
  - Publication bias: **Low** — NEJM; MRC/Heart & Stroke Foundation funded; null result reported
- **Overall GRADE certainty:** **High** (null finding is definitive for CVD in high-risk patients)
- **Assigned evidence level:** Level 1 (large RCT — primary cardiovascular outcome trial)
- **Key finding:** No reduction in MI, stroke, or cardiovascular death (RR 1.05, 95% CI 0.95–1.16). Directly refutes the observational associations from Stampfer and Rimm 1993.
- **Quality score contribution:** 9/10
- **Funding status:** Canadian MRC, Heart & Stroke Foundation (fully independent)

#### Study: miller_2005_mortality — PMID 15537682
- **Citation:** Miller ER, Pastor-Barriuso R, Dalal D, et al. Ann Intern Med. 2005;142(1):37-46
- **Study type:** Meta-analysis, 19 clinical trials, n=135,967, dose-response meta-regression
- **GRADE domains:**
  - Risk of bias: **Low** — systematic meta-analysis; dose-response regression; Ann Intern Med; independent
  - Inconsistency: **Not serious** — dose-response relationship consistent across trials
  - Indirectness: **Not serious** — all-cause mortality is the most direct harm outcome possible
  - Imprecision: **Not serious** — n=135,967; narrow CIs on primary mortality estimate
  - Publication bias: **Low** — funnel plot assessed; includes null trials
- **Overall GRADE certainty:** **Moderate-High** (for harm at high doses)
- **Assigned evidence level:** Level 1 (meta-analysis)
- **⭐ KEY HARM CITATION:** At doses ≥400 IU/day, risk of all-cause mortality increases by 6% (RR 1.06, 95% CI 1.01–1.12, p=0.01 for dose-response). This is the primary quantitative harm signal for high-dose vitamin E.
- **Quality score contribution:** 9/10
- **Funding status:** Independent (Hopkins/NHLBI) — no supplement industry involvement

#### Study: lonn_2005_hopetoo — PMID 15769967
- **Citation:** Lonn E, Bosch J, Yusuf S, et al. (HOPE-TOO investigators). JAMA. 2005;293(11):1338-47
- **Study type:** RCT extension (HOPE-TOO), n=7,030, median 7 years total follow-up
- **GRADE domains:**
  - Risk of bias: **Low** — extension of the HOPE trial; same rigorous methodology; double-blind maintained
  - Inconsistency: **Not serious** — consistent null finding with original HOPE
  - Indirectness: **Not serious** — long-term CVD follow-up; directly relevant population
  - Imprecision: **Not serious** — large n; well-powered for primary endpoints
  - Publication bias: **Low** — JAMA; null/harm result published
- **Overall GRADE certainty:** **High** (null CVD + harm signal for heart failure)
- **Assigned evidence level:** Level 1 (large RCT extension)
- **Key finding:** Null primary CVD endpoint (confirmed HOPE null). Heart failure: RR 1.13 (95% CI 1.01–1.26, p=0.03) — statistically significant increase. This is the first signal of active cardiovascular harm (not just null).
- **Quality score contribution:** 9/10
- **Funding status:** MRC Canada (fully independent)

#### Study: sesso_2008_phsii — PMID 18997197
- **Citation:** Sesso HD, Buring JE, Christen WG, et al. JAMA. 2008;300(18):2123-33
- **Study type:** RCT (PHS II — Physicians' Health Study II), n=14,641 male physicians, median 8 years follow-up; 2×2×2 factorial (vitamin E 400 IU/day vs. vitamin C vs. beta-carotene)
- **GRADE domains:**
  - Risk of bias: **Low** — large, multi-year blinded RCT; factorial design isolates vitamin E component; independent DSMB
  - Inconsistency: **Not applicable** — single RCT
  - Indirectness: **Not serious** — long follow-up; major CVD outcomes
  - Imprecision: **Not serious** — largest single vitamin E CVD RCT (n=14,641)
  - Publication bias: **Low** — JAMA; NIH-funded; null/harm result published
- **Overall GRADE certainty:** **High** (null CVD; moderate-high for hemorrhagic stroke signal)
- **Assigned evidence level:** Level 1 (largest single vitamin E RCT)
- **Key finding:** Null for MI (RR 0.99), total stroke (RR 1.07), CVD mortality (RR 1.07). Significant increase in hemorrhagic stroke (HR 1.74, 95% CI 1.04–2.91, p=0.036) — consistent with anticoagulant mechanism.
- **Quality score contribution:** 10/10 (methodologically strongest single trial)
- **Funding status:** NIH/NCI (fully independent)

#### Study: dysken_2014_alzheimers — PMID 24381967
- **Citation:** Dysken MW, Sano M, Asthana S, et al. JAMA. 2014;311(1):33-44
- **Study type:** RCT (TEAM-AD VA Cooperative Randomized Trial), n=613 mild-to-moderate AD patients, mean 2.27 years follow-up; double-blind, placebo-controlled; 4 arms (2000 IU/day vitamin E alone, memantine alone, combination, placebo)
- **GRADE domains:**
  - Risk of bias: **Low** — well-designed VA cooperative RCT; double-blind; independent DSMB; JAMA publication
  - Inconsistency: **Not applicable** — single RCT
  - Indirectness: **Not serious** — directly tests functional decline in established AD using ADCS-ADL primary outcome
  - Imprecision: **Moderate** — n=613 across 4 arms (~153/arm); ADCS-ADL effect size moderate; combination arm null
  - Publication bias: **Low** — JAMA; VA/NIH-funded; reports both positive and null arms
- **Overall GRADE certainty:** **Moderate** (for functional preservation in mild-to-moderate AD at 2000 IU/day)
- **Assigned evidence level:** Level 2 (RCT — moderate quality; single trial)
- **Key finding:** Vitamin E alone: 3.15-unit slower decline on ADCS-ADL scale vs. placebo (95% CI 0.92–5.39, p=0.03). Annual rate of decline was 19% slower. Memantine alone: null. Combination: null (possible interference). No significant safety concerns at the VA population level.
- **Quality score contribution:** 8/10
- **Funding status:** VA Cooperative Studies Program + NIH (fully independent)
- **Important caveat:** The combination arm (vitamin E + memantine) showed no benefit, suggesting possible antagonism. This single trial has not been replicated. The 2000 IU/day dose is in the range associated with all-cause mortality harm per Miller 2005 — specialist supervision required.

### Summary Judgment

The vitamin E evidence base is **mature, large-scale, and internally consistent on CVD null/harm**. Three independent large RCTs (HOPE n=9,541; HOPE-TOO n=7,030; PHS II n=14,641) and a meta-analysis (n=135,967) definitively refute the observational CVD hypothesis. High-dose (≥400 IU/day) vitamin E is harmful — Miller 2005 establishes a dose-response mortality increase. Tier 2 is appropriate based on the scale of evidence. Positive evidence for immune function (Meydani 1997) and AD functional preservation (Dysken 2014) are both from single, NIH/VA-funded RCTs that have not been independently replicated.

**Evidence Level Designation: Tier 2** — Large, mature, largely independent RCT evidence base; CVD harm and null finding is definitive; specific positive signals for immune and AD endpoints require replication.
