### Decision Tree — Tier 1 vs Tier 2 Discrimination

**Why NOT Tier 1:**
- Tier 1 requires multiple systematic reviews/meta-analyses with consistent positive benefit findings AND large RCTs demonstrating the primary claimed benefit
- Vitamin E's primary historical claim (CVD prevention) is definitively null/harmful — there is no positive Tier 1 candidate claim
- Immune benefit (Meydani 1997) is from a single RCT (n=88) — not replicated; insufficient for Tier 1
- AD benefit (Dysken 2014) is from a single RCT (n=613) — not replicated; insufficient for Tier 1
- The meta-analysis (Miller 2005) is a harm synthesis, not a benefit synthesis

**Why NOT Tier 3:**
- Tier 3 applies to supplements with small RCTs (n<100 each) and limited or contradictory independent synthesis
- Vitamin E has 3 large, well-powered RCTs (HOPE, HOPE-TOO, PHS II) with consistent findings
- Meta-analysis with n>100,000 is definitively above the Tier 3 threshold
- Evidence base is 30+ years mature — classifying as Tier 3 would understate the clinical certainty

**Tier 2 is justified because:**
- Multiple RCTs with large samples (3 with n>7,000)
- At least one meta-analysis (Miller 2005)
- Consistent findings across independent groups for the well-studied outcomes
- Specific positive indications are supported by well-conducted single RCTs
- The evidence is sufficient for clinical guidance — just complex in direction (null/harm for CVD; moderate positive for immune/AD at specific doses)

### Research Maturity: Mature

**Justification:** Vitamin E research is one of the longest-running supplement research programs, spanning 1993 observational studies through 2014 RCTs with n>135,000 in the largest synthesis. The CVD question was thoroughly answered by 2008 (HOPE 2000, HOPE-TOO 2005, PHS II 2008). The high-dose mortality risk was quantified by 2005 (Miller). The immune benefit was demonstrated in 1997 (Meydani). The AD benefit was demonstrated in 2014 (Dysken). "Mature" reflects that the major clinical questions have been answered — the evidence landscape is unlikely to shift substantially for CVD or harm signals. The primary evidence gap is independent replication of the immune and AD findings.

---

### Evidence Gaps Identified

1. **No independent replication of Meydani 1997 immune RCT**: Single 1997 trial remains the foundation of the immune claim. A well-powered replication study (n>200 elderly) would either confirm or challenge the 200 IU/day immune signal.

2. **No independent replication of Dysken 2014 AD trial**: TEAM-AD remains the only large RCT of vitamin E in established AD. The combination arm nullity (vitamin E + memantine) was unexplained and warrants mechanistic follow-up.

3. **No post-2014 large-scale RCT data**: The evidence base has a 12-year evidence gap for new trial data. Updated cardiovascular guidance (post-statins, post-PCSK9 inhibitors) has not been tested with vitamin E supplementation in the contemporary prevention context.

4. **Form heterogeneity**: All major trials used alpha-tocopherol acetate (synthetic or natural). Mixed tocopherols and tocotrienols have different bioavailability and may have different risk profiles. No RCT has directly compared natural d-alpha-tocopherol vs. synthetic dl-alpha-tocopherol vs. mixed tocopherol forms at equivalent doses.

5. **Dose-response for AD**: Dysken 2014 used 2000 IU/day — the same dose range associated with mortality harm per Miller 2005. The benefit/risk calculation at 2000 IU/day in an AD population has not been independently characterized. A dose-response trial in AD (testing 200, 400, 1000, 2000 IU/day) would define the optimal therapeutic window.

6. **Interaction with modern pharmacotherapy**: All large trials predated current standard of care (statins at current prevalence, PCSK9 inhibitors, GLP-1 agonists). Vitamin E's interaction with contemporary cardiovascular pharmacotherapy is unknown.
