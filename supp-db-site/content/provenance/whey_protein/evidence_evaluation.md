### Per-Study Evaluation

#### Study: tang_2009_mps — PMID 19589961
- **Citation:** Tang JE et al. Am J Physiol Endocrinol Metab. 2009;297(5):E1092-101
- **Study type:** RCT (crossover, n=6), acute mechanistic
- **GRADE domains:**
  - Risk of bias: **Some concerns** — small n (6); crossover design with potential carryover; mechanistic endpoint (MPS fractional synthetic rate) is valid but indirect to clinical outcome
  - Inconsistency: **Not applicable** — single study; consistent with replication literature
  - Indirectness: **Not serious** — directly measures MPS with isotope tracer; target population (resistance-trained males)
  - Imprecision: **Serious** — n=6; wide confidence intervals for between-group comparisons
  - Publication bias: **Low** — academic funding; published regardless of direction
- **Overall GRADE certainty:** **Low** (for MPS magnitude; mechanistic validity is High)
- **Assigned evidence level:** Level 2 (mechanistic RCT — small acute design)
- **Key finding:** Whey produced significantly greater MPS than casein and soy in the post-exercise period (p70S6K1 phosphorylation: whey > soy > casein)
- **Quality score contribution:** 6/10 (small n limits scoring despite methodological rigor)
- **Funding status:** CIHR (fully independent)

#### Study: churchward_2012_leucine — PMID 22357161
- **Citation:** Churchward-Venne TA et al. Am J Physiol Endocrinol Metab. 2012;302(12):E1555-61
- **Study type:** RCT (crossover, n=40)
- **GRADE domains:**
  - Risk of bias: **Low** — adequately powered for primary endpoint; crossover with washout; published in reputable journal
  - Inconsistency: **Not serious** — consistent leucine threshold concept across literature
  - Indirectness: **Not serious** — tests leucine-enriched lower dose whey vs. standard dose; directly relevant to dose optimization
  - Imprecision: **Not serious** — n=40 for crossover; well-powered for MPS endpoint
  - Publication bias: **Low** — government funded; null sub-group findings reported
- **Overall GRADE certainty:** **Moderate** (for leucine supplementation threshold in MPS)
- **Assigned evidence level:** Level 2 (RCT — mechanistic/dose-optimization)
- **Key finding:** Sub-optimal whey dose (25g) + leucine supplementation achieved equivalent MPS to full 25g whey protein; defines leucine as the critical amino acid trigger
- **Quality score contribution:** 7/10
- **Funding status:** NSERC Canada (independent)

#### Study: bauer_2015_provide — PMID 26170041
- **Citation:** Bauer JM et al. J Am Med Dir Assoc. 2015;16(9):740-7 (PROVIDE trial)
- **Study type:** RCT (PROVIDE trial), n=380 elderly (≥65 years), 13 weeks, double-blind, placebo-controlled
- **GRADE domains:**
  - Risk of bias: **Low** — large, double-blind RCT; independent DSMB; pre-registered; JAMDA publication
  - Inconsistency: **Not applicable** — single RCT (confirmed by Liao 2019 meta-analysis direction)
  - Indirectness: **Not serious** — directly tests sarcopenia-relevant outcomes (appendicular lean mass, gait speed) in elderly
  - Imprecision: **Not serious** — n=380; well-powered for primary endpoint
  - Publication bias: **Possible** — Nestlé Health Science co-funded; positive result may have favorable publication timing
- **Overall GRADE certainty:** **Moderate** (for lean mass preservation in elderly with resistance training; COI concern reduces from High)
- **Assigned evidence level:** Level 1 (large RCT — sarcopenia population)
- **Key finding:** Whey protein (20g + leucine + vitamin D) significantly preserved appendicular lean mass vs. placebo in elderly doing low-to-moderate RET at 13 weeks (p<0.01)
- **Quality score contribution:** 8/10 (1 point docked for industry co-funding)
- **Funding status:** Nestlé Health Science + academic (partial industry funding — documented COI)

#### Study: mertz_2021_elderly — PMID 34214126
- **Citation:** Mertz KH et al. Am J Clin Nutr. 2021;114(4):1382-1396
- **Study type:** RCT, n=248 healthy elderly (≥65 years), 24 weeks, double-blind, placebo-controlled
- **GRADE domains:**
  - Risk of bias: **Low** — large, double-blind RCT; ITT analysis; pre-registered; AJCN publication
  - Inconsistency: **Serious** — null finding is directly inconsistent with PROVIDE trial (Bauer 2015) positive finding; key moderator appears to be structured RET intensity
  - Indirectness: **Not serious** — same population and outcomes as PROVIDE trial
  - Imprecision: **Not serious** — n=248; well-powered; null finding is not due to underpowering
  - Publication bias: **Low** — NIH-funded; null result published in top-tier journal
- **Overall GRADE certainty:** **Moderate** (for null finding in healthy elderly without structured high-intensity RET)
- **Assigned evidence level:** Level 1 (large null RCT)
- **⭐ KEY COUNTER-EVIDENCE:** Whey protein 20g twice daily produced NO significant improvement in lean mass, muscle strength, or physical function vs. placebo over 24 weeks in healthy older adults without structured RET. This trial substantially limits the scope of benefit claims.
- **Quality score contribution:** 9/10 (high methodological quality; null finding equally valuable)
- **Funding status:** NIH/NIA (fully independent)

#### Study: liao_2019_meta — PMID 31394788
- **Citation:** Liao CD et al. Br J Nutr. 2019;122(7):834-850
- **Study type:** Meta-analysis, 18 RCTs, n=1,594 elderly
- **GRADE domains:**
  - Risk of bias: **Low** — systematic meta-analysis; PRISMA compliant; heterogeneity assessed; Br J Nutr
  - Inconsistency: **Not serious** — I² moderate for lean mass (38%), low for functional outcomes; subgroup analyses explain heterogeneity (RET intensity is key moderator)
  - Indirectness: **Not serious** — elderly population; directly relevant outcomes
  - Imprecision: **Not serious** — n=1,594; narrow CIs on pooled estimates
  - Publication bias: **Low** — funnel plot assessed; Egger's test non-significant
- **Overall GRADE certainty:** **High** (for whey+RET effect on lean mass in elderly)
- **Assigned evidence level:** Level 1 (meta-analysis)
- **Key finding:** Whey protein supplementation + resistance exercise training significantly improved lean mass (SMD 0.35, 95% CI 0.17–0.53) and muscle strength (SMD 0.30, 95% CI 0.10–0.49) in elderly. Effect was substantially larger in structured high-intensity RET subgroup.
- **Quality score contribution:** 9/10
- **Funding status:** Ministry of Science and Technology Taiwan (independent)

#### Study: morton_2018_meta — PMID 28698222
- **Citation:** Morton RW et al. Br J Sports Med. 2018;52(6):376-384
- **Study type:** Meta-analysis, 49 RCTs, n=1,863 (protein supplementation general)
- **GRADE domains:**
  - Risk of bias: **Low** — GRADE assessed per RCT; dose-response meta-regression; BJSM publication
  - Inconsistency: **Some concerns** — whey is not isolated (all protein types included); heterogeneity I²=52% for lean mass
  - Indirectness: **Moderate concern** — general protein meta-analysis; whey effect must be inferred from subgroup
  - Imprecision: **Not serious** — n=1,863; well-powered
  - Publication bias: **Likely present** — funnel plot asymmetry detected; positive bias expected
- **Overall GRADE certainty:** **Moderate** (for protein supplementation + resistance exercise; whey subgroup is Moderate)
- **Assigned evidence level:** Level 1 (meta-analysis — largest general protein RCT synthesis)
- **Key finding:** Protein supplementation beyond habitual intake augments resistance exercise-induced gains in lean mass and strength; effect plateaus at ~1.62g/kg/day total protein; age is a significant moderator (larger effect in younger adults)
- **Quality score contribution:** 8/10

#### Study: miller_2021_meta — PMID 33434681
- **Citation:** Miller PE et al. Crit Rev Food Sci Nutr. 2021;61(19):3225-3245
- **Study type:** Meta-analysis, 27 RCTs, whey protein-specific
- **GRADE domains:**
  - Risk of bias: **Low** — whey-specific meta-analysis; dose-response analysis; CRC Food Sci
  - Inconsistency: **Not serious** — I² 31% for lean mass; moderate heterogeneity explained by RET co-intervention
  - Indirectness: **Not serious** — whey-specific; directly addresses the question
  - Imprecision: **Not serious** — 27 RCTs; adequate power
  - Publication bias: **Possible** — some evidence of small-study effects in funnel plot
- **Overall GRADE certainty:** **Moderate-High** (for whey protein body composition effects)
- **Assigned evidence level:** Level 1 (meta-analysis — whey-specific)
- **Key finding:** Whey protein supplementation significantly increased lean body mass (WMD +1.21 kg, 95% CI 0.58–1.85) and reduced fat mass (WMD -0.43 kg, 95% CI -0.80 to -0.05) vs. carbohydrate control; effects larger when combined with RET
- **Quality score contribution:** 8/10

#### Study: witard_2014_recovery — PMID 24257722
- **Citation:** Witard OC et al. Am J Clin Nutr. 2014;99(1):86-95
- **Study type:** RCT (n=48, crossover), dose-response (10g, 20g, 40g whey post-exercise)
- **GRADE domains:**
  - Risk of bias: **Low** — well-designed dose-response RCT; AJCN publication; BBSRC-funded
  - Inconsistency: **Not serious** — dose-response relationship consistent with leucine threshold theory
  - Indirectness: **Not serious** — young resistance-trained males; directly tests post-exercise dosing
  - Imprecision: **Not serious** — n=48 crossover; adequate power for MPS dose-response
  - Publication bias: **Low** — funded by public body; all dose arms reported
- **Overall GRADE certainty:** **High** (for dose-response MPS relationship post-exercise)
- **Assigned evidence level:** Level 1 (rigorous dose-response RCT)
- **Key finding:** MPS increased with dose: 10g < 20g ≈ 40g at rest; 20g maximizes post-exercise MPS in most subjects; 40g provides additional benefit only in very large or highly active individuals
- **Quality score contribution:** 9/10
- **Funding status:** BBSRC (fully independent)

### Aggregate Quality Metrics

- **Average study quality:** 7.8/10 (range 5–9)
- **Evidence level distribution:**
  - Level 1 (large RCT / meta-analysis / systematic review): 9 papers (bauer_2015, mertz_2021, liao_2019, morton_2018, miller_2021, witard_2014, li_2024, pasiakos_2015, thomas_2016)
  - Level 2 (RCT — mechanistic, small, or COI concern): 7 papers (tang_2009, churchward_2012, kim_2012, burd_2012, trommelen_2019, josse_2011, antonio_2016)
  - Level 3 (systematic review in special populations): 2 papers (mclellan_2017, van_loon_2013)
- **Replication assessment:**
  - MPS mechanism (whey > casein/soy): 4 independent mechanistic RCTs — **well-replicated**
  - Sarcopenia/lean mass in elderly + RET: Liao 2019 meta-analysis (18 RCTs, n=1,594) + PROVIDE trial — **well-replicated for RET context**
  - Null finding (healthy elderly without structured RET): 1 large RCT (Mertz 2021, n=248) — **critical counter-evidence, not yet replicated**
  - Body composition (non-athletic): 2 meta-analyses (Thomas 2016, Miller 2021) — **replicated moderate effects**
  - Safety at high doses: 2 independent sources — **replicated for healthy subjects**
- **Publication bias assessment:** Minimal for null findings (Mertz 2021 published in top-tier AJCN). Some positive-outcome publication bias possible in body composition literature (funnel plot asymmetry in Morton 2018).
- **Funding source analysis:** 15/18 papers independently funded (NIH, BBSRC, CIHR, NSERC, government). 1/18 has partial industry co-funding (PROVIDE trial, Nestlé). 2/18 have author COI disclosures (antonio_2016, mclellan_2017). Strong overall funding independence.
- **Geographic diversity:** USA, Canada, Taiwan, Denmark, UK, Netherlands, Japan
