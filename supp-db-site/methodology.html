1. Evidence Evaluation Pipeline

Every supplement in the SupplementDB database passes through a structured four-stage evaluation pipeline before publication. This process ensures that all data points are accurate, traceable, and grounded in peer-reviewed research.

1

Literature Search

Systematic search across PubMed, PMC, Cochrane, bioRxiv, and ClinicalTrials.gov

2

Citation Verification

DOI resolution, PMID cross-referencing, author/journal confirmation

3

Data Extraction

Benefits, dosages, mechanisms, safety, effect sizes from published studies

4

Evidence Rating

4-tier classification based on research quality, quantity, and consistency

2. Evidence Tier System

Each supplement receives a tier rating that reflects the current state of published clinical research. Higher tiers indicate more robust evidence — not necessarily greater effectiveness.

Tier 1 — Strong Evidence

Consistent findings across multiple large RCTs (n>200 each), supported by at least one systematic review or meta-analysis. High confidence in reported effects.

Typical base: 5+ RCTs, 1+ meta-analyses, large sample sizes • Examples: Omega-3, Magnesium, Creatine, Ashwagandha

Tier 2 — Moderate Evidence

Multiple RCTs with moderate sample sizes showing generally consistent results. Some meta-analytic support but may have limitations in study design or population diversity.

Typical base: 3+ RCTs, n=50–200, some meta-analyses • Examples: Rhodiola Rosea, CoQ10, NAC, Berberine

Tier 3 — Preliminary Evidence

Limited RCTs available. Evidence is drawn primarily from smaller trials, observational studies, or studies with mixed results. Promising but not yet well-established.

Typical base: 1–2 RCTs, mostly small samples (n<50), observational data • Examples: Lion's Mane, PQQ, Phosphatidylserine

Tier 4 — Emerging Evidence

Primarily preclinical research (cell culture, animal models) or very early-phase human investigations. Mechanisms may be well-characterized but human clinical data is limited.

Typical base: In vitro/animal studies, pilot trials • Examples: Fisetin, Sulforaphane, Cistanche

Critical distinction: Evidence tiers measure research maturity, not supplement effectiveness. A Tier 4 supplement may be effective but under-studied, while a Tier 1 supplement has extensive clinical validation. Tiers are updated as new research is published.

3. Citation Verification Process

Every citation in the SupplementDB library is individually verified through a multi-step process. This ensures that no data point in the database is based on fabricated, retracted, or misattributed research.

Verification Step Method Status
DOI Resolution Each DOI is tested to confirm it resolves to the correct publication on the publisher's website All 471+ verified
PMID Cross-Reference PubMed IDs are validated against NLM records; title, authors, and journal are cross-checked All PMIDs confirmed
Study Type Classification Each study is categorized by design: RCT, meta-analysis, systematic review, cohort, pilot, etc. All classified

Effect Size Recording Where reported, standardized effect sizes (Cohen's d, SMD, OR, RR) are documented Available per supplement
Retraction Monitoring Citations are checked against the Retraction Watch database for retractions or corrections Monitored

4. Data Sources & Evidence Hierarchy

We draw from established biomedical databases and prioritize evidence according to a strict hierarchy based on study design quality:

Primary Databases

  • PubMed / MEDLINE — 36+ million biomedical citations from the U.S. National Library of Medicine
  • PubMed Central (PMC) — Full-text archive for open-access biomedical literature
  • Cochrane Library — Gold-standard systematic reviews for evidence-based healthcare
  • ClinicalTrials.gov — Registry and results database for clinical studies
  • bioRxiv / medRxiv — Preprint servers for early research (clearly labeled when cited)

Evidence Hierarchy (Descending Strength)

  1. Systematic reviews & meta-analyses — Highest weight; pool data across multiple studies
  2. Network meta-analyses — Compare interventions that may not have been directly studied head-to-head
  3. Randomized controlled trials (RCTs) — The gold standard for individual study design
  4. Cohort and observational studies — Valuable for long-term safety and real-world patterns
  5. Mechanistic and preclinical studies — Inform biological plausibility, especially for Tier 4 supplements

In plain language: We use the same evidence hierarchy that medical research uses — the strongest evidence comes from pooling many clinical trials together, not from any single study.

5. How We Summarize the Research

For each of the 93 supplements in the database, our team reviews the published literature and distills it into a consistent, reader-friendly format. Here's what we look for and how we present it:

What We Cover How We Approach It
Primary Benefits We summarize the key findings from published clinical trials and meta-analyses, using clear language that reflects what the research actually shows
Dosage Range Dosage ranges are drawn directly from clinical study protocols, noting the forms and standardizations used in the research
Mechanisms of Action We describe the known biological pathways based on mechanistic studies and review papers, explaining how a supplement interacts with the body
Safety Profile Side effects, contraindications, and drug interactions are compiled from published adverse event data and interaction databases
Effect Sizes Where reported, we document standardized measures (Cohen's d, SMD, odds ratios) along with study details and participant counts so you can gauge practical significance
Study Populations We note who was studied — healthy adults, older adults, athletes, specific clinical populations — so you can assess how relevant the findings are to your situation

6. Limitations & Transparency

Rigorous methodology does not eliminate all limitations. We believe transparent acknowledgment of these limitations is essential to maintaining trust:

  • Publication bias — Published research over-represents positive findings. We note this limitation and include effect sizes where available so readers can assess practical significance
  • Research lag — A natural delay exists between publication and database integration. We track update dates per supplement
  • Individual variation — Clinical research reports population averages. Individual responses to supplements vary based on genetics, health status, and concurrent medications
  • Supplement quality variability — Research studies use standardized extracts. Commercial supplement quality varies by manufacturer. We note standardization requirements where relevant
  • Not medical advice — SupplementDB is an informational resource. Always consult a qualified healthcare professional before starting any supplement regimen
