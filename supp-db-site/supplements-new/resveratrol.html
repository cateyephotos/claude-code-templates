Background

Overview

Resveratrol is classified as polyphenol with tier 2: moderate evidence for its primary applications. Multiple meta-analyses (including 1 umbrella meta-analysis of 19 MAs, 81 RCTs, n=4088) confirm anti-inflammatory, body composition, and endothelial benefits. Consistent replication across independent research groups. Evidence tier upgraded from 3; SIRT1 mechanism reclassified to Theoretical after Mansouri 2025 null meta-analysis (11 RCTs).

Deep Dive

Enhanced Evidence Review

Detailed citation analysis organized by research domain.

Mechanisms of Action

Comprehensive pharmacological profile including SIRT1 activation, antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, and cardioprotective mechanisms

Strong Comprehensive Pharmacological Review 2021 Multiple preclinical and clinical studies

Comprehensive pharmacological review of resveratrol. Active mechanisms include SIRT1 pathway activation, NF-kB-mediated anti-inflammatory signaling, antioxidant defense against reactive oxygen species, inhibition of platelet aggregation, anti-adipogenic effects through inhibiting fat accumulation while activating oxidative and lipolytic pathways, and neuroprotective properties. Well tolerated and favorably affects cardiovascular, neurological, and diabetic disorders.

PubMed: 34649335

Cardioprotective mechanisms and therapeutic potential in cardiovascular disease and heart failure

Strong Narrative Review 2019 Multiple preclinical and clinical studies

Narrative review of resveratrol in cardiovascular disease and heart failure. Cardioprotective mechanisms include improvement of endothelial function, reduction of oxidative stress, inhibition of inflammatory pathways, and modulation of lipid metabolism. Clinical evidence supports potential as adjunctive therapy for cardiovascular conditions.

PubMed: 30791450

Molecular mechanisms via SIRT1, AMPK, and PI3K/Akt signaling pathways

Strong Molecular Mechanisms Review 2025 Multiple preclinical studies

Review of resveratrol molecular mechanisms demonstrating activation of SIRT1 (silent information regulator 1), AMPK (AMP-activated protein kinase), and PI3K/Akt signaling pathways. Also modulates Nrf2 antioxidant defense, Wnt/beta-catenin signaling, and BMP-2/Smad pathways. These multi-pathway effects underlie its diverse biological activities.

PubMed: 40243497

Clinical Benefits

Blood pressure reduction significant only at high doses (≥300 mg/day) and in diabetic subgroups

Moderate Evidence Systematic Review & Meta-analysis 2018 17 RCTs Various

Systematic review and meta-analysis of 17 RCTs evaluating resveratrol effects on blood pressure. Overall effect not significant, but subgroup analysis showed significant systolic blood pressure reduction at doses ≥300 mg/day and in patients with type 2 diabetes. Effect size is dose-dependent and population-specific.

PubMed: 29359958

Systolic blood pressure reduction of -11.90 mmHg at high doses (≥150 mg/day)

Moderate Evidence Meta-analysis 2014 6 RCTs, 247 subjects Various

Meta-analysis of 6 RCTs with 247 subjects. High-dose resveratrol (≥150 mg/day) significantly reduced systolic blood pressure by -11.90 mmHg. No significant effect at lower doses. Supports dose-dependent cardiovascular benefit requiring adequate dosing for clinical efficacy.

PubMed: 24731650

Glucose and insulin improvement significant primarily in diabetic populations

Moderate Evidence Meta-analysis 2014 11 RCTs, 388 subjects Various

Meta-analysis of 11 RCTs with 388 subjects evaluating resveratrol effects on glucose homeostasis and insulin sensitivity. Significant improvement in glucose and insulin measures in diabetic participants but not in non-diabetic subjects, suggesting population-specific metabolic benefits.

PubMed: 24695890

Significant reductions in waist circumference, HbA1c, and lipid parameters across 25 RCTs

Strong Evidence Meta-analysis & Systematic Review 2022 25 RCTs, 1,171 participants Various

Meta-analysis of 25 RCTs with 1,171 participants. Resveratrol significantly decreased waist circumference (SMD -0.36), HbA1c (SMD -0.48), total cholesterol (SMD -0.15), LDL-cholesterol (SMD -0.42), and increased HDL-cholesterol (SMD 0.16). Major clinical value is for obese and diabetic patients.

PubMed: 35431994

Lipid profile improvements limited to TC and TG with small effect sizes

Moderate Evidence Umbrella Meta-analysis 2024 Multiple meta-analyses synthesized Various

Umbrella meta-analysis of resveratrol effects on lipid profile. Resveratrol supplementation reduced serum triglycerides (SMD -0.14) and total cholesterol (SMD -0.20) but did not significantly affect HDL-C or LDL-C. Although statistically significant, these decreases are not clinically important. Resveratrol may serve as adjunctive approach in managing dyslipidemia.

PubMed: 39255906

Dose-dependent lipid-lowering effects with significant TC, TG, and LDL-C reductions

Moderate Evidence Dose-response Meta-analysis 2022 Multiple RCTs Various

Dose-response meta-analysis of RCTs. Resveratrol significantly decreased total cholesterol (MD -10.28 mg/dl), triglycerides (MD -8.56 mg/dl), and LDL-C (MD -5.69 mg/dl) but did not alter HDL-C. Non-linear dose-response relationship significant for LDL-C. LDL-C reduction more significant with interventions ≥12 weeks and in type 2 diabetes subjects.

PubMed: 36145131

Potential to delay cognitive decline in Alzheimer's disease patients

Preliminary Evidence Systematic Review 2022 4 RCTs in Alzheimer's patients Various

Systematic review of resveratrol supplementation in Alzheimer's disease. Four RCTs suggest resveratrol may help delay cognitive decline in AD patients through neuroprotective and anti-inflammatory mechanisms. Evidence is preliminary and larger trials are needed to confirm clinical significance.

PubMed: 35187615

No significant effect on cognition in healthy humans despite strong animal model evidence

Weak Evidence Systematic Review & Meta-analysis 2020 11 meta-analyses reviewed Various

Systematic review and meta-analysis published in Ageing Research Reviews. Despite robust evidence of cognitive benefits in animal models, human clinical trials show no significant effect of resveratrol on cognitive function in healthy populations. Highlights the translational gap between preclinical and clinical evidence for resveratrol's nootropic claims.

PubMed: 33303422

Safety & Tolerability

Well tolerated with moderate, reversible adverse effects at doses ≥500 mg/day

Good Safety Profile Clinical Safety Review 2013 Multiple clinical trials and safety studies

Review of resveratrol metabolism, biological effects, and toxicity in humans. Toxicological data confirm resveratrol is well tolerated. Adverse effects (mainly gastrointestinal including nausea, flatulence, abdominal discomfort, and diarrhea) occur at doses of ≥500 mg/day for long periods but remain moderate and reversible. Exhibits antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, glucose/lipid metabolism improvement, and cardiovascular parameter modulation.

PubMed: 23740855

Well tolerated as nutritional additive with favorable safety across cardiovascular, neurological, and diabetic applications

Good Safety Profile Comprehensive Pharmacological Review 2021 Multiple preclinical and clinical studies

Comprehensive review concluding resveratrol is well tolerated and favorably affects cardiovascular, neurological, and diabetic disorders. Can be considered as nutritional additive and complementary drug. No serious adverse events reported in clinical studies. Caution advised with anticoagulant medications due to antiplatelet properties, and with CYP450-metabolized drugs due to potential interactions.

PubMed: 34649335

Safe pharmacokinetics up to 5000 mg oral dosing with no serious adverse events

Good Safety Profile Pharmacokinetic & Safety Study 2014 Healthy volunteers Single dose and 28-day repeated dosing

Human pharmacokinetic and safety study. Resveratrol derivatives were well tolerated up to oral dosing of 5000 mg with no serious adverse events. Plasma concentrations maintained over 24 hours with repeated daily dosing showing predictable pharmacokinetics consistent with theoretical accumulation curves. Supports safety of high-dose supplementation protocols.

PubMed: 24495149

Dosage Research

Blood pressure effects require ≥300 mg/day; lipid effects significant across dose ranges

Evidence-based Multiple Meta-analyses 2018 17+ RCTs for blood pressure, multiple RCTs for lipids

Blood pressure meta-analysis identifies ≥300 mg/day as threshold for significant systolic blood pressure reduction. For lipid effects, dose-response analysis shows benefit across standard supplementation ranges (150-500 mg/day). Most clinical trials use 150-500 mg/day of trans-resveratrol. Take with meals for optimal absorption. Effects typically require 8-12 weeks of consistent supplementation.

PubMed: 29359958

Non-linear dose-response for LDL-C reduction; ≥12 weeks duration recommended

Evidence-based Dose-response Meta-analysis 2022 Multiple RCTs

Dose-response meta-analysis identified non-linear relationship between resveratrol dose and LDL-C reduction. Subgroup analysis showed greater lipid-lowering effects with intervention duration ≥12 weeks and in subjects with type 2 diabetes. Standard supplementation range: 150-500 mg/day of trans-resveratrol. Higher doses (≥500 mg/day) may cause mild GI side effects without proportional benefit increase.

PubMed: 36145131
