Overview

Cinnamon Extract is classified as herbal extracts with moderate evidence (Tier 2) for its primary applications. Multiple studies showing glucose and inflammation benefits

Multiple meta-analyses (28+ RCTs, n>3000) consistently demonstrate significant fasting blood glucose reduction in type 2 diabetes; HbA1c effects are inconsistent across reviews; lipid profile improvements reported; well-tolerated but coumarin content in cassia cinnamon poses hepatotoxicity risk at high doses; ceylon cinnamon preferred for safety

Key study populations: Type 2 diabetics; Prediabetic individuals; Metabolic syndrome

Clinical Benefits

Reduces fasting blood glucose and improves glycemic markers in type 2 diabetes patients

Strong Well-replicated across 28+ RCTs and multiple meta-analyses

Cinnamon improves glucose and lipids of people with type 2 diabetes

Khan A, Safdar M, Ali Khan MM, Khattak KN, Anderson RA (2003) — Diabetes Care

1g, 3g, or 6g cinnamon daily all significantly reduced fasting serum glucose (18-29%),Significant reductions in triglycerides (23-30%), LDL cholesterol (7-27%), and total cholesterol (12-26%),No significant difference between the three dose levels,Beneficial effects persisted for 20 days after cinnamon withdrawal

PubMed: 14633804

Zare R, Nadjarzadeh A, Zarshenas MM, Shams M, Heydari M (2018) — Clinical Nutrition

500mg cinnamon bark twice daily significantly improved all glycemic outcomes vs placebo,Significant reductions in FPG, 2-hour postprandial glucose, HbA1c, fasting insulin, and insulin resistance,Significant improvements in lipid profile (TC, TG, LDL, HDL),Significant reductions in anthropometric indices (BMI, body weight, waist circumference)

Safety & Tolerability

Cassia cinnamon contains high coumarin levels that may cause hepatotoxicity at doses exceeding the tolerable daily intake of 0.1 mg/kg; ceylon cinnamon is significantly lower in coumarin

Moderate

Toxicology and risk assessment of coumarin: focus on human data

Abraham K, Wohrlin F, Lindtner O, Heinemeyer G, Lampen A (2010) — Molecular Nutrition and Food Research

Tolerable daily intake (TDI) for coumarin established at 0.1 mg/kg body weight by EFSA,Cassia cinnamon is the primary dietary source of coumarin,Coumarin hepatotoxicity is dose-dependent and reversible upon cessation,Individual susceptibility varies considerably due to CYP2A6 polymorphisms

PubMed: 20024932

The Relation between Hepatotoxicity and the Total Coumarin Intake from Traditional Japanese Medicines Containing Cinnamon Bark

Iwata N, Kainuma M, Kobayashi D, Kubota T, Sugawara N, Uchida A, Ozono S, Yamamuro Y, Furusyo N, Ueda K, Kinoshita S, Yokoyama T, Inoue K (2016) — Frontiers in Pharmacology

76% of patients exceeded the TDI for coumarin from cinnamon-containing traditional medicines,No significant hepatotoxicity detected despite exceeding TDI in most patients,Suggests a margin of safety exists beyond the conservative TDI,Recommends monitoring liver function with chronic high-dose use

PubMed: 27378929

Quantification of coumarin in cinnamon and cinnamon food products with HPLC-UV

Woehrlin F, Fry H, Abraham K, Preiss-Weigert A (2010) — Journal of Agricultural and Food Chemistry

Cassia cinnamon: 2,650-7,017 mg/kg coumarin content,Ceylon (C. verum) cinnamon: trace to <190 mg/kg coumarin,Massive variation even within the same tree species and origin,Ceylon cinnamon is clearly preferable for supplementation to minimize coumarin exposure

Dosage Research

Meta-analytic evidence suggests 1-2g/day cinnamon in capsule form is optimal for comprehensive metabolic benefits; doses above 2g/day did not show additional HbA1c or insulin resistance benefits

Effects of cinnamon supplementation on metabolic biomarkers in individuals with type 2 diabetes: a systematic review and meta-analysis

de Moura SL, Gomes BGR, Guilarducci MJ, Coelho OGL, Guimaraes NS, Gomes JMG (2025) — Nutrition Reviews

Both doses of 2g/day or less and doses above 2g/day reduced FBG and postprandial glucose,Only doses of 2g/day or less reduced HbA1c (WMD: -0.68%; 95% CI: -1.16 to -0.1),Only doses of 2g/day or less reduced HOMA-IR (WMD: -0.94; 95% CI: -1.21 to -0.67),Only doses of 2g/day or less reduced BMI (WMD: -1.18 kg/m-squared; 95% CI: -1.97 to -0.39),Capsule form was more effective than other delivery methods

PubMed: 38917435

Cinnamon improves glucose and lipids of people with type 2 diabetes

Khan A, Safdar M, Ali Khan MM, Khattak KN, Anderson RA (2003) — Diabetes Care

1g, 3g, and 6g daily all produced similar glucose and lipid reductions,No dose-response relationship detected — 1g was as effective as 6g,Minimum effective dose appears to be 1g/day,40-day duration sufficient for glucose and lipid effects

PubMed: 14633804

Cinnamon for diabetes mellitus

Leach MJ, Kumar S (2012) — Cochrane Database of Systematic Reviews

Mean cinnamon dose across studies was approximately 2g/day,Study durations ranged from 4 to 16 weeks,Insufficient evidence at the time to draw definitive conclusions on optimal dose,Recommended further large-scale trials with standardized dosing protocols
