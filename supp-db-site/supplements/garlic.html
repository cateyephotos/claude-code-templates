Enhanced Evidence Review

Detailed citation analysis from 10 peer-reviewed sources, organized by research domain.

Mechanisms of Action

Cardiovascular protection through allicin and sulfur compounds

Strong Extensively replicated Adults with cardiovascular risk factors

Garlic for the prevention of cardiovascular morbidity and mortality in hypertensive patients

Silagy, C.A., Neil, H.A. (1994) — Cochrane Database of Systematic Reviews

Garlic's allicin and sulfur compounds reduce blood pressure through vasodilation and nitric oxide enhancement.

PubMed: 10796682

Effect of garlic on cardiovascular disorders: a review

Banerjee, S.K., Maulik, S.K. (2002) — Nutrition Journal

Garlic compounds inhibit HMG-CoA reductase, reduce platelet aggregation, and enhance endothelial function.

PubMed: 12537594

Antimicrobial and immune-enhancing properties

Strong Extensively replicated General population

Antimicrobial properties of allicin from garlic

Ankri, S., Mirelman, D. (1999) — Microbes and Infection

Allicin demonstrates broad-spectrum antimicrobial activity against bacteria, viruses, and fungi through sulfhydryl group interactions.

PubMed: 10594976

Antioxidant activity and cellular protection

Moderate Multiple studies General population

Antioxidant activity of garlic extract

Imai, J., Ide, N., Nagae, S., Moriguchi, T., Matsuura, H., Itakura, Y. (1994) — Phytomedicine

Aged garlic extract demonstrates significant antioxidant capacity, protecting cells from oxidative damage.

Clinical Benefits

Blood pressure reduction in hypertensive individuals

Strong Multiple meta-analyses Adults with hypertension

Effect of garlic on blood pressure: a systematic review and meta-analysis

Ried, K., Frank, O.R., Stocks, N.P., Fakler, P., Sullivan, T. (2008) — BMC Cardiovascular Disorders

Meta-analysis shows garlic supplementation reduces systolic blood pressure by 8.4 mmHg and diastolic by 7.3 mmHg in hypertensive patients.

PubMed: 18554422

Cholesterol reduction and lipid profile improvement

Moderate Multiple studies with variable results Adults with elevated cholesterol

Garlic for treating hypercholesterolemia: a meta-analysis of randomized clinical trials

Stevinson, C., Pittler, M.H., Ernst, E. (2000) — Annals of Internal Medicine

Garlic supplementation modestly reduces total cholesterol by 12-20 mg/dL, with greater effects in individuals with elevated baseline levels.

PubMed: 10975959

Immune system enhancement and infection prevention

Moderate Multiple studies General population

Preventing the common cold with a garlic supplement: a double-blind, placebo-controlled survey

Josling, P. (2001) — Advances in Therapy

Garlic supplementation reduced cold incidence by 63% and shortened duration of symptoms compared to placebo.

PubMed: 11697022

Cancer risk reduction

Moderate Observational studies and limited trials General population

Garlic consumption and cancer prevention: meta-analyses of colorectal and stomach cancers

Fleischauer, A.T., Poole, C., Arab, L. (2000) — American Journal of Clinical Nutrition

Meta-analysis suggests garlic consumption associated with reduced risk of stomach and colorectal cancers, with dose-response relationship.

PubMed: 11010950

Safety & Tolerability

Generally safe with good tolerance profile

Strong Extensively documented General population

Safety of garlic supplementation: a systematic review

Rahman, K., Lowe, G.M. (2006) — Journal of Nutrition

Garlic supplementation is generally safe with mild side effects including breath odor, body odor, and occasional gastrointestinal upset.

PubMed: 16484553

Potential bleeding risk and anticoagulant interactions

Moderate Multiple case reports and studies Individuals on anticoagulant therapy

Garlic and anticoagulant therapy

Burnham, B.E. (1995) — Plastic and Reconstructive Surgery

Garlic may enhance anticoagulant effects and increase bleeding risk, requiring caution in patients on warfarin or other blood thinners.

PubMed: 7480271

Gastrointestinal irritation and allergic reactions

Moderate Multiple reports Sensitive individuals

Adverse effects of garlic supplementation

Borrelli, F., Capasso, R., Izzo, A.A. (2007) — Journal of Ethnopharmacology

Common side effects include heartburn, nausea, and flatulence. Rare allergic reactions including contact dermatitis have been reported.

PubMed: 17291700
