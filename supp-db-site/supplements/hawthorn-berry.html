Overview

Hawthorn Berry is classified as herbal extracts with moderate evidence (Tier 2) for its primary applications. Multiple RCTs demonstrating cardiovascular benefits

Cochrane review and multiple meta-analyses confirm hawthorn extract improves exercise tolerance and symptoms in NYHA II-III heart failure; largest trial (SPICE, n=2681) showed non-significant primary endpoint but significant sudden cardiac death reduction in subgroup with LVEF >=25%; 2025 meta-analysis of 7 RCTs confirms significant blood pressure reduction; modest BP effects in hypertensive diabetics; excellent safety profile with >7000 patients analyzed across systematic reviews

Key study populations: Adults with mild heart conditions; Healthy adults 40+

Enhanced Evidence Review

Detailed citation analysis from 21 peer-reviewed sources, organized by research domain.

Mechanisms of Action

Exerts positive inotropic, vasodilatory, anti-arrhythmic, and cardioprotective effects via oligomeric procyanidins and flavonoid bioactives through cAMP-independent mechanisms

Strong Evidence-Based Review 2018 n=Comprehensive review of nonclinical and clinical evidence including >1300 patients from 2-year mortality study

Holubarsch et al. published a comprehensive evidence-based review in American Journal of Cardiovascular Drugs summarizing nonclinical and clinical evidence for hawthorn extract WS 1442. The extract exerts positive inotropic effects improving cardiac contractility via cAMP-independent mechanism, has antiarrhythmic properties, and protects myocardium from ischemia and reperfusion injury. It also improves endothelial NO synthesis and delays endothelial senescence. Active constituents are oligomeric procyanidins (OPCs) and flavonoids.

PubMed: 29080984

Crataegus exerts cAMP-independent positive inotropy, peripheral and coronary vasodilation, anti-arrhythmic properties, and antioxidant and anti-inflammatory effects

Strong Study Design Paper with Pharmacological Review 2000 n=Preclinical and pharmacological review

Holubarsch et al. published the SPICE trial design paper in the European Journal of Heart Failure reviewing the pharmacological basis for hawthorn in heart failure. They documented five key mechanisms: (1) cAMP-independent positive inotropy; (2) peripheral and coronary vasodilation; (3) protection against ischemia-induced ventricular arrhythmias; (4) antioxidative properties; (5) anti-inflammatory effects. These multiple complementary mechanisms distinguish Crataegus from single-target cardiac drugs.

PubMed: 11113721

Crataegus laevigata significantly reduces neutrophil elastase, a key vascular inflammatory marker, and shows hypolipidemic effects in diabetic patients with coronary heart disease

Moderate Randomized Controlled Trial 2011 n=49 diabetic subjects with chronic coronary heart disease

Dalli et al. published in Phytomedicine that Crataegus laevigata significantly reduced neutrophil elastase from 35.8 to 33.2 ng/ml in the treatment group versus an increase from 31.0 to 36.7 ng/ml in placebo (p<0.0001). LDL-cholesterol decreased from 105 to 92.7 mg/dl (p=0.03) as add-on to statins, and non-HDL cholesterol decreased from 131 to 119.6 mg/dl (p<0.001), demonstrating both anti-inflammatory and hypolipidemic mechanisms.

PubMed: 21242072

Crataegus extracts exert a wide range of cardiovascular pharmacological properties including antioxidant, positive inotropic, anti-inflammatory, anti-cardiac remodeling, antiplatelet aggregation, vasodilating, and endothelial protective effects

Strong Evidence-Based Review 2013 n=Review of in vivo, in vitro, and clinical studies

Wang et al. published a comprehensive evidence-based review in Evidence-Based Complementary and Alternative Medicine documenting multiple cardiovascular mechanisms of Crataegus: antioxidant activity, positive inotropic effect, anti-inflammatory effect, anti-cardiac remodeling effect, antiplatelet aggregation effect, vasodilating effect, endothelial protective effect, reduction of smooth muscle cell migration and proliferation, protective effect against ischemia/reperfusion injury, anti-arrhythmic effect, lipid-lowering effect, and decrease of arterial blood pressure.

PubMed: 24459528

Hawthorn extract restores endothelial-dependent vasorelaxation in aging rats through preservation of NO/eNOS pathway and reduction of oxidative stress in aortic tissue

Moderate Animal Study 2012 n=Aged Wistar rats (20 months old)

Idris-Khodja et al. published in the European Journal of Pharmacology that Crataegus extract improved endothelial dysfunction associated with aging. Treatment of aged rats with hawthorn extract restored endothelium-dependent relaxation to acetylcholine, increased eNOS expression and NO production, and reduced vascular oxidative stress markers. The findings suggest hawthorn may counteract age-related vascular endothelial decline through antioxidant and NO-preserving mechanisms.

PubMed: 22621780

Hawthorn extract restores eNOS expression and NO bioavailability in perivascular adipose tissue, reversing endothelial dysfunction caused by PVAT inflammation

Moderate Animal Study 2017 n=Mouse model of PVAT dysfunction

Xia et al. published findings demonstrating that hawthorn extract (WS 1442) restores eNOS expression and NO bioavailability in perivascular adipose tissue (PVAT). PVAT dysfunction contributes to vascular inflammation and endothelial dysfunction. The hawthorn extract reversed PVAT-mediated endothelial dysfunction by restoring the eNOS/NO pathway in PVAT, providing a novel mechanism for its cardiovascular protective effects beyond direct actions on endothelial cells.

PubMed: 28055105

Clinical Benefits

Significantly increases maximal workload, exercise tolerance, and reduces heart failure symptoms (shortness of breath, fatigue) in chronic heart failure NYHA I-III as adjunctive treatment

Strong Cochrane Systematic Review and Meta-analysis 2008 n=855 patients across 14 RCTs (NYHA classes I-III) 3-16 weeks across included trials

Pittler et al. conducted a Cochrane meta-analysis of 14 RCTs with 855 patients. Maximal workload increased by WMD 5.35 Watts (95% CI 0.71-10.00, p<0.02), exercise tolerance increased by WMD 122.76 Watt-min (95% CI 32.74-212.78), pressure-heart rate product decreased by WMD -19.22 (95% CI -30.46 to -7.98, p<0.01), and symptom severity was reduced by WMD -5.47 (95% CI -8.68 to -2.26, p<0.01). This represents the most comprehensive evidence synthesis for hawthorn in heart failure.

PubMed: 18254076

Significantly increases maximal workload and reduces pressure-heart rate product in chronic heart failure patients

Strong Meta-analysis 2003 n=632 patients across 13 RCTs (NYHA classes I-III) 3-16 weeks

Pittler et al. published the first major meta-analysis in the American Journal of Medicine analyzing 13 RCTs with 632 patients. Maximal workload increased by WMD 7 Watts (95% CI 3-11, p<0.01) and pressure-heart rate product decreased by WMD -20 (95% CI -32 to -8, p<0.01). This established the initial quantitative evidence base for Crataegus efficacy in heart failure, later confirmed and expanded by the 2008 Cochrane review.

PubMed: 12798455

Standardized fresh Crataegus berry extract significantly increases exercise tolerance in NYHA II heart failure patients

Moderate Randomized Controlled Trial 2003 n=143 patients (72 men, 71 women, mean age 64.8 years) with NYHA class II heart failure 8 weeks

Degenring et al. published in Phytomedicine an RCT showing exercise tolerance difference of 8.3 watts in favor of standardized fresh Crataegus berry extract (p=0.045 ITT, p=0.047 PP). The extract was well tolerated with high patient acceptability, using standardized extract of fresh Crataegus berries administered as 3x30 drops per day for 8 weeks.

PubMed: 12833999

WS 1442 extract increases exercise tolerance by 10.8% and reduces double product by 26.8% in NYHA class II heart failure

Moderate Randomized Controlled Trial 2001 n=40 outpatients with congestive heart failure NYHA class II 12 weeks

Zapfe published in Phytomedicine that exercise tolerance increased by 66.3 W-min (10.8%) in the WS 1442 group versus a reduction of 105.3 W-min (16.9%) in the placebo group (p=0.06 borderline significant). Double product decreased by 14.4 mmHg/s (26.8%) in the treatment group versus 1.3 mmHg/s (2.7%) in placebo. WS 1442 was standardized to 18.75% oligomeric procyanidins.

PubMed: 11515715

WS 1442 did not significantly reduce time to first cardiac event in the largest hawthorn heart failure trial, but reduced sudden cardiac death by 39.7% in subgroup with LVEF >=25%

Moderate Randomized Controlled Trial (multicenter) 2008 n=2681 patients with NYHA II-III heart failure and LVEF <=35% 24 months

Holubarsch et al. published the SPICE trial results in the European Journal of Heart Failure. The primary endpoint (time to first cardiac event) was not significant with HR 0.95 (95% CI 0.82-1.10, p=0.476). Cardiac mortality showed a reduction trend with HR 0.89 (0.73-1.09, p=0.269). Notably, sudden cardiac death was significantly reduced in the LVEF >=25% subgroup with HR 0.59 (0.37-0.94, p=0.025). The dose was 900 mg/day WS 1442 added to standard pharmacotherapy for 24 months.

PubMed: 19019730

Hawthorn 900mg/day added to optimal medical therapy showed no significant improvement in 6-minute walk distance but modest LVEF improvement in chronic heart failure

Moderate Randomized Controlled Trial 2009 n=120 ambulatory patients with NYHA class II-III chronic heart failure 6 months

Zick et al. published in the European Journal of Heart Failure that there was no significant difference in 6-minute walk distance (p=0.61) or quality of life, functional capacity, neurohormones, oxidative stress, or inflammation markers. However, a modest LVEF improvement favored hawthorn (p=0.04). More adverse events occurred in the hawthorn group (p=0.02), though most were non-cardiac. This negative primary outcome challenges the adjunctive use of hawthorn in patients already on fully optimized modern therapy.

PubMed: 19789403

Produces significant diastolic blood pressure reduction in hypertensive patients with type 2 diabetes taking prescription drugs, with no herb-drug interactions

Moderate Randomized Controlled Trial 2006 n=79 patients with type 2 diabetes (71% on hypotensive drugs) 16 weeks

Walker et al. published in the British Journal of General Practice that there was a significant group difference in diastolic BP reduction (p=0.035): the hawthorn group decreased from 85.6 to 83.0 mmHg versus an increase from 84.5 to 85.0 mmHg in placebo. Systolic BP reduction was non-significant (3.6 vs 0.8 mmHg, p=0.329). No herb-drug interactions were observed at 1200mg/day, with 71% of the population already on hypotensive drugs, supporting safety as adjunctive therapy.

PubMed: 16762125

Meta-analysis of 7 RCTs confirms hawthorn extract significantly reduces blood pressure, with reductions in both systolic and diastolic BP compared to placebo

Strong Meta-analysis 2025 n=7 RCTs in hypertensive and pre-hypertensive adults Various

Szikora et al. conducted a systematic review and meta-analysis published in 2025 analyzing 7 RCTs evaluating hawthorn extract effects on blood pressure. The pooled analysis confirmed that hawthorn supplementation significantly reduces both systolic and diastolic blood pressure compared to placebo. This represents the first comprehensive meta-analysis specifically focused on the blood pressure lowering effects of hawthorn, confirming earlier individual trial findings.

PubMed: 40732315

No dose-response effect on brachial artery flow-mediated dilation at doses of 1000-2500mg, suggesting blood pressure lowering likely via NO-independent mechanism

Moderate Randomized Controlled Cross-over Trial 2012 n=21 prehypertensive or mildly hypertensive adults 3.5 days per dose period with 4-day washout

Asher et al. published in BMC Complementary and Alternative Medicine that there was no evidence of dose-response effect on flow-mediated dilation or blood pressure at 1000mg, 1500mg, and 2500mg versus placebo. The extract was standardized to 50mg OPC per 250mg. The short-duration design (3.5 days per dose) may have been insufficient to detect chronic effects. Most participants were willing to use hawthorn for blood pressure if evidence supported it.

PubMed: 22458601

Safety & Tolerability

Excellent safety profile established across systematic review of 24 studies with >7000 patients; adverse events rare and predominantly mild (dizziness, GI complaints, headache, palpitations)

Good Safety Profile Systematic Review 2006 n=7311 enrolled, 5577 analyzed across 24 studies

Daniele et al. published a systematic review in Drug Safety analyzing 24 studies with 7311 enrolled and 5577 analyzed patients. Only 166 adverse events were reported. The most frequent were dizziness (n=15), GI complaints (n=24), headache (n=9), migraine (n=8), and palpitation (n=11), all mild to moderate. Eight clinical trials (n=4056) reported no side effects at all. Dose range of 160-1800mg/day was well tolerated with no drug interactions reported.

PubMed: 16752934

Very favorable safety profile as both monotherapy and add-on therapy in heart failure; no drug interactions observed in clinical trials or post-marketing surveillance; positive benefit-risk assessment for NYHA II-III

Good Safety Profile Evidence-Based Review 2018 n=>1300 patients exposed in 2-year mortality study; post-marketing surveillance data

Holubarsch et al. conducted an evidence-based review in American Journal of Cardiovascular Drugs including data from a 2-year mortality study with over 1300 patients exposed to WS 1442. No specific adverse reactions were identified. The extract was safe in a polypharmacy context with multiple cardiac medications for up to 24 months. No drug interactions were found in clinical trials or post-marketing surveillance, leading to a positive benefit-risk assessment for NYHA class II-III heart failure.

PubMed: 29080984

Hawthorn extract (CSE 900mg/day) may increase early heart failure progression risk when added to optimal medical therapy, especially in patients with LVEF <=35%; caution warranted in severe systolic dysfunction

Safety Concern Retrospective Analysis of Randomized Controlled Trial 2008 n=120 patients with mild to moderate heart failure (retrospective analysis of HERB CHF)

Zick et al. published a retrospective analysis of the HERB CHF trial in the European Journal of Heart Failure. CSE did not reduce heart failure progression overall (OR 1.14, 95% CI 0.56-2.35, p=0.86). However, after adjustment, the CSE group was at increased risk of early heart failure progression (OR 6.4, 95% CI 1.5-26.5, p=0.011). In patients with LVEF <=35%, the CSE group had significantly greater risk (OR 3.2, 95% CI 1.3-8.3, p=0.02). This suggests caution in patients with severe systolic dysfunction.

PubMed: 18490196

Dosage Research

Most clinical trials used 160-900mg/day of standardized extract; optimal dose appears to be 900mg/day WS 1442 (18.75% OPCs) based on the largest trials; minimum effective duration 3-8 weeks

Evidence-based Cochrane Systematic Review 2008 n=855 patients across 14 RCTs

Pittler et al.'s Cochrane review of 14 RCTs analyzed dose ranges from 160-1800mg/day of standardized extracts. The most commonly studied preparation was WS 1442 standardized to 18.75% oligomeric procyanidins. Also studied were LI 132 (2.25% flavonoids), Crataegisan, and other preparations. The minimum effective duration appears to be 3-8 weeks for exercise tolerance improvement. Higher doses (900mg/day) were used in the largest and longest trials.

PubMed: 18254076

900mg/day WS 1442 (standardized to 18.75% OPCs) well tolerated for up to 24 months of continuous use in the largest hawthorn heart failure trial (n=2681)

Evidence-based Randomized Controlled Trial (multicenter) 2008 n=2681 patients with NYHA II-III heart failure

The SPICE trial by Holubarsch et al. used 900mg/day WS 1442 in the largest randomized hawthorn trial with 2681 patients published in the European Journal of Heart Failure. The dose was well tolerated for up to 24 months of continuous supplementation. This dose was selected based on prior dose-finding studies showing optimal efficacy-safety balance. Adverse events were comparable between WS 1442 and placebo groups throughout the 2-year follow-up period.

PubMed: 19019730

Hawthorn has demonstrated roles in prevention and treatment of cardiovascular diseases at various extract standardizations; 1200mg/day safe in diabetic patients on multiple medications

Evidence-based Review and Clinical Data 2005 n=Review of clinical trials and mechanistic studies

Chang et al. published a comprehensive review in the American Journal of Chinese Medicine documenting hawthorn's potential in prevention and treatment of cardiovascular diseases including hypertension, hyperlipidemia, and congestive heart failure. Multiple extract standardizations have been studied across clinical trials including WS 1442 (OPC-standardized) and LI 132 (flavonoid-standardized). Walker et al. (2006) subsequently demonstrated safety of 1200mg/day in diabetic patients on multiple prescription medications with no herb-drug interactions.

PubMed: 15844828
