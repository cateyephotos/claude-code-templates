Overview

Moringa is classified as herbal extracts with moderate evidence (Tier 2) for its primary applications. Growing clinical evidence for nutritional and health benefits

Moringa oleifera possesses strong preclinical evidence for anti-inflammatory and antioxidant mechanisms via NF-κB/Nrf2 pathways, but the most rigorous meta-analysis of human RCTs with GRADE assessment (Crișan et al. 2025, 9 RCTs, 649 participants) found very low certainty for all cardiometabolic outcomes with no consistent significant benefits

Key study populations: Nutritional deficiency; Antioxidant support; General wellness

Clinical Benefits

The most rigorous meta-analysis of 9 RCTs with 649 participants found very low certainty evidence for all cardiometabolic outcomes, with only modest diastolic BP reduction that was not robust in sensitivity analyses

Limited Mixed results across studies

Moringa oleifera supplementation and cardiometabolic outcomes in adults: a systematic review and meta-analysis with GRADE assessment

Crișan M et al. (2025) — Nutrients

GRADE certainty rated 'very low' for ALL cardiometabolic outcomes assessed,No significant effects on most outcomes including fasting glucose, HbA1c, total cholesterol, LDL, triglycerides,Only modest diastolic BP reduction (SMD -0.41) observed but not robust in sensitivity analyses,Subgroup analysis suggested potential benefits at doses <10 g/day, age <50, and interventions ≥12 weeks

PubMed: 41305552

Double-blind RCT showed 2400 mg/day moringa leaf powder decreased FBG and HbA1c in prediabetic subjects compared to placebo, where placebo showed increases

Moderate Single study - not replicated

Moringa oleifera Leaf Supplementation as a Glycemic Control Strategy in Subjects with Prediabetes

2400 mg/day moringa leaf powder capsules vs placebo,Significant favorable changes in fasting blood glucose rate of change vs placebo,HbA1c showed opposite directions: decreased in moringa, increased in placebo,No differences found in gut microbiota or appetite-controlling hormones

PubMed: 35010932

Meta-analysis of 44 studies in 699 diabetic rodents showed large pooled effect size of -3.92 for blood glucose reduction

Strong (Preclinical) Extensively replicated in animal models

Watanabe S et al. (2021) — Molecules

Pooled effect size -3.92 for blood glucose reduction in diabetic rodent models,Consistent hypoglycemic effects across diverse moringa preparations,Effects observed with leaf extracts, leaf powder, and seed extracts,Strong preclinical signal supporting antidiabetic potential requiring human translation

Safety & Tolerability

Comprehensive review found no adverse effects in human studies, with animal safety studies indicating a high degree of safety for aqueous leaf extracts

Good Safety Profile Confirmed across multiple populations

Review of the Safety and Efficacy of Moringa oleifera

Stohs SJ, Hartman MJ (2015) — Phytother Res

No adverse effects reported in any human studies reviewed (5 published studies at time of review),Animal safety studies with aqueous leaf extracts indicate high degree of safety,Long history of traditional food use in South Asia and Africa,Standardization of products remains an issue for consistent dosing

PubMed: 25808883

Moringa oleifera: a systematic review of its botany, traditional uses, phytochemistry, pharmacology and toxicity
