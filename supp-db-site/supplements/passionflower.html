Enhanced Evidence Review

Detailed citation analysis organized by research domain.

Mechanisms of Action

Passiflora incarnata extracts elicit GABA currents in hippocampal neurons via direct GABAergic...

Strong In Vitro Electrophysiology Study 2010 Rat hippocampal neurons

Electrophysiology study demonstrating that Passiflora incarnata extracts elicit GABA currents in hippocampal neurons. GABA itself is a prominent ingredient in passionflower extracts, and its concentration varies significantly depending on extraction method. Water-based extractions yielded higher GABA content. Provides direct mechanistic evidence for GABAergic anxiolytic activity.

PubMed: 20382514

GABAergic and opioidergic dual-pathway mechanisms underlying anxiolytic and antinociceptive effects

Strong Animal Pharmacology Study 2016 ICR mice

Pharmacological study in mice demonstrating that Passiflora incarnata exerts anxiolytic and antinociceptive effects through both GABAergic and opioidergic pathways. Effects were blocked by flumazenil (GABA-A antagonist) and naloxone (opioid antagonist), confirming dual-pathway mechanism. Supports traditional use as both anxiolytic and analgesic.

PubMed: 26912265

Comprehensive phytochemical profile including flavonoids, glycosides, and alkaloids with...

Strong Comprehensive Phytochemical Review 2004 Chemical and pharmacological data synthesis

Comprehensive review of Passiflora genus identifying key bioactive compounds: C-glycosyl flavonoids (chrysin, vitexin, isovitexin, orientin), indole alkaloids (harman, harmine, harmaline), and the benzoflavone moiety (BZF). Chrysin demonstrates high affinity for GABA-A benzodiazepine receptors. Multiple species show CNS-depressant, anxiolytic, and sedative properties in validated pharmacological models.

PubMed: 15261959

GABAergic modulation comparable to pharmaceutical anxiolytics in clinical contexts

Strong Clinical Applications Review 2025 Multiple preclinical and clinical studies

Review of Passiflora incarnata anxiolytic potential focusing on GABAergic modulation mechanisms. Passionflower demonstrates anxiolytic effects comparable to midazolam and oxazepam in clinical settings. Active compounds modulate GABA-A receptor subtypes, enhancing inhibitory neurotransmission without the dependence risk associated with benzodiazepines.

PubMed: 41392563

Clinical Benefits

Systematic review of 9 clinical trials confirming anxiolytic and neuropsychiatric benefits

Strong Evidence Systematic Review 2020 9 clinical trials reviewed Various

Systematic review of Passiflora incarnata in neuropsychiatric disorders covering 9 clinical trials. Evidence supports anxiolytic effects across multiple clinical contexts including generalized anxiety, preoperative anxiety, and dental anxiety. No significant adverse effects reported across trials. Also found preliminary evidence for benefits in insomnia, ADHD, and opioid withdrawal.

PubMed: 33352740

Preoperative anxiety reduction comparable to pharmaceutical anxiolytics at 500 mg

Strong Evidence Human RCT (Double-blind, Placebo-controlled) 2008 60 patients undergoing inguinal herniorrhaphy Single dose 90 minutes before surgery

Landmark double-blind RCT of 60 patients receiving 500 mg oral Passiflora incarnata or placebo 90 minutes before inguinal herniorrhaphy. Passionflower significantly reduced anxiety (p<0.001) compared to placebo without causing sedation, changes in psychomotor function, hemodynamics, or level of sedation. Demonstrates rapid anxiolytic onset without cognitive impairment.

PubMed: 18499602

Anxiolysis before spinal anesthesia without sedation or psychomotor impairment

Strong Evidence Human RCT (Prospective, Double-blind) 2012 60 patients undergoing spinal anesthesia (ASA I-II) Single dose 30 minutes before procedure

Prospective double-blind RCT of 60 patients receiving 700 mg oral Passiflora incarnata or placebo 30 minutes before spinal anesthesia. Passionflower suppressed the increase in anxiety without affecting psychomotor test results, sedation level, or hemodynamics. Numerical Rating Scale anxiety scores were significantly lower in the treatment group.

PubMed: 22048283

Cochrane review finding anxiolytic effects comparable to benzodiazepines

Moderate Evidence Cochrane Systematic Review 2007 2 RCTs, 198 participants Various

Cochrane systematic review of Passiflora for anxiety disorder. Only 2 studies met inclusion criteria with 198 total participants. One study found passionflower comparable to oxazepam for generalized anxiety with fewer job-related impairment side effects. Authors concluded too few studies to draw firm conclusions but noted promising results warranting further research.

PubMed: 17253512

Polysomnography-confirmed sleep improvement in adults with insomnia

Strong Evidence Human RCT (Double-blind, Placebo-controlled) 2020 110 adults aged 20-55 with insomnia 2 weeks

Double-blind RCT of 110 adults with insomnia receiving Passiflora incarnata extract or placebo for 2 weeks. Polysomnography-confirmed significant increase in total sleep time. Represents the largest and most rigorously designed passionflower sleep trial with objective PSG measurement rather than subjective sleep diaries alone.

PubMed: 31714321

Subjective sleep quality improvement with passionflower tea in healthy adults

Moderate Evidence Human RCT (Double-blind, Placebo-controlled, Crossover) 2011 41 healthy adults aged 18-35 1 week per treatment with 1-week washout

Double-blind crossover RCT of 41 healthy adults. Passionflower herbal tea consumed for 7 days significantly improved subjective sleep quality ratings compared to placebo tea (p<0.01). PSG subsample (10 participants) showed no significant differences, suggesting low-dose tea benefits are primarily subjective. Supports mild sleep-promoting effects at dietary tea-level doses.

PubMed: 21294203

Significant stress reduction and sleep improvement with standardized extract

Moderate Evidence Human RCT (Double-blind, Placebo-controlled) 2024 65 participants with stress and sleep complaints 4 weeks

Double-blind RCT of 65 participants receiving standardized Passiflora incarnata extract for 4 weeks. Significant improvement in stress measures and sleep quality scores compared to placebo. Supports the dual anxiolytic-sleep benefit profile of passionflower supplementation in adults with stress-related sleep disturbances.

PubMed: 38646244

Safety & Tolerability

Comprehensive safety profile from ethnopharmacological evidence synthesis

Good Safety Profile Comprehensive Ethnopharmacology Review 2013 Preclinical, clinical, and pharmacovigilance data

Comprehensive ethnopharmacology review of Passiflora incarnata covering pharmacokinetics, efficacy, and safety. Generally good safety profile established through centuries of traditional use and modern clinical trials. Rare adverse events include dizziness, drowsiness, and confusion at high doses. Caution advised with sedative medications due to potential additive CNS depression. Isolated case reports of hepatotoxicity with multi-herb preparations (causality uncertain).

PubMed: 24140586

Strong evidence for anxiolytic efficacy with low adverse event profile across herbal supplements

Good Safety Profile Systematic Review 2010 Multiple RCTs of herbal supplements for anxiety

Systematic review of nutritional and herbal supplements for anxiety disorders. Strong evidence found for passionflower with characteristically low adverse event rates compared to conventional anxiolytics. One RCT demonstrated fewer job-related impairment side effects than oxazepam. Supports favorable risk-benefit ratio for anxiety management.

PubMed: 20929532

No significant adverse effects across 9 clinical trials in systematic review

Good Safety Profile Systematic Review 2020 9 clinical trials

Systematic review covering 9 clinical trials of Passiflora incarnata found no significant adverse effects reported in any included study. Occasional mild side effects (drowsiness, dizziness, GI discomfort) reported at low frequency. May potentiate effects of sedative, anxiolytic, and anticoagulant medications. Not recommended during pregnancy due to uterine stimulant alkaloids.

PubMed: 33352740

Dosage Research

Effective acute anxiolytic dose: 500-700 mg extract before procedures

Evidence-based Multiple RCTs 2008 120+ surgical patients across studies

Preoperative RCTs establish 500-700 mg as effective single-dose range for acute anxiolysis. Movafegh et al. used 500 mg oral dose 90 minutes before surgery with significant anxiety reduction. Aslanargun et al. used 700 mg 30 minutes before spinal anesthesia. Effects onset within 30-90 minutes. Higher end of range may be needed for more acute anxiety situations.

PubMed: 18499602

Chronic supplementation: 400-800 mg/day extract or 2g dried herb as tea for anxiety and sleep

Evidence-based Systematic Review & Multiple RCTs 2020 Multiple studies synthesized

Clinical trial dosing ranges from systematic review: standardized extract 400-800 mg/day in divided doses for chronic anxiety and sleep support. Passionflower herbal tea (approximately 2g dried herb per cup) also effective for mild sleep quality improvement. For sleep, consumption 30-60 minutes before bedtime is recommended. Effects may build over 1-4 weeks of consistent use.

PubMed: 33352740
