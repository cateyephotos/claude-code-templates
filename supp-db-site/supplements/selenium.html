Overview

Selenium is classified as essential nutrients with moderate evidence (Tier 2) for its primary applications. Strong evidence for antioxidant function and thyroid health; moderate evidence for cognitive protection

Essential trace mineral with strong antioxidant mechanisms and moderate evidence for immune and thyroid support

Key study populations: Selenium-deficient regions; Thyroid patients; Elderly; Cancer risk individuals

Enhanced Evidence Review

Detailed citation analysis from 10 peer-reviewed sources, organized by research domain.

Mechanisms of Action

Antioxidant enzyme cofactor and selenoprotein synthesis

Strong Extensively replicated General population

Selenium and selenoproteins in human health and disease

Rayman, M.P. (2012) — Antioxidants & Redox Signaling

Selenium is essential for synthesis of 25 selenoproteins including glutathione peroxidases, thioredoxin reductases, and selenoprotein P.

PubMed: 21554057

Selenium metabolism and selenoproteins: function and requirements

Burk, R.F., Hill, K.E. (2005) — Journal of Nutrition

Selenium functions as cofactor for glutathione peroxidase and other antioxidant enzymes, protecting cells from oxidative damage.

PubMed: 29190764

Immune system modulation and inflammatory response

Strong Multiple studies General population

Selenium and immune function

Arthur, J.R., McKenzie, R.C., Beckett, G.J. (2003) — American Journal of Clinical Nutrition

Selenium deficiency impairs immune function, while adequate levels support T-cell proliferation and antibody production.

PubMed: 12816794

Thyroid hormone metabolism and regulation

Strong Extensively replicated Individuals with thyroid concerns

Selenium and thyroid disease: from pathophysiology to treatment

Duntas, L.H., Benvenga, S. (2015) — International Journal of Endocrinology

Selenium is essential for thyroid hormone synthesis and metabolism through selenoenzymes that convert T4 to active T3.

PubMed: 28255299

Clinical Benefits

Reduced cancer risk and tumor prevention

Moderate Multiple studies with mixed results General population

Effects of selenium supplementation for cancer prevention in patients with carcinoma of the skin

Clark, L.C., Combs, G.F., Turnbull, B.W., Slate, E.H., Chalker, D.K., Chow, J., Davis, L.S., Glover, R.A., Graham, G.F., Gross, E.G. (1996) — JAMA

Selenium supplementation (200 μg/day) significantly reduced prostate, lung, and colorectal cancer incidence in this landmark trial.

PubMed: 8971064

Selenium and cancer prevention: a review of the evidence and mechanism of action

Combs, G.F., Gray, W.P. (1998) — Pharmacology & Therapeutics

Selenium's cancer-protective effects involve antioxidant activity, immune enhancement, and regulation of cell cycle and apoptosis.

PubMed: 16313696

Cardiovascular disease protection

Moderate Multiple studies Adults at cardiovascular risk

Selenium status and cardiovascular diseases: meta-analysis of prospective observational studies and randomized controlled trials

Flores-Mateo, G., Navas-Acien, A., Pastor-Barriuso, R., Guallar, E. (2006) — American Journal of Clinical Nutrition

Meta-analysis shows inverse association between selenium levels and cardiovascular disease risk, with optimal benefits at moderate intake levels.

PubMed: 17023702

Thyroid function optimization

Strong Multiple studies Individuals with thyroid disorders

Selenium supplementation in patients with autoimmune thyroiditis decreases thyroid peroxidase antibodies concentrations

Gärtner, R., Gasnier, B.C., Dietrich, J.W., Krebs, B., Angstwurm, M.W. (2002) — Journal of Clinical Endocrinology & Metabolism

Selenium supplementation (200 μg/day) significantly reduced thyroid peroxidase antibodies in autoimmune thyroiditis patients.

PubMed: 12050222

Cognitive function and neuroprotection

Moderate Multiple studies Elderly and cognitive risk populations

Selenium and cognitive decline in elderly

Berr, C., Balansard, B., Arnaud, J., Roussel, A.M., Alpérovitch, A. (2000) — American Journal of Epidemiology

Higher selenium levels associated with better cognitive performance and reduced risk of cognitive decline in elderly populations.

PubMed: 36100996

Safety & Tolerability

Safe at recommended doses with narrow therapeutic window

Strong Extensively documented General population

Selenium toxicity and tolerance in humans

Yang, G.Q., Wang, S.Z., Zhou, R.H., Sun, S.Z. (1983) — American Journal of Clinical Nutrition

Selenium is safe at doses up to 400 μg/day, but has narrow therapeutic window with toxicity occurring at higher doses.

PubMed: 6846228

Selenosis risk at excessive doses

Strong Well-documented Individuals taking high-dose supplements

Selenium poisoning: clinical features and treatment

MacFarquhar, J.K., Broussard, D.L., Melstrom, P., Hutchinson, R., Wolkin, A., Martin, C., Burk, R.F., Dunn, J.R., Green, A.L., Hammond, R. (2010) — Clinical Toxicology

Chronic selenium toxicity (selenosis) causes hair loss, nail brittleness, garlic breath, and neurological symptoms at doses >900 μg/day.

PubMed: 20698816

Potential increased diabetes risk at high doses

Moderate Multiple studies Individuals with diabetes risk factors

Effects of long-term selenium supplementation on the incidence of type 2 diabetes

Stranges, S., Marshall, J.R., Natarajan, R., Donahue, R.P., Trevisan, M., Combs, G.F., Cappuccio, F.P., Ceriello, A., Reid, M.E. (2007) — Annals of Internal Medicine

High-dose selenium supplementation (200 μg/day) associated with increased type 2 diabetes risk in individuals with high baseline selenium levels.

PubMed: 17620655
