Overview

Vitamin B6 is classified as essential nutrients with moderate evidence (Tier 2) for its primary applications. Strong evidence for neurotransmitter synthesis and mood

Key study populations: Women with PMS; Elderly; Depression patients; General adults

Enhanced Evidence Review

Detailed citation analysis from 11 peer-reviewed sources, organized by research domain.

Mechanisms of Action

Pyridoxal 5'-Phosphate (PLP) as Universal Enzyme Cofactor

Strong

Vitamin B6: a long known compound of surprising complexity

Mooney S, Leuendorf JE, Hendrickson C, Hellmann H (2009) — Molecules

PLP serves as coenzyme for over 140 enzymatic reactions in humans, encompassing amino acid metabolism, gluconeogenesis, lipid metabolism, neurotransmitter biosynthesis, and nucleic acid metabolism; deficiency impairs all these pathways simultaneously.

PubMed: 19145213

Disorders affecting vitamin B6 metabolism

Wilson MP, Plecko B, Mills PB, Clayton PT (2019) — Journal of Inherited Metabolic Disease

Inborn errors of metabolism affecting PLP homeostasis cause seizures, developmental delay, and systemic metabolic disruption by impairing aminotransferases, decarboxylases, and other PLP-dependent enzymes.

PubMed: 30671974

Neurotransmitter Biosynthesis via Aromatic Amino Acid Decarboxylase

Strong

B6-responsive disorders: a model of vitamin dependency

Clayton PT (2006) — Journal of Inherited Metabolic Disease

PLP is an obligate cofactor for aromatic amino acid decarboxylase (DOPA decarboxylase), catalyzing biosynthesis of dopamine, serotonin, GABA, and other neurotransmitters; B6-responsive epilepsy demonstrates that PLP depletion causes neurological dysfunction through neurotransmitter depletion.

PubMed: 16763894

Mutations in antiquitin in individuals with pyridoxine-dependent seizures

Mills PB, Struys E, Jakobs C, Plecko B, Baxter P, Baumgartner M, Willemsen MA, Omran H, Tacke U, Uhlenberg B, Vreken P, Clayton PT (2006) — Nature Medicine

Mutations in antiquitin (ALDH7A1) cause accumulation of alpha-aminoadipic semialdehyde that inactivates PLP, resulting in pyridoxine-dependent seizures; establishes molecular basis for B6-responsive epilepsy.

PubMed: 16491085

Immune Function Modulation via Lymphocyte Proliferation and Cytokine Production

Moderate

Vitamin B6 and immune competence

Rall LC, Meydani SN (1993) — Nutrition Reviews

B6 deficiency impairs T-lymphocyte proliferation, reduces IL-2 production, diminishes natural killer cell activity, and decreases antibody production; repletion restores immune competence; elderly most vulnerable.

PubMed: 8302491

Clinical Benefits

Safety & Tolerability

Chronic doses exceeding 500 mg/day can cause severe sensory peripheral neuropathy; threshold for neuropathy may be as low as 200 mg/day with prolonged use

Moderate

Sensory neuropathy from pyridoxine abuse: a new megavitamin syndrome

Schaumburg H, Kaplan J, Windebank A, Vick N, Rasmus S, Pleasure D, Brown MJ (1983) — New England Journal of Medicine

Chronic pyridoxine megadose use (2–6 g/day) causes severe sensory neuropathy with ataxia and progressive limb weakness; 7 patients developed incapacitating neuropathy; symptoms partially reversible upon discontinuation; landmark index series establishing B6 neurotoxicity.

PubMed: 6308447

Doses up to 100 mg/day for PMS and 75 mg/day for NVP are generally well-tolerated without significant adverse effects

Low

Efficacy of vitamin B-6 in the treatment of premenstrual syndrome: systematic review

Wyatt KM, Dimmock PW, Jones PW, O'Brien PMS (1999) — BMJ

B6 doses up to 100 mg/day across 9 PMS trials showed no significant adverse event signals; neuropathy risk is primarily documented at doses ≥500 mg/day in the literature.

PubMed: 10334745

Disorders affecting vitamin B6 metabolism

Wilson MP, Plecko B, Mills PB, Clayton PT (2019) — Journal of Inherited Metabolic Disease

Clinical B6 supplementation at moderate doses is safe; standard adult supplementation doses (1–100 mg/day) well-tolerated; safety issues primarily arise at megadose levels.

PubMed: 30671974

Dosage Research

Therapeutic B6 for PMS uses 50–100 mg/day with evidence of efficacy; for NVP 10–25 mg every 8 hours per ACOG guidelines; RDA (1.3–2.0 mg/day) sufficient for maintenance in non-deficient adults; chronic doses above 200 mg/day carry dose-dependent neuropathy risk

Efficacy of vitamin B-6 in the treatment of premenstrual syndrome: systematic review

Wyatt KM, Dimmock PW, Jones PW, O'Brien PMS (1999) — BMJ

B6 doses used in PMS trials range 50–100 mg/day; this range effective for PMS symptom reduction; OR 2.32 vs placebo.

PubMed: 10334745

Interventions for nausea and vomiting in early pregnancy

Matthews A, Haas DM, O'Mathuna DP, Dowswell T (2015) — Cochrane Database of Systematic Reviews

Pyridoxine dose for NVP typically 10–25 mg every 6–8 hours; incorporated into ACOG first-line NVP treatment guidelines; RR 0.88 vs placebo.

PubMed: 26348534

Sensory neuropathy from pyridoxine abuse: a new megavitamin syndrome

Schaumburg H, Kaplan J, Windebank A, Vick N, Rasmus S, Pleasure D, Brown MJ (1983) — New England Journal of Medicine

Upper safety boundary: 2–6 g/day causes severe neuropathy; regulatory agencies set tolerable upper limit at 100 mg/day based on case data and safety margin considerations.

PubMed: 6308447
