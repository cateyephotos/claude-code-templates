Overview

Zeaxanthin is classified as antioxidants with moderate evidence (Tier 2) for its primary applications. Clinical evidence for eye health and cognitive benefits

Zeaxanthin is a macular carotenoid with strong mechanistic evidence for blue light filtration and retinal antioxidant protection. The landmark AREDS2 trial (4,203 participants) and Cochrane review (19 RCTs) show modest, non-significant reductions in AMD progression with lutein/zeaxanthin supplementation, while network meta-analyses demonstrate significant improvements in macular pigment optical density and visual function parameters

Key study populations: Age-related macular degeneration; Computer vision syndrome; Eye health

Clinical Benefits

Modest AMD risk reduction as beta-carotene substitute in AREDS formulation

Strong Landmark trial with 5-year follow-up, confirmed by secondary analyses

Lutein + zeaxanthin and omega-3 fatty acids for age-related macular degeneration: the Age-Related Eye Disease Study 2 (AREDS2) randomized clinical trial

Age-Related Eye Disease Study 2 (AREDS2) Research Group (2013) — JAMA

Lutein + zeaxanthin (10 mg + 2 mg daily) showed HR 0.90 (98.7% CI: 0.76-1.07, p=0.12) for advanced AMD vs placebo — non-significant,5-year probability of advanced AMD: 31% placebo vs 29% lutein/zeaxanthin group,Beta-carotene group had more lung cancers (23 vs 11, p=0.04), mostly in former smokers,Lutein/zeaxanthin identified as appropriate carotenoid substitute for beta-carotene in AREDS formula,Secondary analyses suggested benefit when directly compared to beta-carotene arm

PubMed: 23644932

Antioxidant supplementation including lutein/zeaxanthin may modestly reduce late AMD progression

Antioxidant vitamin and mineral supplementation: OR 0.72 (CI 0.58-0.90) for late AMD — statistically significant,Lutein/zeaxanthin specifically: RR 0.94 (95% CI: 0.87-1.01) for late AMD — low-certainty evidence,AREDS2 was the main source of evidence for lutein/zeaxanthin component,Authors concluded lutein/zeaxanthin may have little or no effect on AMD progression,No evidence of harm from lutein/zeaxanthin supplementation

Strong Confirmed across multiple AREDS2 reports and subsequent studies
